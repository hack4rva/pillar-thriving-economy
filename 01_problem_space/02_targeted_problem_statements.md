# Targeted Problem Statements - Thriving Economy

## 1) Helping Minority-Owned Businesses Identify Relevant City Contract Opportunities

Score: 26/32 — Strong

### Problem statement
How might we use technology to help minority-owned businesses in Richmond more easily identify and understand City contracting opportunities that match their capabilities, so that more firms can confidently explore and pursue public work?

### Context
The City publishes procurement opportunities through its vendor portal (https://mvendor.cgieva.com/Vendor/public/AllOpportunities.jsp?&agencyname=City%20of%20Richmond%20Government). While the portal provides transparent access, it is designed for vendors already familiar with government procurement. Small or first-time contractors — including minority-owned businesses — may struggle to determine which opportunities are relevant, interpret procurement terminology, or understand what the first step toward participation might be.

### Constraints
- Existing procurement policies and legal requirements cannot be changed
- The existing procurement portal must remain the official source
- The solution should rely on publicly available procurement data
- Must be accessible to businesses with little or no government contracting experience

### Success would mean
- Businesses can more quickly determine whether an opportunity matches their services
- Procurement listings are easier for first-time vendors to interpret
- More minority-owned businesses feel confident exploring City contracting
- Businesses are better prepared before contacting City procurement staff

### Gaps to close
- Clarify procurement portal data format (API, CSV export, or scraping)
- Identify user tech context (mobile/desktop), digital literacy range
- Specify expected output type for weekend demo
- Link City Contracts Registry open data URL

## 2) Small Business Navigation & Business Setup Friction

Score: 24/32 — Strong

### Problem statement
How might we use technology to improve how emerging and home-based entrepreneurs start and register a business in Richmond so that they can understand the right steps, complete them in the right order, and connect to relevant City services without confusion or repeated handoffs, while respecting existing departmental structures, program eligibility rules, and staffing capacity?

### Context
First-time and small-scale entrepreneurs in Richmond often need to interact with multiple City departments — including licensing, Minority Business, Economic Development, code enforcement, and workforce-related services. There is no clear front door, no coordinated pathway, and no simple way to understand which requirements apply, what order steps should happen in, or where to go for help.

### Constraints
- Existing departmental roles and structures remain in place
- Program eligibility rules must remain intact
- No creation of a new large concierge office
- Data-sharing between departments is limited
- Licensing and regulatory policies cannot be altered

### Success would mean
- Entrepreneurs can quickly understand what applies to them
- Required steps are completed in the correct sequence
- Fewer businesses unintentionally operate out of compliance
- Staff spend less time redirecting inquiries
- Business owners have a clearer, more customer-friendly starting experience

### Gaps to close
- Document the decision tree: which permits apply to which business types, in what order (create a 1–2 page artifact)
- Current City website URLs for each relevant department
- Sample "business registration journey" based on a real path
- Specify expected output type for weekend demo
