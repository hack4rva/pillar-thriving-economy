Red Flags (Use as guardrails while researching or scoping)

- Treating a hackathon prototype as if it can provide authoritative legal, licensing, regulatory, or procurement advice.
- Assuming all needed Richmond business-startup logic is already available in structured data.
- Building a chatbot that sounds helpful but cannot cite actual Richmond pages, forms, rules, or portal entries.
- Confusing public transparency with user usability.
- Assuming minority-owned businesses need “AI” in the abstract instead of better interpretation, sequencing, and clarity.
- Building an end-to-end entrepreneur concierge that depends on interdepartmental integration, policy authority, or internal data-sharing that is limited.
- Using “matching” language without explaining what data supports the match and what the limitations are.
- Overclaiming likely impact (e.g., increased contracting, improved compliance, or staff time savings) without evidence.
- Ignoring mobile access, digital literacy, plain-language needs, or translation needs.
- Failing to identify a likely continuation owner, which is a known weakness.
- Treating historical contract or licensing data as current guidance without checking recency and relevance.
- Producing a glossy front end with no grounded Richmond service map underneath it.
