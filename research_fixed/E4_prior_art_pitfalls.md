# E4 Prior-Art Pitfalls
*(A concise guide for patent practitioners – updated 2026-03)*

## Executive Summary
Prior-art rejections under 35 U.S.C. § 102 and § 103 are the primary drivers of office-action delays and application abandonments. Navigating the America Invents Act (AIA) requires a precise understanding of statutory categories, particularly regarding confidential sales, electronic publications, and the strict requirements for inventor-originated grace period exceptions. This document outlines the core statutory frameworks, debunks common misconceptions (such as the "secret sale" myth dismantled by the Supreme Court), and provides a practical checklist for utilizing 37 CFR § 1.130 affidavits to overcome invalidating references.

---

## 1. Why Prior-Art Matters

Prior-art rejections are the single biggest cause of **office-action delays** and **application abandonments**. A significant percentage of all first-office actions involve § 102/§ 103 rejections, most of which could be avoided with a clearer early analysis. 

*Implication*: Every missed nuance — from the definition of "printed publication" to the on-sale bar — can translate into months of extra prosecution cost and lost market entry windows.

## 2. Core Statutory Categories

| Category | Governing Statute (AIA) | Typical Evidence | Key MPEP Section |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Printed Publication** | 35 U.S.C. § 102(a)(1) | Journal article, conference paper, website (publicly accessible) | § 2128 [1] |
| **Public Use** | 35 U.S.C. § 102(a)(1) | Demonstration, testing, public display | § 2152.02(c) [2] |
| **On-Sale** | 35 U.S.C. § 102(a)(1) | Commercial offer or sale, even if confidential | § 2152.02(d) [2] |
| **Inventor-Originated Disclosure (Grace Period)** | 35 U.S.C. § 102(b)(1)(A) | Publication ≤ 1 yr before filing by inventor or joint inventor | § 2153 [3] |
| **Foreign Patent Publication** | 35 U.S.C. § 102(a)(2) | Published foreign application (WIPO, EPO) | § 2152 [2] |

## 3. Common Misconceptions

| Misconception | Reality (Evidence) | Risk if Ignored |
|---|---|---|
| "A secret sale is **not** prior art because the buyer is under NDA." | **Helsinn Healthcare S.A. v. Teva** held that a confidential sale can still be *on-sale* prior art under § 102(a)(1) [4] [5]. | Failure to cite the on-sale bar leads to later invalidity or unenforceability. |
| "If a document is dated after our filing date, it can't be prior art." | Publication date (not filing date) governs; early-online posting can create an earlier "effective" date. | Over-reliance on filing chronology may miss hidden print-publication rejections. |
| "Grace-period exceptions apply automatically to any inventor-originated disclosure." | The disclosure must be **attributed** to the inventor (or joint inventor) and must be **within one year** of filing; otherwise it is plain prior art [3]. | Missing the attribution requirement forces an unnecessary § 102(b)(1)(A) rebuttal. |
| "Electronic-only content is never a printed publication." | MPEP treats many electronic media as "printed publications" if they are publicly accessible [1]. | Ignoring web-based references can cause surprise rejections. |

---

## 4. Key Judicial Signals

1. **Helsinn Healthcare S.A. v. Teva** (U.S. Supreme Ct., 2019) – *On-sale* bar applies even when the sale is confidential; the "otherwise available to the public" phrase in the AIA does **not** narrow the on-sale definition [4] [5]. 
2. **Pfaff v. Wells Electronics** (U.S. Supreme Ct., 1998) – *Secret commercial use* can be an on-sale bar [4]. 
3. **Blue Calypso** (Fed. Cir., 2015) – *Public accessibility* is the "touchstone" for determining a printed publication [1]. 

*Takeaway*: When evaluating any alleged prior-art reference, first ask **(i)** *Is the reference publicly accessible?* and **(ii)** *Does the reference constitute a commercial offer or sale?*

## 5. Practical Tools & Remedies

### 5.1 Affidavit / Declaration (37 CFR § 1.130)
*When to use* 
- To **exclude** an inventor-originated disclosure within the grace period (§ 102(b)(1)(A)) [3] [6]. 
- To **disqualify** a reference that was **obtained directly from the inventor** (§ 102(b)(2)(A)) [2] [6]. 

*Key requirements* (MPEP § 2155) 
- Identify the subject matter and exact disclosure date [6]. 
- Attach a **copy of the printed publication** (if any) [6]. 
- Provide a **statement of attribution** (inventor, joint inventor, or derived from them) [6]. 

### 5.2 Prior-Art Search Cut-off Strategy
- Set the **search cut-off date** to the *effective filing date* **minus** the one-year grace-period window. 
- Flag any references published **≤ 1 yr** before filing for possible § 102(b)(1) rebuttal [3].

## 6. Pitfall Checklist

| ✅ Item | Question | Action |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Date Accuracy** | Have all dates been expressed as `YYYY-MM` (or `YYYY` only)? | Review each citation; replace impossible dates with `Unknown` **only** if no source exists. |
| **2. Attribution** | Is the reference **inventor-originated** or **derived** from the inventor? | Prepare an affidavit under 37 CFR § 1.130(a) if yes; otherwise treat as plain prior art [6]. |
| **3. Public Accessibility** | Can the public obtain the reference without a confidentiality agreement? | Conduct a "public-access" test (Blue Calypso standard) [1]. |
| **4. On-Sale Test** | Does the reference involve a **commercial offer or sale**, even under NDA? | Cite Helsinn decision; consider on-sale rejection [4] [5]. |
| **5. Electronic vs. Printed** | Is the reference an electronic publication that meets "printed publication" criteria? | Apply MPEP § 2128 guidance; include URL in citation [1]. |
| **6. Prior-Art Exception Eligibility** | Does the reference fall within any § 102(b) exception (e.g., grace period, inventor-originated)? | Draft appropriate declaration; reference MPEP § 2153 [3]. |
| **7. Citation Integrity** | Are all URLs live and authoritative? | Verify each link; replace dead URLs with alternatives (e.g., USPTO). |
| **8. Narrative Consistency** | Does the section maintain the original tone (clinical-legal, concise, no AI-disclaimer)? | Remove any AI-generated disclaimer sentences. |
