## City Government Structure and Departmental Actors

The City of Richmond operates under a strong Mayor-Council system with nine elected city council members representing individual voting districts, each serving four-year terms.[1] The Mayor serves as the chief executive officer with ceremonial duties and provides overall direction to the Chief Administrative Officer, who manages the delivery of government services. This structure creates a clear hierarchy for policy authority, with the Richmond City Council serving as the legislative body responsible for local government oversight, budget allocation, and policy adoption.[1] The Department of Neighborhood and Community Services maintains direct contact with residents through the RVA 311 service system, making it the primary frontline organization for fielding citizen requests and concerns.[15] This department operates across multiple portfolios, including neighborhood services, community engagement, and support coordination, positioning it as a critical access point for understanding resident needs and problems.

The City's operational structure includes several departments with distinct mandates and knowledge bases. The Department of Finance manages the city's budget and financial operations, maintaining detailed knowledge about resource allocation and fiscal constraints.[1] The Department of Information Technology oversees digital infrastructure and data systems, becoming increasingly important as the city develops integrated data systems for performance tracking and accountability. The Department of Public Utilities manages the city's infrastructure for water, sewer, and stormwater services, holding specialized operational knowledge about the technical systems that support urban living. The Planning and Development Review department administers zoning, site plans, and the Richmond 300 master plan, making it the repository of knowledge about land use, development patterns, and long-term planning vision.[16] The Department of Parks, Recreation and Community Facilities operates 47 parks throughout the city and manages community centers and recreation programs, serving as both a service provider and community convener.[14]

Several departments handle critical safety and wellbeing functions. The Richmond Police Department includes the Community, Youth and Intervention Services unit, which operates community code enforcement, community care initiatives, and specialized units for LGBTQ+ liaison work and school resource officers.[18] The Richmond Public Schools system, with 28 elementary schools, nine middle schools, eight high schools, and several specialized schools, represents the largest city institution by employment and budget after the police department. The office coordinates with education institutions, employers, and community organizations through formal partnerships, creating accountability for results and impact. ## Housing, Community Development, and Homelessness Services Ecosystem

Richmond's housing ecosystem includes several specialized institutions managing different aspects of housing policy and service delivery. This structure separates operational administration from policy oversight, allowing the board to maintain independence while being accountable to elected officials. The city's housing department holds specialized knowledge about development costs, financing mechanisms, and the nonprofit and for-profit developers capable of delivering affordable housing projects.

The Department of Planning and Development Review maintains responsibility for implementing the Richmond 300 master plan, which establishes twenty-year vision for housing, neighborhoods, and sustainability.[33] The plan's six "Big Moves" include expanding housing opportunities throughout the city to decentralize poverty, reconnecting neighborhoods divided by highways, and providing greenways and parks within a ten-minute walk of all residents. Implementation of these strategies requires coordination across multiple departments and external partners, placing the planning department in a knowledge coordination role. Council-appointed boards including the City Planning Commission, Historic Preservation Commission, and Zoning Appeals Board provide specialized expertise and checks on planning decisions, ensuring that policies reflect community input and professional standards.

The homelessness services ecosystem operates through the Greater Richmond Continuum of Care (GRCoC), a coordinated regional system operating across the city of Richmond and eight surrounding counties.[50] The GRCoC functions as a collective impact backbone organization, coordinating over forty agencies at regional meetings, facilitating trainings, and managing the coordinated entry system that directs people experiencing homelessness to appropriate services. Homeward, the planning and coordinating organization for homeless services, operates the backbone functions, employing specialized staff focused on different aspects of homelessness prevention and response. CARITAS Emergency Shelter provides safe housing for adults experiencing homelessness, while also providing case management and workforce development services in partnership with Daily Planet Health Services.[27]

This ecosystem illustrates clear differentiation between functions: the City of Richmond provides policy direction and funding through the budget process; the GRCoC operates as the backbone organization coordinating regional strategy; nonprofit providers like CARITAS and Homeward deliver services directly; and individuals with lived experience of homelessness participate as advisors through formal structures. Knowledge ownership distributes across this ecosystem, with frontline providers understanding client needs and barriers, the backbone organization understanding system gaps and coordination opportunities, and the City maintaining policy authority over funding and statutory obligations.
