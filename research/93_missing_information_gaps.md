# Comprehensive Research Assessment: Critical Information Gaps and Validation Needs for Richmond's Business Support and Entrepreneurial Ecosystem Development Initiatives

This research report identifies and analyzes significant gaps in current knowledge, validation requirements, and continuation planning necessary to sustain and enhance Richmond's business support ecosystem. Drawing from available documentation and government resources, this assessment reveals multiple areas where targeted research, stakeholder validation, and systematic documentation could strengthen coordination, reduce barriers to entrepreneurship, and improve outcomes for Richmond residents and business owners attempting to navigate the city's regulatory and support landscape. The analysis addresses continuity planning, technical infrastructure validation, stakeholder engagement documentation, user research needs, process mapping requirements, and evidence gathering for initiatives that will require sustained commitment beyond the current planning cycle ending March 29, 2026.

## Institutional Continuity and Leadership Commitment Validation

### The Critical Challenge of Named Champions and Knowledge Transfer

One of the most significant gaps in Richmond's current business support infrastructure is the absence of formally documented continuation champions for specific initiatives and systems improvements. The City of Richmond has launched substantial upgrades to its RVA Business Portal in January 2026, with new features including asset copying from prior years, expanded bill and payment history viewing, exception dashboards, and enhanced document management capabilities[1][1]. However, there appears to be no publicly available documentation identifying which specific staff members or departments are committed to maintaining these systems, iterating on them based on user feedback, or championing further improvements after March 29, 2026. This represents a critical vulnerability in the city's ability to sustain momentum on technological modernization that reduces compliance burden for small businesses.

The Business, Professional, and Occupational License (BPOL) tax threshold has been doubled from $250,000 to $500,000, meaning thousands of Richmond businesses with gross receipts between that range now pay only a $30 flat license fee[1][1][1]. This policy change, approved through ORD. 2024-187, is designed to reduce compliance burden on small businesses while maintaining essential city services. Yet without named champions who have explicitly committed to reviewing implementation outcomes, gathering data on how many additional businesses now benefit from this reduced threshold, and advocating for any necessary policy refinements, the long-term impact of this reform remains uncertain. The city needs documented evidence of which department leaders, council members, or staff members are taking responsibility for measuring this policy's success and making the case for sustained investment in small business support.

Similarly, the Office of Minority Business Development (OMBD) operates registration and certification programs for Minority Business Enterprises (MBEs) and Emerging Small Businesses (ESBs)[10][28]. The organization states that it is "dedicated to advancing opportunities for Minority Business Enterprises (MBEs), Emerging Small Businesses (ESBs), and Disadvantaged Business Enterprises (DBEs) to fully participate in contracting opportunities available within the City of Richmond[10]." However, there is no clear public documentation identifying which leadership figures at OMBD, in the minority business development field, or among city leadership have committed to championing these programs forward, expanding their reach, or addressing documented barriers that minority-owned businesses face in accessing city contracting opportunities. Research validation in this area requires identifying and interviewing key personnel who can articulate their commitment to these initiatives and outline their plans for sustained support.

### Documentation of Stakeholder Engagement and Accountability Structures

The City of Richmond has engaged various stakeholders in planning economic development initiatives, including the launch of transformational redevelopment projects like the Diamond District and City Center Innovation District[5]. Additionally, the city's 2026 General Assembly legislative agenda includes requests for inclusionary zoning authority, a Long-term Owner Occupancy Program (LOOP) for property tax relief, and anti-rent gouging regulations[17]. These initiatives demonstrate sophisticated policy thinking and engagement with multiple constituencies. However, there is minimal public documentation of the stakeholder input processes that led to these proposals, the business community's response to them, or the accountability structures that would hold city officials responsible for implementing these initiatives once they receive legislative approval.

Research validation should include documentation of which stakeholder groups have been engaged in developing these policies, what feedback they provided, and whether that feedback was incorporated into final proposals. Without this documentation, future administrations or city council members may lack the context needed to understand why certain policies were prioritized or how they were intended to work in practice. Additionally, the city should establish and publicly document accountability mechanisms specifying which departments and leaders are responsible for implementing each approved policy, when implementation should occur, and what success metrics will be used to evaluate outcomes.

## Technical Infrastructure and Digital Accessibility Validation

### Structured Data Exposure and Procurement Portal Capabilities

The City of Richmond operates a Supplier Portal that provides businesses access to tools for doing business with the city efficiently[2]. The portal requires registration, completion of IRS W-9 forms, direct deposit banking information, and supporting documentation. However, the search results provide no information about whether this portal exposes structured data in machine-readable formats, such as XML, JSON, or CSV files, or whether it can only be scraped by web tools. This distinction is crucial for entrepreneurs and businesses seeking to analyze procurement opportunities, identify market gaps, or build tools that could help other businesses navigate city contracting[2].

Similarly, the city's Procurement Transparency Dashboard claims to make "contract information available so residents and businesses can see how public funds are used"[22]. The dashboard reportedly includes agency name, contract number, dollar amounts, supplier names, procurement type, contract descriptions, purchase categories, and effective dates. However, the validation needed here is whether this data is truly accessible through structured formats or only through manual web interface navigation, which would severely limit its usefulness to researchers, business analysts, and the broader entrepreneurial community seeking to understand procurement patterns and identify business development opportunities.

The City of Richmond's new Online Permit Portal (OPP) enables submission of commercial and residential building permits, electrical and mechanical permits, and other construction-related documentation[13][13]. Research validation should assess whether applicants can export permit status data, receive structured notifications about application progress, or integrate portal data with their own business management systems. Additionally, because the portal processes multiple types of permits reviewed by different departments, validation research should examine whether applicants can receive coordinated guidance across departments or whether they still experience fragmented review processes that may cause delays or require repeated interactions.

### Data Quality and Real-Time Access Verification

The Vacant Building List maintained by the Property Maintenance & Code Enforcement Division is only published twice yearly and may not have the most current information[6]. For entrepreneurs considering real estate development or renovation opportunities in Richmond, this limitation creates friction. Validation research should determine whether more frequent publication is technically feasible, whether property status information could be published in real-time as buildings are occupied or deteriorated, and whether business owners can subscribe to alerts when specific properties or neighborhoods change status. Such improvements would support entrepreneurs in identifying redevelopment opportunities and could accelerate neighborhood stabilization efforts.

## Minority-Owned Business Certification and Support Data Visibility

### Availability and Accessibility of Business Directory Information

The City of Richmond maintains directories of registered Minority Business Enterprises (MBEs) and Emerging Small Businesses (ESBs), with information including company names, contact persons, physical addresses, phone numbers, websites, business types, and commodity codes[22]. However, research validation is needed to determine whether these directories are available in a comprehensive, downloadable format that procurement professionals, other businesses seeking subcontractors, and researchers can access without manual website navigation. The current accessibility of this information—whether it is easily browsable online, available in spreadsheet format, or requires individual phone calls to access—directly impacts whether minority-owned businesses can effectively market themselves to potential customers and whether other businesses can identify qualified minority-owned suppliers.

Additionally, the documentation should be validated to clarify what percentage of minority-owned businesses in Richmond are registered in these directories, what barriers may prevent registration, and whether the registration process itself could be streamlined. Without understanding these metrics, the city cannot effectively advocate for more diverse supplier participation in city contracting or identify where additional outreach is needed to ensure that minority business owners know about these registration opportunities[9][23].

### Certification Timing and Business Development Support Structures

Emerging Small Business certification has specific requirements including fewer than ten full-time employees, annual gross receipts of $500,000 or less for construction businesses or $250,000 or less for non-construction businesses, and principal place of business within the City of Richmond Enterprise Zone[23][23]. The processing time for registration is approximately 24-48 hours if information is complete, while certification takes less than 30 days[22]. However, research validation is needed to understand what businesses do during those 24-48 hours to two-week windows while awaiting certification. Are they missing opportunities to bid on contracts? Are they losing momentum with potential customers? The city should document real timelines from application submission to first contract award and identify whether processing delays represent a barrier to business growth for ESBs.

Furthermore, while the city's FY27 budget includes resources to support small business facades, economic development performance grants, and a new Carytown Business Improvement District, there is limited public documentation of how the city identifies which businesses and which neighborhoods receive this support and what criteria guide these allocation decisions[32][32]. Validation research should produce a decision framework showing which support mechanisms are available to which business types, under what circumstances businesses become eligible, and how businesses can access this support without requiring insider knowledge or personal relationships with city officials.

## Comprehensive Regulatory Navigation and Permitting Process Mapping

### Business Registration Decision Tree Development

The City of Richmond requires new businesses to obtain licenses before beginning operations, with licenses required within 30 days of opening date[4][35]. However, the specific licenses, permits, and regulatory reviews required vary significantly by business type. Restaurants require health permits and, if serving alcohol, ABC licenses. General contractors must register with the Virginia Department of Professional Occupancy Regulation (DPOR) and provide workers' compensation insurance certification. Peddlers require liability insurance, sidewalk vendors in the central business district need specific regulatory approval, and dance establishments require special permits[4][35].

A critical missing resource is a comprehensive business registration decision tree or flowchart that walks entrepreneurs through determining what permits, licenses, and approvals their specific business requires, in what order, and from which agencies. Currently, a prospective entrepreneur might need to consult multiple documents, contact several city departments, and potentially make multiple trips to City Hall or spend significant time on phone calls to understand what they actually need to do to operate legally. Validation research should map this entrepreneur journey by business type, documenting each step, where entrepreneurs get stuck, what questions they must ask, which answers they receive, and what could be simplified.

Such a resource could be created by interviewing business license staff about the most common questions they receive, reviewing applications and documentation requirements across business types, and testing a draft decision tree with prospective business owners from different industries. The Virginia Department of Small Business and Supplier Diversity offers a Business One Stop resource that addresses some of these questions statewide, but localized, Richmond-specific guidance would significantly reduce friction for entrepreneurs trying to navigate city-specific requirements[40][42].

### Permitting Timeline and Coordination Challenges

The city's permitting process typically involves review by multiple departments including Building Code review, Zoning, Planning and Preservation, Land Use Administration, Commission of Architectural Review, Public Works, Public Utilities, Water Resources, and Code Enforcement[13][13]. Processing times for some applications may take as long as two to three weeks, and applications are reviewed in the order received[13]. However, there is minimal public documentation of typical timelines by permit type, what causes delays, and where applicants most commonly encounter problems requiring rework or resubmission.

The city has introduced TechDesk, "a structured consultation program designed to help commercial permit applicants navigate complex technical questions that arise during the permitting process[13]." This initiative brings together representatives from multiple reviewing departments to provide coordinated guidance and identify actionable next steps. However, research validation is needed to understand how many applicants know about TechDesk, what criteria make a project eligible for TechDesk support, how much time TechDesk consultations save compared to standard permitting, and whether outcomes data from TechDesk cases could inform broader permitting process improvements. Without evidence showing that TechDesk is effective and accessible, the city cannot argue convincingly that it has solved permitting coordination problems.

## User Research and Digital Access Needs Documentation

### Target User Device Patterns and Digital Literacy Assessment

The RVA Business Portal, Online Permit Portal, and other digital tools developed by the city assume that business owners and applicants have access to functioning computers, reliable internet connectivity, and sufficient digital literacy to navigate online systems[1][1][47]. However, research validation is urgently needed to determine what percentage of Richmond's small business owners, particularly those from disadvantaged or underrepresented communities, actually meet these assumptions. A Richmond Federal Reserve article on closing the digital divide documents that significant populations, particularly low-income households and households of color, rely primarily on smartphones for internet access and may lack access to wired broadband, computers for productive work, or digital literacy training[36].

The city should commission targeted research with small business owners from diverse demographics to understand what devices they actually use to access city services, whether they have reliable internet connectivity, what digital literacy gaps prevent them from using online systems, and what alternative access mechanisms they need. This research should specifically include immigrant entrepreneurs, entrepreneurs with limited English proficiency, older business owners, and entrepreneurs from communities that have historically faced barriers to economic participation. Only with this information can the city design systems that are genuinely accessible to all Richmond residents.

### Language Access and Accessibility Requirements

While the city's website includes a Google Translate option and mentions translation services through the Language Access Plan, there is no public documentation of what languages are most commonly needed by Richmond's business community, how many business support documents are actually translated into those languages, and whether translated versions are equally accessible and user-friendly[28][26]. Validation research should include surveys and interviews with business owners from linguistic minorities to understand what language barriers they encounter in accessing business licensing, permits, and support services.

Additionally, the city should document whether its business support websites meet Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) standards for screen readers, keyboard navigation, color contrast, and other accessibility features needed by people with disabilities. Without this validation, the city risks creating systems that exclude entrepreneurs with disabilities from effectively accessing business support services and contracting opportunities.

### Support Mechanism Effectiveness: Phone, In-Person, Virtual, and Community-Based Options

The city provides business support through multiple channels including RVA 311 phone services (804-646-7000), in-person appointments at city offices, virtual appointments, and increasingly through online portals[1][7][1][26][35]. However, there is no public documentation comparing the effectiveness of these different channels, which business owners prefer which methods, whether outcomes differ by support channel, or whether some populations are better served through certain mechanisms than others.

For example, does a business owner calling RVA 311 get the same quality information as someone meeting in person with a revenue administration specialist? Do virtual appointments work well for complex questions or only for simple transactions? Are there business types for which certain support mechanisms work better? Are there demographic groups that prefer particular channels? Validation research should answer these questions by tracking outcomes across channels, surveying business owners about their experiences, and identifying barriers that might be addressed through better channel coordination or design.

## Documented Entrepreneur Journey Examples and Barrier Identification

### Real-World Business Launch Case Studies

While the search results provide information about notable Richmond entrepreneurs and startup success stories—including profiles of founders like Melissa Goulet (Corgibytes and BrandVox), Brian Marks (Bonfire Funds and Belle Isle Craft Spirits), and Larkin Garbee (founder of the co-working space 804RVA and MemberMoose)—there is minimal documentation of the steps these entrepreneurs took to navigate Richmond's regulatory requirements, where they encountered friction, and what resources or support would have made their path easier[24]. These founder success stories demonstrate that entrepreneurship thrives in Richmond, but they do not document the regulatory and support system experience that less-visible entrepreneurs encounter.

The city should commission documented case studies of entrepreneurs across different industries and demographics who have recently launched businesses in Richmond. These case studies should document what business licenses and permits were required, what the actual timeline was from conception to legal operation, where they got stuck or confused, what support resources they tried to access and whether they found them, and what they would do differently if they had to start over. Such research would generate invaluable evidence about which regulatory and support barriers are actual problems rather than theoretical ones, which interventions would have genuine impact, and where the city's current support systems are failing.

### Journey Mapping for Specific Business Types and Scenarios

Different business types require fundamentally different regulatory pathways. A solo freelance consultant requires different documentation than a restaurant with employees, which requires different approvals than a construction company, which requires different processes than a retail storefront with inventory. Validation research should produce detailed journey maps for at least five to ten common Richmond business types, showing each required step, estimated timeline, required documentation, regulatory agencies involved, and decision points where entrepreneurs might become confused.

Additionally, specific scenarios warrant particular attention. What does the regulatory pathway look like for an immigrant entrepreneur launching a business in a language they do not speak fluently? What about an entrepreneur with a criminal record trying to launch a business and navigate any regulatory restrictions that might apply? What about a young entrepreneur under twenty-five trying to access city support for youth entrepreneurs? What about an existing business owner trying to expand into a second location? Documenting these specific scenarios would reveal whether the city's support systems work for all residents or only for those with certain privileges or circumstances.

## Support and Service Resource Inventory and Gap Analysis

### Comprehensive Directory of Business Support Services

Richmond offers extensive business support services through multiple organizations and agencies, including the Office of Minority Business Development (OMBD), Career Services and workforce development programs through the Office of Community Wealth Building (OCWB), Richmond Economic Development office, Startup Virginia, Lighthouse Labs accelerator, Capital One's Michael Wassmer Innovation Center, SCORE mentoring, and various VCU entrepreneurship programs[5][7][8][10][15][24][37][38][44][46][49]. However, a prospective entrepreneur seeking business support services would need to search multiple websites, make numerous phone calls, and potentially attend multiple events to understand what resources are available, who is eligible for which programs, how to apply, and what support they might receive.

Validation research should produce a comprehensive, easy-to-navigate directory of all business support services available to Richmond entrepreneurs, organized by service type (mentoring, financing, training, workspace, pitch events, etc.), target population (youth, minorities, women, returning citizens, etc.), stage of business development (pre-launch, early stage, scaling, etc.), and industry sector. This directory should include contact information, program descriptions, eligibility requirements, application processes, and timelines. The directory should be published in multiple formats—online interactive tool, printable guide, and phone-based directory—and maintained by an identified owner responsible for keeping information current.

### Program Accessibility and Outreach Effectiveness

The city's workforce development and small business support programs have documented outcomes. The Office of Community Wealth Building reports that in 2025, 20 small business owners advanced through entrepreneurship training, 262 residents completed homeownership and financial education, and 27 youth were placed into paid apprenticeships. However, there is no public documentation of how many Richmond residents know about these programs, how the city reaches potential participants, whether awareness differs by demographic group or neighborhood, or what barriers prevent interested residents from accessing these programs.

Validation research should assess program awareness and outreach effectiveness by surveying Richmond residents and small business owners about which business support programs they know about and through what channels they learned about them. This research should identify whether awareness is concentrated among certain demographics, geographic areas, or business types and where increased outreach is needed. Additionally, for programs with documented eligibility requirements, the city should document whether applicants are being rejected due to not meeting requirements or whether barriers in the application or enrollment process itself prevent participation.

## Existing Initiative Assessment and Duplication Reduction Strategy

### Current Initiatives Addressing Regulatory Simplification and Access

The city's recent investments in digital infrastructure have created several initiatives that partially address regulatory navigation challenges. The upgraded RVA Business Portal streamlines business license and property tax filing[1][1][1]. The Online Permit Portal provides centralized submission and tracking of building and trade permits[13][13]. The new TechDesk program provides coordinated multi-department guidance for complex commercial permits[13]. Career Services and workforce development programs help job seekers gain employment skills[7]. The Office of Minority Business Development registers and certifies minority-owned businesses for contracting opportunities[10]. Startup Virginia, Lighthouse Labs, and Capital One's Innovation Center provide entrepreneurship education, mentoring, and sometimes funding[15][37][49].

However, there is no evidence of systematic coordination among these initiatives or documentation of how they work together to support entrepreneurs. A prospective entrepreneur might access the RVA Business Portal to obtain a license, then the Online Permit Portal for building permits, TechDesk for permitting questions, Startup Virginia for general mentoring, Lighthouse Labs if they are seeking venture capital, OCWB for workforce development if they need to hire employees, and potentially OMBD if they qualify for minority business support. These resources are not currently integrated, and there is no clear mechanism for helping entrepreneurs understand which resources are relevant to their situation and in what sequence they should access them.

Validation research should document each existing initiative's objectives, target population, services offered, outcomes achieved, and potential synergies with other initiatives. This inventory should identify where initiatives are duplicating efforts, where gaps exist in service provision, and where coordination could improve outcomes for entrepreneurs. Such analysis would support more strategic resource allocation and could potentially surface opportunities to consolidate some services or create better handoff mechanisms between programs.

### Sustainable Ecosystem Development Versus Temporary Initiatives

Many of Richmond's business support initiatives depend on external funding, corporate partnerships, or temporary appropriations. Capital One's Michael Wassmer Innovation Center depends on Capital One's corporate commitment and employee volunteer time[49]. Startup Virginia and Lighthouse Labs depend on philanthropic funding and corporate support[15][37]. Career Services and OCWB programs depend on federal workforce funding and city budget allocations[7]. The city's new funding initiatives—including the $250,000 for commercial corridor facade improvements, the Carytown Business Improvement District seed funding, and economic development performance grants—are documented in the FY27 budget but may not be continued in future budget cycles depending on city priorities and revenue availability[32][32].

Validation research should examine the funding sustainability of each major business support initiative, identifying which are one-time investments and which represent ongoing commitments. For initiatives funded through external sources or temporary appropriations, the research should document what conditions would lead to program continuation or discontinuation and what would happen to entrepreneurs currently served by these programs if funding ends. This research would support more realistic planning about what support services can be promised to entrepreneurs and would identify areas where the city might need to invest ongoing resources to sustain critical services.

## Clear Output Expectations and Measurement Framework Development

### Defining Success Metrics and Outcome Indicators

The City of Richmond has articulated goals related to business support, small business equity, and economic opportunity in multiple policy documents and budget proposals[32][32]. The city aims to reduce compliance burden on small businesses, increase contracting opportunities for minority-owned businesses, support workforce development and job creation, and activate underused commercial corridors. However, there is minimal public documentation of specific success metrics or measurable outcome indicators for these goals.

Validation research should develop a comprehensive measurement framework defining what success would look like for Richmond's business support ecosystem. Specific metrics might include numbers like: percentage of business owners who report that the licensing process is easy to understand and complete; percentage of businesses that report being able to access necessary permits and approvals within documented timelines; percentage of small business owners aware of city support services; percentage of minority-owned businesses that are registered for city contracting consideration; percentage of entrepreneurs who report that they found needed mentoring and support; percentage of new businesses that survive their first three years; percentage of businesses founded by residents from disadvantaged communities; job creation by type and quality level; average wages of jobs created; and revenue generated by minority-owned businesses on city contracts.

For each metric, the research should identify data sources, collection mechanisms, baseline data if available, and realistic targets for improvement. This measurement framework would enable the city to track progress, identify where interventions are working, and adjust strategies based on evidence rather than assumptions.

### Output Format and Deliverable Specifications

The search results do not specify what format Richmond's business support improvements should take—whether a redesigned portal, a printed or digital service blueprint, a data tool or dashboard, a staff training program, community education materials, or some combination. Without clarity on output format and specification, efforts to improve the system risk producing materials that do not address the actual problems or that are not accessible to intended users.

Validation research should involve deliberate co-design processes with intended users—business owners, entrepreneurs, city staff, business support organizations, and community leaders—to specify what outputs would actually address their needs. For some problems, a simple, comprehensive written guide might be most useful. For others, an interactive online tool, a mobile app, or in-person facilitation might be more appropriate. For regulatory process complexity, a visual flowchart or decision tree might help where written descriptions fail. Different user groups may need different formats. The specification process should result in clear deliverable descriptions including purpose, intended users, features and functionality required, success criteria for each deliverable, and timeline for completion.

## Recommendations for Advancing Critical Research and Validation

### Immediate Priority Research Projects

The research gaps identified above fall into several categories with different levels of urgency. Certain validation needs should be addressed immediately, within the next two weeks, because they directly impact decisions about how to sustain or expand initiatives before the March 29, 2026 transition date. These immediate priorities include identifying and documenting named continuation champions for each major initiative, assessing technical accessibility of existing digital platforms (portals, data dashboards, directories), and beginning preliminary research on small business owner awareness and satisfaction with current support systems.

Medium-term research priorities, to be completed within two months, include comprehensive business registration journey mapping by business type, detailed program accessibility assessment across workforce and small business support services, and stakeholder engagement documentation related to recent policy initiatives. These projects require more extensive data collection and analysis but would produce foundational information needed for better system design.

Longer-term research investments, extending beyond the March 29 transition date, should include longitudinal tracking of business outcomes, periodic measurement against success metrics once established, and ongoing assessment of whether support systems are reaching all Richmond residents equitably. These sustained research commitments demonstrate institutional commitment to continuous improvement and evidence-based decision-making.

### Organizational and Partnership Recommendations

The successful advancement of this research and the implementation of resulting recommendations will require sustained organizational commitment and partnerships. The Office of Community Wealth Building, Department of Economic Development, Department of Finance, Office of Minority Business Development, and Department of Planning and Development Review should establish a formal Business Support Coordination Council meeting at least quarterly to share data, coordinate initiatives, and address identified gaps. Representation should include city staff from each relevant department, representatives from major business support organizations (Startup Virginia, Lighthouse Labs, SCORE, VCU entrepreneurship programs), and business community representatives.

Additionally, the city should commission research from external evaluators (potentially a local university research center or experienced consulting firm) to conduct the comprehensive user research, outcome evaluation, and program assessment outlined above. While city staff can contribute valuable internal knowledge, external evaluators bring objectivity and research expertise that can strengthen findings and recommendations. The research should be publicly reported, creating accountability for addressing identified gaps and transparency about the state of Richmond's business support system.

## Conclusion: The Path Forward for Richmond's Business Support Ecosystem

Richmond has invested significantly in business support infrastructure, regulatory modernization, minority business equity, workforce development, and entrepreneurship support. The city's recent upgrades to the RVA Business Portal, implementation of BPOL tax threshold increases, establishment of the TechDesk program, and sustained commitment to accelerator programs like Lighthouse Labs demonstrate serious institutional commitment to supporting business growth and economic opportunity. However, the research and validation gaps identified in this report reveal that significant work remains to ensure these investments are maximized, accessed equitably, and sustained beyond current budget cycles and leadership tenures.

The most urgent need is to identify and formally document the individuals, teams, and departments committed to championing each major initiative forward, understanding that Richmond's business support ecosystem will only remain strong if specific people and organizations accept ongoing responsibility for maintaining and improving these systems. Equally urgent is validation of whether digital platforms genuinely serve their intended users and whether support services reach all Richmond residents regardless of demographic characteristics or geographic location.

Completing the research and validation projects outlined in this report would provide Richmond with a clearer understanding of what business support services are actually working, where gaps and barriers exist, and what improvements would have meaningful impact. This evidence would support more strategic resource allocation, more equitable distribution of support across communities and business types, and more realistic planning for sustainable ecosystem development. As the city approaches the March 29, 2026 transition point, investing in this foundational research now would strengthen continuity, improve outcomes, and ensure that Richmond's commitment to supporting entrepreneurs of all backgrounds translates into systems that genuinely serve those entrepreneurs' needs.
