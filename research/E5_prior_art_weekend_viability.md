# Prior-Art Weekend Viability
*An operational guide for practitioners on how the one-year grace period is treated when the deadline falls on a Saturday, Sunday, or Federal holiday.*

## Executive Summary
Under the Leahy-Smith America Invents Act (AIA), patent applicants are afforded a one-year grace period for public disclosures under 35 U.S.C. 102(b)(1)(A) [1] [2]. A critical operational question arises when this exact one-year deadline falls on a weekend or federal holiday. According to 37 CFR 1.7 and MPEP 2153, if the one-year grace period ends on a Saturday, Sunday, or federal holiday, the deadline is officially extended to the next succeeding business day [3] [4]. This rule provides a vital strategic buffer for patent practitioners, ensuring that inventors do not lose their grace period simply because the United States Patent and Trademark Office (USPTO) is closed [5]. However, practitioners must carefully navigate filing mechanics, particularly avoiding paper mail deposits on weekends, to ensure the effective filing date is properly recorded without requiring costly petitions for correction [6].

## Background & Statutory Basis
The America Invents Act (AIA) preserved a one-year grace period under 35 U.S.C. 102(b)(1)(A) [1]. When the effective filing date of a patent application is calculated, the deadline is exactly one year after the earliest public disclosure by the inventor, unless an exception applies [2]. 

A critical exception occurs when that date lands on a non-business day. The one-year grace period in AIA 35 U.S.C. 102(b)(1)(A) is extended to the next succeeding business day if the end of the one-year grace period otherwise falls on a Saturday, Sunday, or federal holiday [3]. This statutory safeguard prevents inventors from being penalized by the USPTO's operating schedule, ensuring they receive the full benefit of the 365-day window to file their application after a public disclosure [7].

## Regulatory Implementation (37 CFR 1.7 & MPEP 2153)

The statutory basis is implemented through specific federal regulations and USPTO manual procedures. The following table outlines the primary regulatory frameworks governing weekend and holiday extensions.

| Regulatory Source | Provision Focus | Operational Rule |
|-------------------|-----------------|------------------|
| **37 CFR 1.7** | Times for taking action; Expiration on Saturday, Sunday, or Federal holiday | When the last day for taking any action falls on a Saturday, Sunday, or Federal holiday within the District of Columbia, the action may be taken on the next succeeding business day [4] [8]. |
| **MPEP 2153** | Prior Art Exceptions Under 35 USC 102(b)(1) | Explicitly states that the one-year grace period is extended to the next succeeding business day if it otherwise falls on a Saturday, Sunday, or federal holiday [3]. |
| **MPEP 505** | Date of Receipt Stamp | The Patent and Trademark Office is not open for the filing of correspondence on any day that is a Saturday, Sunday, or Federal holiday [5]. |
| **MPEP 512** | Certificate of Mailing | The date indicated on the Certificate of Mailing will be used to determine if the paper was deposited in the United States Postal Service in a timely manner [6]. |

### Why the rule matters
* **Predictability:** Inventors can plan disclosures without fearing a weekend cliff where they effectively lose one or two days of their grace period [7]. 
* **Risk mitigation:** Counsel can advise filing on the last business day before a weekend or intentionally utilize the Monday extension to secure the earliest possible effective filing date while maximizing preparation time.

## Practical Implications for Filing Strategy

To effectively leverage these rules, patent practitioners must integrate specific timing checks into their docketing systems.

1. **Determine the critical date:** Take the disclosure date, add one calendar year, then check the day of the week. If it lands on a Saturday, Sunday, or Federal holiday, shift the deadline to the next business day [3] [4]. 

2. **Document the actual filing date:** The USPTO's Certificate of Mailing or electronic filing timestamp serves as the official record of submission [6]. 

3. **Leverage the extension intentionally:** When a disclosure occurs on a Friday, filing on the following Monday (instead of the preceding Friday) still satisfies the one-year deadline, providing an extra weekend buffer for application preparation [3]. 

4. **Beware of mail-room timing:** Paper filings deposited on a Saturday are not considered received until the next business day, as the USPTO is not open for filing correspondence on weekends [5].
