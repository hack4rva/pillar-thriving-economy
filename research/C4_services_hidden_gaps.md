# Hidden or Hard-to-Use Interventions: Barriers to Service Discovery and Integration

**This report examines a critical gap in service delivery systems: the extensive problem of available interventions, treatments, and support services that remain hidden from users, are difficult to interpret, or cannot be connected into coherent care pathways. Through synthesis of implementation science research, health services literature, and user experience studies, this analysis identifies specific barriers to intervention discoverability and proposes systematic approaches to surface and sequence services effectively. The findings reveal that the absence of unified navigation systems, poor information architecture, fragmented organizational structures, and communication failures create an "intervention visibility crisis" affecting vulnerable populations disproportionately. Evidence-based solutions emphasize the need for integrated pathways, simplified access mechanisms, human navigation support, and transparent system design.**

## The Phenomenon of Intervention Invisibility and Inaccessibility

The problem of available services that remain underutilized due to poor visibility and accessibility represents a fundamental failure in health and social service systems. This phenomenon, which researchers describe using various terminology including "missingness" in healthcare, the "treatment gap," the "knowledge-practice gap," and the "intervention-implementation gap," reflects a systemic disconnect between what services exist and what populations actually access.[1][28] The core issue extends beyond simple awareness deficits; it encompasses a complex web of structural, communicative, organizational, and technological barriers that render even well-designed interventions invisible or incomprehensible to potential users.

The scope of this problem is substantial. Research demonstrates that **individuals requiring assistance from formal support services frequently fail to access them, with a significant proportion of eligible people never engaging with available programs**.[25] In the context of healthcare transitions for children with special needs, studies identified that while multiple care interventions and implementation strategies exist, there is no consistent group of services or interventions being consistently used, meaning potential users encounter bewildering variation rather than clear, unified pathways.[2] Similarly, in mental health services, researchers have identified comprehensive "care gaps" encompassing not just treatment access but also the availability of psychosocial and physical health interventions that remain unavailable or unknown to those who need them.[42]

The consequences of intervention invisibility are not neutral. When services remain hidden or incomprehensible, **the populations most in need of support—those with complex social circumstances, limited resources, low health literacy, or multiple disadvantages—are least likely to access available help**.[1][8][25] This creates an inverse care effect, where those with the greatest needs face the highest barriers. Administrative complexity, fragmented systems, poor communication, and lack of integration between services compound the problem, creating what some researchers describe as a "labyrinth" that average consumers cannot navigate alone.[30]

Understanding this phenomenon requires examining multiple levels of intervention invisibility: the system level where services are scattered across organizations with no unified entry points, the organizational level where referral processes are unclear or cumbersome, the informational level where available services are poorly documented or described in inaccessible language, and the individual level where people lack knowledge about or ability to reach available services.

## Systemic Fragmentation and the Absence of Coherent Pathways

One of the most significant barriers to intervention discoverability stems from **fundamental fragmentation in how health and social services are organized and delivered**. Rather than presenting as integrated systems where users can move smoothly from one service to another, services typically exist as isolated silos with minimal communication or coordination.[29] This fragmentation occurs both horizontally (across different types of services at the same level of care) and vertically (between primary and specialized, or pediatric and adult services).

The consequences of this fragmentation are severe. In outpatient settings, researchers found that **gaps in information at visit closure—where clinical intent and referral decisions are not systematically captured—represent a common but underrecognized source of continuity breakdown**. When patients are referred from one service to another, the referral loop frequently fails to close due to poor information systems, unclear responsibilities, and lack of coordination mechanisms. A healthcare system implementing structured referral pathways found that without systematic capture and operational support for referrals, referral volumes were fewer than two per week, indicating that referrals were happening verbally and informally rather than through documented, trackable systems.

The fragmentation problem is particularly acute in pediatric-to-adult care transitions, where children with special healthcare needs encounter dramatically different service configurations between systems.[2] The research explicitly identifies that "adult models of care cannot effectively accommodate children with special healthcare needs transitioning from pediatric services," creating a gap where services simply do not exist in coordinated form.[2] This is not a case of services being unavailable; rather, they exist in incompatible configurations that cannot be navigated as a coherent pathway.

Similar fragmentation affects mental health care, where treatment services, psychosocial interventions, and physical health care exist in separate systems with different funding, governance, and access mechanisms.[42] Individuals with mental health conditions who need coordinated care across these domains must navigate multiple, disconnected entry points, eligibility criteria, and provider networks.

**The solution to fragmentation requires more than communication; it requires structural integration at the organizational level**.[29] Research on integrated care pathways shows that simply adding more interventions without addressing the underlying fragmentation creates additional confusion rather than solving the problem. Effective integration requires formal agreements about financial responsibility, information sharing protocols, and coordinated governance structures between previously separate organizations.[29]

## Information Architecture Failures and Service Invisibility

Beyond organizational fragmentation, many available interventions fail to achieve visibility due to **poor information architecture—the way information about services is organized, labeled, presented, and made discoverable to users**.[16][25] Service visibility problems manifest in several specific ways that render interventions effectively hidden despite their availability.

First, services often lack clear, centralized information repositories where potential users can learn what exists.[25] Individuals seeking support frequently report not knowing what services are available, where to find them, or what specific programs they offer.[8][25] In one comprehensive study of barriers to accessing community resources, participants consistently described information gaps as a major obstacle, with many people unaware of available services or unable to determine their eligibility.[8] This information invisibility persists even when organizations maintain websites or brochures; the information is scattered, uses professional jargon, and is not organized around user needs or pathways through the system.

Second, when information about interventions is available, it is often **presented in ways that do not align with how users think about their problems or needs**.[8][16] Service systems tend to organize themselves around organizational structure (which department provides what) rather than around patient problems or journeys (what sequence of services would solve my problem). A patient seeking support for managing chronic pain, for example, may not know whether to start with physical therapy, a pain management clinic, a primary care physician, a community health program, or an online intervention program—each potentially valuable—but no system guides them toward understanding these options as part of a coherent pathway.[8]

Third, the **linguistic and conceptual frameworks used to describe services create significant accessibility barriers**.[20][30][47] Services are typically described using professional terminology, organizational categories, and administrative language that differs substantially from how potential users conceptualize their needs. Research on communication effectiveness shows that when organizations use jargon, unclear categories, or complex descriptions, adoption rates plummet.[20][47] This is particularly problematic for individuals with limited literacy, cognitive impairments, or those experiencing crisis who struggle to decode complex informational materials.[8][25]

Fourth, services suffer from what might be called **discoverability deficits in the user experience sense**. User experience research identifies that **users can navigate and access interfaces more successfully when features, functions, and content are intuitively discoverable**, with clear navigation, consistent design language, and minimal cognitive load.[16] Service systems typically fail on these dimensions—there are inconsistent pathways to discovery, varied application processes across different services, and no clear sense of what comes next in a treatment or support pathway.[16]

The solution requires redesigning service information systems around user needs and user mental models rather than organizational structures.[16][24][47] This includes creating unified entry points where users can search services by their problem or need rather than organizational affiliation, using plain language descriptions that translate professional terminology into user-friendly language, and organizing information to show clear pathways connecting related services.[16][24][47]

## Administrative and Bureaucratic Barriers to Service Access

Even when interventions are visible and conceptually understood by potential users, **significant administrative and procedural barriers often prevent engagement**. These barriers are not accidental; they frequently result from policy choices designed to restrict access or shift costs.[31] However, regardless of intent, they render otherwise available interventions functionally inaccessible to many who need them.

Research on administrative burdens in safety net programs identifies multiple specific barriers that prevent eligible individuals from accessing available services:[31] Complex application processes requiring extensive paperwork, lengthy wait times for processing or service provision, strict eligibility criteria that create confusing inclusions and exclusions, frequent recertification requirements that demand repeated documentation, invasive questioning requirements, and limited or inflexible contact methods for applying or following up.[31] Each of these barriers is individually significant; in combination, they create a cumulative burden that deters access, particularly for individuals already struggling with poverty, disability, unstable housing, or limited time availability.

In healthcare specifically, the administrative complexity surrounding insurance coverage creates substantial barriers to understanding what services are available and whether they are covered.[30] The "complex labyrinth" of managed care networks, utilization review requirements, prior authorization, and varying coverage rules means that **half of insured adults report having difficulty understanding at least some aspects of their health insurance coverage**.[30] Provider directories are frequently inaccurate, formulary information is complex and updated inconsistently, and the rules about in-network versus out-of-network services create confusion.[30] These administrative complexities are not bugs in the system; they reflect policy choices about how services are organized and funded, but the net effect is that many people cannot determine whether a service they need is actually available to them.

Research also demonstrates that **complex appointment scheduling mechanisms and cumbersome contact procedures create barriers particularly for vulnerable populations**. In one study examining barriers to accessing community resources, participants frequently reported struggling to make initial contact with service providers, encountering back-and-forth phone calls, being unable to connect despite repeated attempts, or receiving inconsistent information from different staff members.[8] These procedural difficulties are especially burdensome for individuals in crisis, those without reliable transportation or phone access, and those with cognitive or communication impairments.[8]

Solutions to administrative barriers require **systematic redesign of access processes to eliminate unnecessary complexity**.[31] This includes simplifying application processes, reducing documentation requirements, automating eligibility determinations where possible, expanding contact methods beyond phone calls (including online, text, email, and in-person options), reducing or eliminating appointment scheduling barriers through walk-in options or online scheduling, and providing customer navigation support to help people navigate remaining requirements.[31]

## Referral Process Failures and Broken Continuity

One particularly important category of intervention invisibility involves **failures in referral processes that break the continuity of care**. Even when a healthcare provider or social service worker recognizes that a client needs an intervention and makes an appropriate referral, the referral frequently fails to connect the client to that intervention.[2][8] The referral process represents a critical juncture where visibility and accessibility failures can completely prevent access to needed care.

Research on pediatric-to-adult care transitions demonstrates this problem vividly. **Despite clinical recognition that children with special healthcare needs require specific interventions during transition, providers often lack clear referral pathways, face delays in referral transmission, and cannot verify whether referrals were received or acted upon**.[2] Missing or inadequate transition planning documentation, incomplete treatment histories, and delays stemming from limited capacity among adult providers all contribute to referrals failing to connect.[2]

In general outpatient care, structured evaluation of referral processes revealed that **before implementing standardized referral systems, the average number of documented inter-clinic referrals was fewer than two per week**, indicating that most referrals were happening verbally or informally rather than through trackable systems. After implementation of a structured referral system with visible documentation and tracking mechanisms, referral volume increased to more than 800 per week within four months, and more than 70% of referrals resulted in a scheduled appointment or same-day access, demonstrating effective closure of the referral loop.

The barriers to effective referral completion are multiple. They include **unclear referral pathways and acceptance criteria at receiving organizations, lack of coordination between referring and receiving providers, absent or delayed information transfer about patients, insufficient follow-up to verify referral completion, and patient-level barriers such as transportation difficulties, scheduling conflicts, or inability to contact the receiving provider**.[2][8]

Solutions require what might be called "referral system engineering"—creating structured, documented, tracked referral processes with clear accountability for each step, technology systems that support information transfer and tracking, designated responsibility for follow-up and problem-solving when referrals stall, and support for patients in navigating the transition between providers.

## Communication Gaps and Knowledge-Practice Disparities

A distinct but critical category of intervention invisibility involves **failures in communication and knowledge transfer that prevent implementation of evidence-based interventions even when providers and organizations know about them**. This phenomenon, described as the "research-practice gap" or "knowledge-practice gap," reflects a systemic failure to translate implementation science knowledge into practice.

Research demonstrates that **despite decades of implementation science research producing extensive evidence about how to successfully implement interventions, healthcare practitioners frequently do not apply this knowledge**. This is not because practitioners are resistant or incompetent; rather, implementation knowledge is not systematically taught in basic healthcare training and only rarely in continuing education. As a result, practitioners are not expected to be knowledgeable about implementation science and lack practical guidance on how to actually use implementation knowledge in real-world contexts.

The consequence is paradoxical: **the field of implementation science has produced knowledge of great relevance to healthcare improvement, but this knowledge remains predominantly in the domain of researchers rather than practitioners**. Practitioners attempting to implement evidence-based interventions often struggle without the benefit of implementation science frameworks, may not understand why their implementation efforts fail, and lack systematic approaches to learning from implementation failures and adapting their strategies.

This gap manifests in multiple ways. Healthcare teams implementing new interventions often do not adequately engage stakeholders, do not assess organizational readiness, do not systematize feedback loops, do not track implementation fidelity, and do not adapt based on contextual barriers.[43] Without implementation science guidance, teams are essentially "winging it"—attempting to make interventions work without systematic methods for understanding what works in their specific context.

Solutions require what researchers call "practical implementation science"—translating implementation science research into user-friendly tools, resources, and processes that practitioners can actually use. This includes developing implementation frameworks in accessible formats, providing training on implementation science concepts to healthcare teams, creating implementation planning tools, and establishing communities of practice where practitioners can learn from others' implementation experiences.

## Transportation, Location, and Physical Access Barriers

An often-overlooked category of intervention invisibility involves **geographic and physical barriers that render services effectively inaccessible despite their existence**. Even when individuals know about and want to access interventions, **transportation difficulties, long distances to service locations, and lack of flexible service hours create insurmountable barriers**.[8][25][31]

Research on barriers to accessing community resources identifies transportation and location as consistent obstacles, particularly affecting individuals with limited financial resources, mobility impairments, rural residence, or those without reliable transportation.[8][25] In one study, participants frequently described how reaching service locations was physically difficult, time-consuming, and costly, creating disincentives to access despite recognizing their need for support.[8] For individuals with chronic health conditions causing mobility limitations, the physical effort required to reach a service location can exceed their capacity.[8]

Transportation barriers are not uniform across populations. **Individuals living in rural areas may face service locations many hours away; individuals with disabilities face inaccessible transportation or lack of accommodations; individuals without private vehicles depend on public transportation that may be limited or expensive; and individuals in crisis or experiencing severe mental health symptoms may lack the capacity to navigate complex transportation systems**.[8][25]

The digital divide adds a related dimension of access barriers. While digital services can overcome geographic barriers, **lack of reliable internet access, unaffordable data plans, outdated devices, and limited digital literacy prevent many individuals from accessing online interventions**.[11] In one comprehensive analysis, researchers noted that even when basic internet access exists, significant secondary barriers prevent benefiting from digital services, including poor website accessibility for people with disabilities, complex digital interfaces, and lack of digital skills.[11]

Solutions require multifaceted approaches including: **ensuring services are distributed geographically with multiple access points; providing transportation support through vouchers, subsidized rides, or volunteer transportation; offering flexible service hours including evening and weekend availability; developing telehealth and online options to overcome geographic barriers; ensuring digital services are fully accessible and mobile-friendly; and providing human support to help individuals navigate access challenges**.[8][23][25][46]

## System Complexity and Information Overload

Beyond specific service barriers, **individuals navigating health and social service systems frequently experience information overload, making it impossible to understand and act on available options**.[30] The sheer volume, complexity, and interconnectedness of information required to successfully access services exceeds the processing capacity of many users.

In the healthcare domain, this manifests as **policy complexity around insurance coverage, prior authorization requirements, provider networks, and cost-sharing that creates impossible decision-making contexts for consumers**.[30] When half of insured adults struggle to understand their coverage, this is not a personal failure but a system design failure that makes available services incomprehensible to users.[30] The complexity is compounded when individuals must make these determinations while facing health crises, limited health literacy, or cognitive impairment.

Research on information overload identifies that excessive information creates measurable performance losses, affecting both the quality of decisions and the likelihood of taking action. When individuals face too many options, unclear trade-offs, confusing terminology, and complex decision trees, they frequently respond by either avoiding decision-making or making poor decisions by default. In the context of service access, information overload may lead individuals to give up entirely on accessing services rather than attempting to navigate impossible complexity.

The problem is exacerbated by fragmentation: **when services are not integrated, users must independently gather information from multiple sources, translate between different terminology systems, and construct their own understanding of how services fit together**.[10][50] Different service systems use different terminology, follow different eligibility rules, maintain separate data systems, and communicate in different ways. Users must essentially become system translators, a cognitive burden that many cannot sustain.[10][50]

Solutions include: **reducing information complexity through careful curation and presentation; using clear, plain language descriptions; organizing information around user needs and journeys rather than organizational structure; integrating data systems so users do not need to contact multiple providers; and providing human navigation support to help users understand options and make decisions**.[16][24][46][47]

## Intervention Complexity and Unclear Sequencing

Some interventions remain hard to use not because they are hidden, but because their complexity is not adequately explained and users do not understand how to sequence steps through multi-component interventions. **Complex interventions with multiple components, decision points, and sequences of activities may not be accompanied by clear guidance about how to engage with them in order**.[4][24][40]

Research on adaptive interventions and intervention sequencing demonstrates that when interventions involve multiple stages and decision points, success depends on users understanding the logic of sequencing.[4][40] In clinical contexts, decisions about whether to start with one intervention versus another, or how to sequence interventions for optimal effect, require understanding of underlying theory and evidence that is often not transparently communicated to users.[4]

Additionally, when individuals need to coordinate across multiple services—such as children transitioning between pediatric and adult care, or individuals with multiple health and social needs requiring support from different providers—the **lack of clear intervention sequencing creates confusion about what to access first, what comes next, and how services interconnect**.[2][15][21]

Solutions require developing what researchers call "intervention logic models" that transparently explain how multi-component interventions work, what sequence they should be engaged in, what decisions determine sequencing, and what outcomes each component contributes to.[40] Additionally, coordinated care pathways should explicitly show sequencing of services and decision points where users or providers determine next steps.[21]

## Digital Design and User Experience Failures

The emerging use of digital platforms and online interventions introduces new categories of invisibility and inaccessibility. **When digital services are designed without attention to user experience principles, accessibility standards, or inclusive design, they become invisible or unusable to many users despite theoretical availability**.[16][45][46]

Research on discoverability in user experience design identifies that **many digital services fail because users cannot understand, find, identify, or access features and functions within interfaces**.[16] Users get lost in navigation, cannot find what they are looking for, do not understand labels or categories, or encounter interfaces that are cognitively overwhelming.[16] For services targeting health and wellness, poor digital design creates barriers precisely when individuals are least able to navigate complexity—during health crises, while managing chronic conditions, or while experiencing mental health challenges.

Accessibility failures specifically impact individuals with disabilities, older adults, and those with limited digital literacy.[11][45] Digital platforms designed without accessibility standards—including lacking keyboard navigation, missing alt text for images, poor color contrast, small fonts, and complex interaction patterns—become completely inaccessible to many users.[11][45][46]

Additionally, **assuming digital access when many populations lack reliable internet, appropriate devices, or digital skills effectively renders digital-only services invisible to significant segments of the population**.[11][45] The digital divide is not simply about device access; it encompasses sustained connectivity, sufficient data plans, device capability, and digital literacy.[11]

Solutions require applying user-centered design principles to digital services, conducting usability testing with representative users including those with disabilities, ensuring accessibility compliance, providing multiple access modalities beyond digital options, and offering human support for digital navigation.[16][24][45][46]

## Suggestions for Surfacing and Sequencing Hidden Interventions

Addressing the phenomenon of hidden or hard-to-use interventions requires comprehensive, systematic approaches operating at multiple levels simultaneously. No single solution addresses all dimensions of the problem; rather, effective intervention requires integrated strategies targeting system integration, information architecture, administrative simplification, and user support.

### System-Level Integration and Unified Pathways

**First and foundationally, health and social service systems must move toward greater integration and alignment** rather than continuing to exist as fragmented silos.[29] This requires formal mechanisms including integrated governance structures, shared funding models, aligned data systems, and coordinated service delivery standards.[29] While complete organizational mergers may not be feasible, intermediate approaches including regional alliances, shared patient-centered co-commissioning groups, and bundled payment models can align previously separate systems around shared outcomes.[48]

Integration should focus specifically on creating **coherent care pathways that guide users sequentially through related services in logical order**.[21] Rather than expecting users to understand how separate services connect, care pathways should be deliberately designed to show the sequence of services, decision points, and transitions between providers. Technology can support this through systems that track patient progress, identify the next appropriate step, and facilitate warm handoffs between providers.

Care coordination infrastructure is essential for managing transitions and ensuring continuity when patients move between services.[21][34] Designated care coordinators or navigators should have responsibility for guiding individuals through sequential steps, ensuring appointments are scheduled, addressing barriers to attendance, and following up when services do not connect as planned.[34][35]

### Information Architecture Redesign

**Second, service systems must fundamentally redesign how they organize and present information about available interventions**, shifting from organization-centered structures to user-centered information architectures.[16][24][47] This includes:

Creating unified entry points where users can search by problem, symptom, or need rather than having to know organizational names or service categories.[24][47] Rather than requiring someone to know "I should contact the behavioral health division" or "I need pediatric physical therapy services," systems should allow searching for "help managing anxiety" or "physical therapy for my child."

Developing comprehensive service directories that translate professional terminology into plain language, explain what each service does and how to access it, clarify eligibility criteria, and show connections between related services.[25][47] These directories should be designed for diverse literacy levels and should include visual elements, clear headings, and minimal jargon.

Organizing information around patient journeys and common problem pathways rather than organizational structure.[24][40][47] For example, rather than listing all pediatric services, then all adult services, then all mental health services, a patient-centered directory might show "Care pathways for children transitioning to adult care" that walks users through the sequence of steps and services typically needed during transition.

Implementing multiple search and discovery mechanisms acknowledging that users have different ways of finding services—some use search functions, others use browse and explore, others follow recommendations from providers or peers.[16] Service directories should support all discovery modalities.[16][24]

### Administrative Simplification and Access Barriers Reduction

**Third, organizations must systematically simplify administrative processes and reduce barriers to access**, recognizing that every procedural requirement excludes someone.[31] This includes:

Streamlining application processes by eliminating non-essential documentation requirements, automating eligibility determinations where possible, and using technology to pre-populate information that is already known to the system.[31] The goal should be to reduce application complexity to the minimum necessary for legitimate eligibility determination rather than adding requirements as a means of deterring access.

Expanding contact and application methods beyond phone calls to include online portals, secure email, text messaging, and in-person options, recognizing that individuals have varying access to phone service and different preferences for communication.[31][46] Walk-in intake options should be preserved for individuals who cannot plan ahead or schedule appointments.

Reducing or eliminating wait times for initial access by implementing systems that allow rapid assessment and same-day or next-day provision of services when appropriate. Technology and efficient workflow redesign can support reducing wait times in many contexts.

Providing navigation support to help individuals understand requirements, complete applications, and overcome logistical barriers.[31][35] Navigation can be provided by trained staff, peer navigators with lived experience of the service system, or a combination.[35]

### Referral System Engineering

**Fourth, organizations should implement structured, documented, tracked referral systems** that make referrals visible and accountable at each step, replacing informal verbal referrals. This includes:

Developing technology systems that capture referrals at the point of clinical decision-making rather than leaving referrals to informal communication. Electronic referral systems should include structured data about the reason for referral, urgency, clinical information needed by the receiving provider, and appointment preferences.

Establishing clear acceptance criteria and procedures at receiving organizations so referring providers understand how referrals are processed and can anticipate whether a referral will be accepted. Receiving organizations should provide structured feedback to referring providers about referral outcomes.

Implementing systematic follow-up and problem-solving for referrals that stall or fail to connect, identifying barriers (such as patient contact difficulty or insurance authorization issues) and working to overcome them. Accountability should be clear about who is responsible for follow-up.

Providing support to patients in navigating transitions between providers, including clear information about the next appointment, what to bring, what to expect, and how to reach the receiving provider if they have questions.

### Patient Navigation and Human Support

**Fifth, organizations should invest in patient navigation and care coordination roles**, recognizing that some individuals cannot navigate complex systems alone regardless of information architecture improvements.[17][35] Patient navigators should:

Understand the full ecosystem of available services and be able to guide individuals toward appropriate services based on their specific needs and circumstances.[35] Navigators should know not just what services exist but how they connect and what sequences make sense for different situations.

Provide emotional support and help individuals overcome psychological barriers to accessing services, including overcoming stigma, building confidence, and addressing trauma responses.[35] Access barriers are not only logistical; they are also emotional and psychological.

Address concrete logistical barriers by coordinating transportation, scheduling, financial navigation, and problem-solving when barriers emerge.[35] Navigators should have authority to solve problems rather than simply directing individuals to resources.

Maintain continuity of relationships over time as individuals move through services, helping ensure that engagement with one service leads appropriately to next services and that individuals do not fall through cracks between providers.[35]

Navigation can be provided by clinical staff, social workers, community health workers, peer navigators with lived experience, or technology-enabled systems.[35] The most effective models use combinations of human and technological support.

### Transparency in Intervention Logic and Sequencing

**Sixth, interventions and service pathways should be designed with transparency about their underlying logic, what components they include, how they sequence, and what decisions determine sequencing**.[4][40] This includes:

Developing clear logic models or theories of change that explain how multi-step interventions work and why sequencing matters.[40] These should be communicated in user-accessible formats, not just in academic papers.

Showing decision trees or algorithms that explain how providers or users should determine the next step in an intervention sequence.[4][40] When clinical judgment is involved, this should be made explicit rather than hidden in professional training.

Explaining what intermediate outcomes should occur to indicate progress and signal readiness for next steps.[40] Users should understand what success looks like at each stage and how to recognize it.

Providing flexibility in sequencing when evidence supports alternative sequences, while making this flexibility explicit rather than creating confusion through inconsistency.[4]

### Co-Design and User Involvement

**Seventh, service systems should involve actual and potential users in designing services, access processes, and information systems** through co-design and participatory approaches. Users bring invaluable knowledge about barriers, needs, and effective access approaches that experts may miss. Co-design processes should:

Involve diverse users including those with lived experience of service navigation, people from marginalized communities, individuals with disabilities, and others with specific barriers to access.

Include users in identifying problems in current systems, designing solutions, and testing prototypes of new access approaches.

Explicitly address equity and inclusion, ensuring that design choices do not inadvertently worsen access for vulnerable populations.

Continue user involvement through implementation and evaluation, learning from what actually works in practice rather than assuming design intentions are realized.

### Communication Campaigns and Public Awareness

**Eighth, organizations should invest in targeted communication and awareness campaigns** to help populations understand what services exist, how to access them, and where they can help.[26] Effective campaigns should:

Use diverse communication channels including mass media, social media, community organizations, peer networks, and direct outreach to reach different populations.[26] Different populations access information differently and through different trusted sources.

Employ trusted messengers who have credibility within specific communities to communicate about services.[26] Messages delivered by community members, respected leaders, or individuals with lived experience carry more weight than messages from institutions.

Employ consistent messaging over time through periodic campaigns rather than one-time announcements, recognizing that awareness-building is an ongoing process.[26]

Use plain language, visual communication, and multiple languages to ensure accessibility of campaign messaging.[26]

Frame messages positively and humanistically rather than relying solely on statistics, emphasizing how services help people achieve their goals and improve their lives.[26]

### Data Integration and Feedback Loops

**Ninth, organizations should integrate data systems and establish feedback loops** that allow monitoring of access patterns, identifying populations that are underserving themselves, and identifying specific barriers preventing access.[23] This includes:

Creating dashboards that show who is accessing services, who is not, and what their demographics and characteristics are, disaggregated by race, ethnicity, language, geography, disability status, and other relevant dimensions.[23] Many access disparities become visible only when data is disaggregated.

Establishing mechanisms for identifying specific barriers blocking access for particular populations—for example, if certain language groups have low service utilization, investigating whether language access is the barrier or whether the information architecture makes services invisible to that group.[23]

Creating feedback loops where barriers identified through data or user experience testing inform redesign of systems and processes.[23]

Regularly publishing transparency reports showing access patterns, identified barriers, and improvements being made, holding organizations accountable for progress on access and equity.[23]

### Implementation Science and Guided Improvement

**Tenth, organizations implementing service improvements should apply implementation science frameworks and methods** to increase the likelihood that improvements will actually be adopted and sustained. This includes:

Engaging implementation scientists or internal improvement teams trained in implementation science methods to guide improvement efforts.

Using structured frameworks such as the Consolidated Framework for Implementation Research (CFIR), the Implementation Research Logic Model, or the Quality Implementation Framework to systematically assess facilitators and barriers to implementation.[43]

Conducting stakeholder engagement and assessing organizational readiness before implementation to identify likely barriers and build necessary support.

Using quality improvement methodologies such as Plan-Do-Study-Act cycles to test improvements on small scales, learn from results, and refine approaches before full implementation.

Measuring implementation fidelity and engagement to understand whether improvements are being adopted as designed and used by end-users.

Establishing communities of practice where organizations implementing similar improvements can learn from each other's experiences.

## Conclusion and Path Forward

The phenomenon of available interventions remaining hidden or hard to use represents a critical gap between evidence and practice, between service availability and service utilization, and between what is theoretically accessible and what is functionally accessible to those who need it most. This gap particularly harms vulnerable populations—those with complex needs, limited resources, low health literacy, and multiple disadvantages—precisely those who most need coordinated, accessible service systems.

Addressing this problem requires moving beyond viewing service access as a problem of individual awareness or motivation and recognizing it as a systems problem requiring comprehensive solutions. No single intervention—not awareness campaigns alone, not better information systems alone, not simplified administrative processes alone—will solve the problem. Rather, addressing intervention invisibility requires integrated approaches that simultaneously address system fragmentation, information architecture, administrative barriers, referral processes, user support, and organizational implementation capacity.

The evidence presented in this analysis shows that effective solutions are possible and have been demonstrated in various contexts. Healthcare organizations implementing structured referral systems dramatically increased referral documentation and completion. Providers investing in patient navigation and care coordination improved access for vulnerable populations. Service systems redesigning around user-centered information architecture increased utilization. Communities implementing integrated care pathways improved continuity and outcomes.

The path forward requires commitment to systematic change at multiple levels: policy changes to align funding and governance across services, organizational investments in data systems and care coordination infrastructure, workforce development to prepare staff for integrated, user-centered service delivery, and sustained attention to equity ensuring that improvements reach populations most affected by access barriers. Most fundamentally, it requires recognizing that service system design is a matter of justice—that available interventions are only truly available when they are visible, understandable, reachable, and sequenced into coherent pathways to health and wellbeing.
