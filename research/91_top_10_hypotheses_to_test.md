The structural evidence supporting this hypothesis comes from multiple angles. However, research into government procurement barriers reveals a different picture. This suggests that the problem is not scarcity of opportunity but rather a mismatch between how opportunities are advertised and how potential vendors can identify which opportunities are relevant to their business.

Current procurement systems typically present opportunities through centralized portals such as SAM.gov or state-specific procurement sites, which organize contracts through systems designed for government internal use rather than for small business discoverability[38]. The interface often requires potential vendors to understand procurement terminology, navigate complex search filters, and decipher technical requirements written in government-specific language. For a first-time vendor or a minority business owner who may have limited experience with government contracting, this presentation creates a cognitive barrier distinct from the barrier of opportunity scarcity. The research on minority entrepreneurship and technical assistance bears out this hypothesis even more directly. By contrast, organizing opportunities through user-centered service categories—such as "janitorial and cleaning services," "technology and software development," "construction and repair," or "health and wellness services"—creates an immediate cognitive match with how business owners understand their own capabilities.

The underlying principle—that improved information presentation meaningfully affects behavior—appears repeatedly in research on government service uptake.

Further supporting evidence comes from research on how users navigate complex service ecosystems. The principle is straightforward but powerful: users can navigate systems more effectively when those systems are designed around how users think about problems rather than how government institutions organize themselves.

This transparency about sources and limits maintains trust better than polished presentation without grounding.

Research on human-centered policy design emphasizes that "prototyping to represent the policy toward shared understanding" should include transparent representation of rules and logic[42]. For a navigation prototype, this would mean making explicit the decision logic ("If you answer X about your business type, then you are directed to Y resource") rather than hiding this logic in a black box. Users are more likely to trust a system that makes its logic transparent, even if that logic is not perfectly comprehensive, than a system that makes confident recommendations without transparent grounding.

## Synthesis: Integrating the Hypotheses into a Coherent Framework

The ten hypotheses, examined individually through research evidence, integrate into a coherent framework about barriers to small business and procurement access, and about how targeted design interventions could meaningfully reduce those barriers. The framework moves from diagnosis (hypotheses 1-2) to root cause identification (hypotheses 3-6) to intervention design (hypotheses 7-10).

Hypotheses 1-2 establish that the problem is not scarcity of opportunities or programs but rather presentation, discovery, and navigation challenges. State and local governments spend over one trillion dollars on procurement, and the SBA maintains extensive networks of support organizations, but first-time vendors and minority business owners struggle to understand what opportunities are relevant and what the first steps should be. Plain-language simplification and service-category reorganization could directly address these challenges.

Hypotheses 3-6 establish that meaningful prototypes can be built with publicly available data and rule-based logic, and that the core problem can be addressed through information architecture improvements before attempting deep system integration. The problem is information fragmentation and sequencing confusion, not absence of information or programs. Reorganizing existing public information to provide clear, sequenced guidance would address the core barrier.

Hypotheses 7-10 establish that effective prototypes should be narrowly scoped to specific user personas and specific journey branches, should prioritize clarity and transparency over claims of automation, should produce useful artifacts for staff and partners, and should ground their recommendations transparently in official sources. These design principles reflect how civic technology actually achieves adoption and impact in government contexts.

The overall implication is that a practical path forward involves developing a discovery prototype or explainer tool focused on a specific user persona (e.g., minority-owned landscaping business seeking procurement opportunities, or a female entrepreneur at the early startup phase) and a specific journey branch (e.g., registration and first opportunity identification for procurement, or initial business formation for startup). The tool would organize publicly available information according to plain-language service categories and clear sequencing, implement rule-based logic to guide users through decision trees, produce useful artifacts (process maps, decision trees, categorization schemes) for organizational partners, and ground all recommendations transparently in official sources.

This approach would test the hypotheses while generating practical artifacts that could inform ongoing improvement efforts. If the hypotheses prove correct—that information fragmentation and sequencing confusion are the primary barriers—then this focused intervention would reduce user confusion and increase takeup. If the hypotheses prove incorrect—if, for instance, users still cannot navigate even with improved information architecture—the prototype would generate actionable insights about what the actual barriers are.

## Implications and Recommendations

The research synthesized through these ten hypotheses suggests several actionable recommendations for improving small business and procurement access in government contexts like Richmond:

First, government agencies and small business support organizations should collaboratively map current user journeys for key personas and explicitly identify sequencing confusion points. This mapping exercise would clarify where information is fragmented, where sequencing is unclear, and where rule-based navigation could help. The mapping should involve not just staff but also actual users and entrepreneurs who represent the target personas.

Second, support organizations should work together to develop shared decision trees and process guides that help users understand sequences and connections between different programs and services. Rather than each organization optimizing its own program, a coordinated approach would present a unified view of how different programs connect. This could be done through a shared informational artifact—a guide, a website page, or a tool—without requiring deep system integration between organizations.

Third, business support organizations should conduct plain-language review of their public materials and procurement information to identify confusing terminology and reorganize information by user task rather than by government department or program. This might involve translation of terms (from "small disadvantaged business" to "businesses owned by people from historically underrepresented groups") and reorganization of content.

Fourth, a pilot prototype project should be initiated to test the hypotheses about what types of information architecture and design approaches most effectively help first-time vendors and early-stage entrepreneurs navigate the business startup and procurement systems. This prototype should be narrowly scoped to specific personas, should prioritize transparency about sources and limitations, and should focus on producing artifacts useful to staff and partners.

Finally, scaling of successful prototypes should occur through artifacts and standardization rather than through software expansion. If a navigation approach, a categorization scheme, or a decision tree proves effective, it should be standardized, documented, and used across multiple organizations and channels rather than locked into a single software tool.

## Conclusion

The ten hypotheses examined in this research suggest that barriers to small business and procurement access are fundamentally information architecture and user experience challenges rather than absence of opportunities or programs. Evidence from government procurement research, civic technology initiatives, human-centered design, and policy analysis consistently supports this diagnosis. The hypotheses further suggest that focused, narrowly-scoped prototype interventions can meaningfully test whether improved information presentation would reduce confusion and increase access. The most promising path forward involves developing tools and artifacts that help small business owners and minority entrepreneurs understand what opportunities are relevant to them, what sequence of steps they need to follow, and what concrete next steps to take.

By focusing design efforts on clarity, plain language, explicit sequencing, transparent grounding in official sources, and production of useful organizational artifacts, prototypes can generate genuine impact while producing insights that inform ongoing improvements. The hypotheses represent a testable framework for understanding barriers and for evaluating whether specific interventions meaningfully reduce those barriers. Investment in this type of focused, evidence-based redesign of information architecture and user experience in government procurement and small business support systems has potential to expand opportunity access for entrepreneurs and small businesses that currently navigate these systems with difficulty.
