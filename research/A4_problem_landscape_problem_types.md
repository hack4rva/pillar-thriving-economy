# Problem Landscape Classification: Differentiating Information-Access, Navigation, Interpretation, and Institutional Process Problems

This report presents a comprehensive framework for classifying problems encountered in digital products and organizational systems across four distinct categories: information-access problems, navigation problems, interpretation problems, and institutional process problems. By distinguishing between these problem types, organizations can make more informed decisions about what software can realistically address and what requires systemic organizational change. Understanding this landscape is critical for defining appropriate minimum viable product (MVP) scope, as misalignment between problem type and solution approach consistently leads to implementation failures, wasted resources, and unmet user needs. The analysis that follows draws on research from user experience design, organizational change management, information architecture, and software feasibility studies to provide actionable guidance for practitioners determining which problems merit technical investment and which demand institutional intervention.

## Information-Access Problems: The Challenge of Finding What You Need

Information-access problems represent situations where users possess the information they require somewhere within a system or organization, but they struggle to locate or retrieve it when needed[20][28]. These problems manifest when information exists but remains invisible, undiscoverable, or improperly indexed within the information environment. The distinction between information-access problems and other problem types is crucial because information-access challenges are often the most directly addressable through software and information architecture improvements. When studying information wayfinding behavior, researchers identified three primary modes through which users interact with information: the locate mode, the explore mode, and the meander mode[20]. In locate mode, users know precisely what they are looking for but need help discovering where to find it[20]. This is fundamentally an information-access problem. A user might know they need an expense filing deadline, but they cannot find it on a company website; they know they need a specific tax regulation, but it is buried in documentation; or they need contact information for a particular department but cannot navigate to the right page.

The severity and scope of information-access problems can vary dramatically across organizational contexts. In some cases, information exists but is poorly labeled or categorized, making retrieval difficult despite technically correct organizational systems. In other cases, information is entirely absent from systems where users reasonably expect to find it, creating gaps between user mental models and actual system architecture. Research on digital privacy and information management reveals how widespread information-access problems have become in modern organizations[6]. Organizations collect vast amounts of personal data but often struggle to give users visibility over what data is being collected, how it is being used, and where it is being shared[6]. This represents a fundamental information-access problem: the information exists in corporate databases, but individual users cannot access it or understand it in meaningful ways[6].

Information-access problems typically produce specific symptoms. Users may report spending excessive time searching for information, frequently contacting support or colleagues to locate resources, or expressing frustration that information should be easier to find[20]. Users might rely on workarounds—such as asking a colleague who might know or checking multiple sources sequentially—that indicate the formal information discovery system is inadequate. From a behavioral perspective, users may abandon tasks when information is difficult to access, particularly when they perceive the effort required as disproportionate to the value of the information sought.

The architectural response to information-access problems involves organizing information, labeling content clearly, and creating pathways that align with user mental models. Information architects address these problems through techniques like card sorting, which helps uncover how users naturally categorize information, and tree testing, which validates whether information hierarchies make sense to target users[7]. When properly designed, information architecture creates **labeled content**, **navigation systems**, and **search functionality** that work together to reduce access friction[7]. The root cause of information-access problems is almost always poor information architecture or inadequate labeling systems, not the absence of information itself. This is a critical distinction because it means information-access problems are fundamentally addressable through software-based solutions focused on organization, indexing, and discovery.

However, not all information-access problems are equal in terms of software tractability. Some information-access problems require solving institutional problems first. For example, if a company has not actually defined its policies on data use and sharing, then no information architecture solution can surface information that does not formally exist. Similarly, if organizational processes are poorly documented and exist only in people's heads, information-access problems cannot be solved through software design alone—the information must first be codified and documented. But where information exists and is documented, software and good information architecture can dramatically improve access. The research evidence strongly supports this: users perceive systems designed with clear information organization, consistent labeling, and effective search capabilities as significantly more usable than those lacking these features[49]. Information-access problems are therefore among the most software-tractable of the four problem types discussed in this report.

## Navigation Problems: Beyond Simple Wayfinding

Navigation problems, while related to information-access challenges, represent a distinct category of problems focused on how users move through and orient themselves within digital or physical information environments[1][20]. Navigation problems emerge when users can theoretically find information but struggle with the journey to reach it—when the path itself is confusing, inefficient, or creates cognitive burden that exceeds the value of the destination. The distinction matters because a user might technically be able to access information but may take so many steps, experience so many dead ends, or become so disoriented that they effectively fail to complete their goal.

Navigation problems manifest at multiple levels of user experience. At the most basic level, users may struggle with literal wayfinding—they do not understand how to move from one screen or location to another[20]. At a higher level, users may become lost not in the physical interface but in their conceptual understanding of where they are in relation to where they need to be. Navigation researchers have identified the importance of **positional cues** that tell users their current location within an information environment, similar to a "you are here" marker on a physical map[20]. Without such cues, users experience a sense of being lost, even if they are following a technically correct path.

The research on clinical decision support systems provides compelling evidence of how navigation design affects user confidence and performance[49]. In one study comparing two interface approaches, the "expand/contract" interface—which required users to progressively click through hierarchical levels to find information—produced significantly lower usability scores and user confidence than an "at-a-glance" interface that presented information in a more accessible format[49]. The accuracy of decisions was similar between the two approaches, but users felt less confident navigating the hierarchical interface, particularly when its depth required multiple clicks to access needed information[49]. This illustrates how navigation problems differ from information-access problems: the information was accessible through both interfaces, but the navigation path itself created friction and reduced confidence.

Navigation problems can also emerge from inconsistency in navigational patterns across different areas of a system[44]. When users learn how to navigate one section of an application or website, they develop mental models about how navigation should work. When they encounter a different navigation pattern in another section, they must rebuild their mental model, increasing cognitive load and the likelihood of becoming disoriented[44]. This consistency issue represents a distinct navigation problem: the information is accessible, but the path to accessing it varies unpredictably, creating confusion.

Organizations increasingly recognize that navigation problems extend beyond user interface design into broader information architecture and organizational workflows. The challenge of navigating complex cross-organizational processes represents a form of navigation problem that impacts both employees and external stakeholders[10]. For example, an order-to-cash process might span sales, finance, and operations departments, each with its own systems and workflows. Users must navigate across multiple systems and handoffs, and without clear navigation cues about what happens next or who is responsible for each step, the process feels broken even if no individual component is poorly designed[10]. This illustrates how navigation problems can be partially addressable through software (improving pathways and providing positional cues) but also require institutional fixes (clarifying ownership and standardizing handoffs).

The neuroscience of navigation impairment provides an interesting lens on this problem category[1]. Navigation researchers have identified specific cognitive domains involved in successful navigation: landmark recognition (identifying key reference points), location processing (understanding spatial positions), and path knowledge (knowing how to connect locations)[1]. When any of these domains is impaired, users struggle to navigate effectively, even if information is accessible. Digital systems often fail to support all three domains adequately. A system might clearly display relevant landmarks (key information) but fail to provide adequate location context (where the user is in a larger information structure) or clear paths forward (what to do next). Successful navigation design requires supporting all three domains through clear reference points, consistent positioning information, and well-marked paths.

## Interpretation Problems: Making Sense of What You Find

Interpretation problems arise not from difficulty accessing information or navigating to it, but from difficulty understanding its meaning, significance, or applicability once located[18][44]. These problems emerge when users successfully find information but struggle to comprehend it, apply it to their context, or extract value from it. Interpretation problems are qualitatively different from access and navigation problems because they are primarily cognitive rather than structural challenges. Users have found what they need; they simply cannot understand what it means.

The research on cognitive load provides essential context for understanding interpretation problems[21]. Cognitive load theory distinguishes between intrinsic load (the inherent complexity of the information being communicated), germane load (the effort required to process and integrate new information), and extraneous load (the load imposed by how information is presented)[21]. Interpretation problems often arise when extraneous load is excessive. A user might find a policy document (information-access problem solved) but the document is written in dense corporate language, uses undefined jargon, presents information in disorganized ways, and uses formatting that makes parsing difficult. The extraneous load—the effort required simply to understand the presentation—becomes so high that users either give up or make errors in interpretation.

Usability research consistently demonstrates how interpretation problems affect decision-making and task completion. When interfaces present information in ways that impose high cognitive load, users make more errors, take longer to complete tasks, and express lower confidence in outcomes[44]. This occurs even when the information technically exists and is theoretically findable. For example, a data visualization might technically display all necessary information but through a format that makes relationships difficult to discern, requiring viewers to perform extensive mental calculations to extract meaning[21]. A form might collect necessary information but through a sequence that does not align with how users naturally think about the domain, creating confusion about what information is required and why.

At the journey and relationship levels of customer experience, interpretation problems become even more consequential[18]. A user might understand individual interactions but fail to understand how those interactions connect into a larger journey. A customer completes a purchase, receives a shipping notification, sees a tracking update, and receives a delivery confirmation, but fails to understand at what point they can expect the package or what to do if they miss the delivery window. Each individual piece of information is accessible and navigable, but collectively they fail to create a coherent narrative that enables the user to interpret their customer journey. This represents an interpretation problem at the journey level.

The connection between interpretation problems and organizational culture cannot be overlooked. Information often fails to be interpreted correctly because users lack context about organizational objectives, values, or decision-making frameworks[11]. An employee might receive a strategic announcement but fail to interpret its significance for their day-to-day work because they do not understand how it connects to established organizational priorities[11]. This is not purely an information problem; it is an institutional problem about shared understanding, which creates an interpretation problem as a symptom.

Addressing interpretation problems through software involves multiple strategies. Information can be presented with reduced cognitive load through better visual organization, clearer language, progressive disclosure of complexity, and contextualization that helps users understand significance[21][44]. Data can be visualized in ways that make relationships and patterns more apparent[21]. Systems can provide scaffolding that guides users through complex interpretation tasks. However, some interpretation problems resist purely technical solutions. If an organization has not clarified what information means or how it should be applied, then no software interface can convey that clarity. If users lack the domain knowledge needed to interpret technical information, then presenting the information clearly helps but does not solve the fundamental knowledge gap.

## Institutional Process Problems: Where Software Reaches Its Limits

Institutional process problems represent the fourth and most complex category in this classification framework. These are problems embedded in how organizations actually operate, how decisions are made, how work is divided and coordinated, and how accountability is established. Institutional process problems cannot be solved by software alone because they are fundamentally about people, organizational structure, decision-making authority, and organizational culture. Software might address symptoms of institutional process problems or provide tools that enable better processes, but software cannot fix the underlying institutional issues.

The research on business process reengineering failure provides extensive evidence of this problem category[10][42]. Organizations invest substantial resources redesigning workflows to be more efficient, but the redesigned processes fail to produce expected improvements because they rely on unchanged institutional structures. Process redesign often assumes that work will flow smoothly across organizational boundaries, but in reality, unclear ownership, weak accountability, informal politics, and communication failures create friction at handoffs[10]. A beautifully designed workflow fails in practice because the institution does not support it. Similarly, process management efforts often fail not because the recommended processes are poorly designed but because organizational culture, reward structures, and informal power dynamics work against adoption[42].

One of the most instructive findings from organizational change research concerns what happens when redesigned processes go live[10]. Initially, there may be enthusiasm for new systems, but as day-to-day pressures return, people revert to familiar informal systems—email threads, hallway conversations, spreadsheets, and ad-hoc coordination—that provide the relationship-based flexibility they need but undermine the designed process[10]. This is an institutional problem masquerading as a software problem: the software is not "wrong," but the institutional context does not support the process it is designed to enable.

Institutional process problems frequently manifest as broken handoffs across teams and departments[29]. When work must transition from one person or team to another, a clear institutional process is necessary: Who is responsible for the handoff? What information must be transferred? How is success defined? What happens if information is incomplete? When these institutional questions are not answered, software cannot fill the gap. A workflow automation system can route work from one queue to another, but it cannot ensure that the receiving team understands the context, has the authority to act, or is held accountable for outcomes[29]. This is why so many business process management initiatives struggle: they automate the mechanics of processes without addressing the institutional foundations those processes require.

Resistance to change represents a major institutional process problem[32][32][46]. Research consistently demonstrates that 70% of large-scale organizational change efforts fail, and the primary reason is human resistance, not technical failure[42]. This resistance emerges from fear of the unknown, concerns about job security, lack of trust in leadership, misalignment with organizational culture, and failure to involve employees in change design[32]. No software system can overcome institutional resistance to change. At best, software can enable processes that leaders have successfully convinced people to adopt. At worst, software can amplify institutional problems by automating broken processes or imposing unwanted change on an unprepared organization.

Data governance and access control represent another category of institutional process problems[6]. Organizations struggle to implement data privacy protections, not because they lack technical tools, but because they lack institutional clarity about who is responsible for data protection, how to balance data utility with privacy, how to allocate resources across competing priorities, and how to enforce policies when business pressures push against them[6]. A technical system can enforce access controls, but it cannot resolve the institutional questions about what access should be granted, by whom, and under what circumstances.

The distinction between information-access problems and institutional process problems becomes especially clear when examining data visibility challenges. Organizations may lack visibility into data practices because the data infrastructure is inadequate (a technical problem), but they may also lack visibility because responsibility for data governance is unclear, data practices are undocumented, and departmental data activities are not coordinated with each other (institutional problems)[6]. Software can help solve the technical part but cannot solve the institutional part.

## Software-Addressable vs. Non-Addressable Constraints: A Practical Distinction

The distinction between problems that software can meaningfully address and problems that require institutional change is not always clear-cut, but it is crucial for making sound MVP and product strategy decisions. Software is most effective at addressing structural and design problems—how information is organized, how interfaces present choices, how data flows through systems, and how processes are mechanically executed. Software is least effective at addressing problems rooted in organizational culture, authority structures, incentives, and human resistance to change.

Information-access problems typically sit at the most software-addressable end of this spectrum[7][20]. When users struggle to find information because it is poorly organized, inadequately indexed, or inconsistently labeled, software solutions focused on information architecture and search functionality can substantially reduce friction. The research evidence strongly supports this: well-designed information architecture, clear labeling systems, and effective search significantly improve user ability to access information[20]. However, even information-access problems sometimes require institutional intervention. If information does not formally exist within organizational systems—if policies are undocumented, decisions are not recorded, or knowledge exists only in people's heads—then no information architecture solution will help until the information is first documented and made systematically available.

Navigation problems typically occupy the middle ground on the software-addressability spectrum. Navigation design improvements—clearer wayfinding, more consistent patterns, better positional cues, more efficient paths—can substantially improve user experience and task completion rates[49][20]. These are largely software-addressable problems. However, some navigation problems are rooted in institutional confusion about process flows, ownership, and handoffs that software cannot resolve. If organizational processes are not clearly defined, if responsibility is ambiguous, and if handoffs lack clear governance, then improving software navigation will only partially address the problem.

Interpretation problems also occupy ambiguous territory. Software can reduce extraneous cognitive load through better information presentation, clearer language, improved visual design, and contextual scaffolding[21][44]. These are software-addressable improvements. However, interpretation problems often stem from lack of shared organizational context or understanding. If an organization has not clarified what information means or how it should be applied, then software improvements to presentation will help but not fully solve the problem. If users lack domain knowledge, improved presentation helps but does not create the knowledge they need.

Institutional process problems are substantially less software-addressable. While software can enable better processes, it cannot create the institutional foundations those processes require. Software cannot establish clear authority and accountability, cannot overcome organizational resistance to change, cannot resolve conflicts between organizational priorities, and cannot create shared understanding of how work should be done. Where institutional problems exist, software implementation typically fails or produces only marginal improvements.

However, this categorization should not lead to the conclusion that software is useless for institutional problems. Rather, software can support institutional improvements if—and only if—the institutional foundation is already in place or is actively being addressed through change management, leadership commitment, and organizational redesign[10][46]. When organizations successfully implement business process management improvements, they typically combine process redesign, clear assignment of ownership, investment in change management, executive sponsorship, and enabling software tools. The software is one necessary ingredient but not sufficient on its own.

The research on software implementation failures provides clear evidence of this distinction[46]. When implementations fail, the reasons typically include poor system selection, unclear requirements, inadequate change management, resistance from employees, and lack of executive sponsorship—all institutional problems[46]. When implementations succeed, success typically correlates with clear requirements, strong change management, executive sponsorship, early involvement of employees, and identification of process owners who are accountable for outcomes[46]. The software that is selected matters, but institutional factors matter more.

## MVP Scope Implications: Focusing on Software-Addressable Problems

The classification of problems into information-access, navigation, interpretation, and institutional process categories has direct implications for defining appropriate MVP scope. MVP strategy should systematically prioritize software-addressable problems and either defer or separate institutional process problems from the MVP roadmap.

The most productive MVP approach focuses on information-access and navigation problems where institutional context is stable and reasonably clear[4][13][40]. These problems offer the best return on MVP investment because software improvements directly address user needs and can be validated through user feedback relatively quickly. An MVP focused on organizing information more effectively, making it more discoverable, or improving navigation patterns can be built, tested with real users, and iterated rapidly. These are the problems where MVP validation produces the clearest answers about whether the proposed solution addresses real user needs.

Interpretation problems require more careful consideration in MVP scoping. If an organization has not clarified how information should be presented and interpreted, then an MVP focused on interpretation improvement will struggle to find a stable target. The MVP might improve presentation clarity, but validation will be difficult because the organization has not agreed on what clear interpretation should look like. However, if an organization has done the institutional work to clarify language, domain understanding, and decision frameworks, then an MVP focused on reducing cognitive load and improving information presentation can produce valuable learning.

Institutional process problems should typically be addressed outside the MVP process or through a distinct institutional change initiative that precedes MVP development[46][50]. This is a critical principle: attempting to use MVP to solve institutional process problems typically results in failed implementations. An MVP cannot determine how to establish clear ownership, cannot resolve organizational resistance, and cannot create the institutional alignment necessary for process redesign success. When institutional process problems are addressed outside the MVP, they become constraints that shape MVP scope rather than targets for MVP innovation.

The practical implication of this framework is that MVP teams should conduct a comprehensive problem landscape analysis before defining scope. This analysis should classify problems according to the framework presented here, identify which problems are information-access or navigation focused (high software-tractability), which are interpretation focused (moderate software-tractability with institutional considerations), and which are institutional process problems (low software-tractability). The MVP should then be scoped to focus on high-tractability problems, with clear acknowledgment of institutional constraints and requirements.

Product teams frequently make the mistake of trying to use software to solve institutional problems, resulting in failed implementations and disappointed stakeholders[46]. A team might design an elegant workflow automation system, only to discover that the organization has not clarified decision authority, assigned ownership, or established the change management foundation the system requires. The software is not wrong; the institutional foundation is missing. MVP frameworks help avoid this error by requiring teams to distinguish between problem types upfront and make explicit choices about which problems the MVP will address.

Additionally, MVP teams should recognize that information-access and navigation problems often exist in concert with interpretation and institutional problems. A holistic problem landscape analysis must identify this interconnection. An information-access problem might exist because information is poorly organized (software-addressable) but also because organizational units are not cooperating to make information available (institutional). In such cases, the MVP might address the software component while institutional issues are being separately addressed, but the MVP success depends on both being tackled.

## Multi-Level Problem Manifestation and Interaction

The framework for classifying problems by type (information-access, navigation, interpretation, institutional) also intersects with levels of problem manifestation identified in customer experience research: interaction level, journey level, and relationship level[18]. Information-access problems can manifest at any of these levels. At the interaction level, a user cannot find a button or field they need. At the journey level, a user cannot find the information they need to progress through a multi-step process. At the relationship level, a customer cannot access the aggregated information about their history with an organization.

Similarly, navigation problems can manifest at all three levels. At the interaction level, a single interface lacks clear navigation patterns. At the journey level, navigation patterns change as users progress through different sections, creating confusion. At the relationship level, users struggle to understand their overall relationship to an organization because the organization's systems do not help users see the larger narrative of their interactions.

Interpretation problems also follow this multi-level pattern. At the interaction level, individual information is difficult to understand due to poor wording or presentation. At the journey level, individual interactions are understandable but do not collectively create a coherent understanding of process or progress. At the relationship level, a user might understand their individual interactions with a company but fail to understand what they collectively mean about their status as a valued customer or their likely future relationship with the company.

Institutional process problems also operate across all three levels, though they are particularly consequential at the journey and relationship levels. At the interaction level, an institutional problem might cause a support person to provide inconsistent information because organizational standards are unclear. At the journey level, institutional problems cause handoffs to break down because ownership is unclear. At the relationship level, institutional problems cause long-term customers to churn because no one in the organization is accountable for the relationship.

This multi-level perspective is important for MVP scoping because it helps teams understand that addressing a problem at one level might not address it at other levels. An MVP might solve an information-access problem at the interaction level (making a button easy to find) without addressing the journey-level manifestation of the same problem (providing the information in the right sequence at the right moment in the user journey). Similarly, an MVP might improve navigation at the interaction level without addressing journey-level navigation problems caused by inconsistent patterns across the user flow.

## Data Silos and Disconnected Systems: A Case Study in Problem Classification

One particularly instructive example of how problems can be classified and addressed is the widespread organizational problem of data silos and disconnected systems[22][42]. On the surface, this appears to be a technical problem: different departments use different systems that do not communicate with each other. However, deeper analysis reveals that the data silo problem typically involves elements of all four problem categories.

At the information-access level, data silos create a problem: information exists in multiple systems but users cannot access it from a single location. A sales representative cannot see what finance knows about a customer. A product team cannot access customer service data about product usage issues. This is fundamentally an information-access problem. Software solutions that create unified data views, integrate disparate systems, or provide cross-system search can substantially reduce this friction.

At the navigation level, data silos create problems when users must navigate across multiple systems to accomplish a single task. An order-to-cash process might require navigating from the sales system to the finance system to the fulfillment system to the accounting system. Each navigation step creates friction and opportunity for errors or miscommunication. Software solutions that integrate systems into a unified workflow or provide clear navigation across systems can address these navigation problems.

At the interpretation level, data silos create problems because the same concept is defined and recorded differently in different systems. One system calls it "customer," another calls it "account," another calls it "client." One system records a transaction one way, another system records it differently. When users encounter this inconsistency, they must interpret what the differences mean and whether records refer to the same entity. This is partly addressable through software (creating common definitions and mappings) and partly not (the organization must agree on common definitions before software can enforce them).

At the institutional level, data silos often reflect deeper organizational problems: unclear process ownership, lack of standards, competing departmental priorities, and inadequate governance. Finance and sales use different systems because no one established standards for data collection and sharing. Customer service data sits separate from product data because no one assigned ownership for customer insights. These are institutional problems that cannot be solved by software. Software integration can provide workarounds (creating bridges between systems), but if the institutional problems are not addressed, new data silos will soon emerge elsewhere.

Successfully addressing data silo problems therefore requires working at all four levels. Software solutions should focus on information access and navigation improvements, which they can directly address. Data standardization and interpretation improvements require institutional work to establish shared definitions. Full resolution of data silos requires addressing institutional problems: establishing governance, assigning ownership, creating standards, and aligning incentives.

An MVP attempting to solve data silo problems should be scoped specifically to the information-access and navigation components: making existing data more accessible through improved architecture and creating pathways that help users navigate across existing systems. The MVP should explicitly exclude the institutional components—data standardization, governance establishment, standards setting—or acknowledge them as out-of-scope constraints that the MVP assumes are being addressed separately.

## Conclusion: Toward Strategic Problem Classification

The classification framework presented in this report—distinguishing between information-access problems, navigation problems, interpretation problems, and institutional process problems—provides a practical tool for organizations attempting to understand what software can meaningfully address and what requires institutional intervention. This framework rests on a fundamental insight supported by research across multiple fields: problems rooted in information organization and user interface design are substantially more software-addressable than problems rooted in organizational structure, culture, and governance.

Information-access problems, characterized by difficulty finding information that exists somewhere in organizational systems, are among the most reliably addressable through software focused on information architecture, labeling, and search functionality. The research evidence consistently supports that well-designed information architecture substantially improves user ability to access information and reduces the friction users experience in finding what they need[20][49]. MVP scope should prioritize these problems.

Navigation problems, characterized by difficulty moving through information or process environments even when destinations are theoretically accessible, are also substantially addressable through software that improves wayfinding, consistency, and clarity of pathways. Research on interface design demonstrates that users exhibit significantly higher confidence and success when navigation is clear and consistent[49]. These problems are appropriate MVP targets.

Interpretation problems, characterized by difficulty understanding information even after accessing and navigating to it, occupy more ambiguous territory. Software can meaningfully improve interpretation through better presentation, reduced cognitive load, and contextual scaffolding[21][44]. However, some interpretation problems require institutional work—establishing shared language, clarifying concepts, and building shared understanding—that software cannot accomplish. MVPs should address the software-side of interpretation problems while acknowledging institutional requirements.

Institutional process problems, characterized by issues embedded in organizational structure, decision-making authority, accountability, and culture, are substantially less addressable through software. While software can enable better processes when institutional foundations are in place, software cannot create those foundations. Organizations attempting to use software to solve institutional process problems typically encounter implementation failures and disappointed stakeholders[46]. MVPs should be scoped to exclude these problems or to separate institutional change work from MVP scope.

The practical implication for product teams and organizations is to conduct systematic problem landscape analysis before defining MVP scope. This analysis should classify problems by type, assess software-tractability, and make explicit decisions about which problems will be addressed in the MVP and which will be addressed through separate institutional initiatives or excluded from initial scope. By aligning problem classification with MVP scope, organizations can focus development effort on problems where software can deliver demonstrable value, validate learning quickly, and iterate based on user feedback. This approach substantially increases the likelihood of MVP success and reduces the risk of failed implementations resulting from misalignment between problem type and solution approach.
