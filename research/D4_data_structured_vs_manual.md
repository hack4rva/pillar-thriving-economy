# Structured Versus Manual Data Extraction for Entrepreneur Decision Trees: Availability, Challenges, and Prototype Design Implications

This comprehensive report examines the landscape of data availability for building entrepreneur decision support systems, distinguishing between information readily accessible through structured data sources and that requiring manual extraction through unstructured channels. A critical analysis reveals that while substantial structured datasets exist for financial metrics, regulatory compliance, and market positioning, significant gaps remain in qualitative assessment areas, competitive intelligence nuance, and real-time operational insights. Organizations designing decision tree prototypes must account for these asymmetries, implementing hybrid approaches that leverage automated data APIs for foundational metrics while establishing systematic manual collection workflows for contextual business intelligence that cannot be industrialized. The interplay between data automation and human intelligence fundamentally shapes both the feasibility and efficacy of decision-making tools for entrepreneurial ventures.

## Understanding the Landscape of Data Availability for Decision Making

Before categorizing which decision inputs exist in structured versus manual form, one must first understand the fundamental distinction between these data paradigms and their implications for decision support systems. **Structured data** refers to information organized in predefined formats with fixed schemas, typically stored in relational databases or standardized file formats where each data element has a consistent definition, location, and relationship to other elements[4]. This includes financial metrics, regulatory classifications, and standardized business metrics. **Unstructured data**, by contrast, comprises information without predefined models or schemas—text from interviews, qualitative observations, narrative business descriptions, and contextual notes that require interpretation and abstraction before analysis[4]. Entrepreneurs building decision support systems must understand that their information sources span this entire spectrum, with critical business intelligence distributed unevenly across structured and unstructured domains.

The relevance of this distinction to entrepreneur decision trees becomes immediately apparent when one considers the core question such systems must address: should we pursue this opportunity, and with what confidence level? Answering this requires input on quantifiable factors (market size, financial requirements, competitive positioning) available through structured sources, alongside subjective assessments (founder capability, team chemistry, organizational culture fit) that typically emerge only through manual investigation. The distribution of these data types fundamentally constrains what can be automated in a decision support prototype versus what must remain anchored in human expertise and judgment.

## Structured Data Elements Readily Available Through APIs and Public Sources

A substantial and expanding ecosystem of data providers makes foundational business intelligence available in structured, machine-readable formats. These resources dramatically reduce the manual work required to populate baseline decision tree inputs. **Company financial data and business formation records** represent one of the richest structured data domains. Multiple commercial APIs provide direct access to business registration information, financial metrics, ownership structures, and historical performance data[2][25][29]. Platforms including Coresignal, CompanyData.com (Bold Data), and Bright Data aggregate company profiles encompassing incorporation details, revenue estimates, employee counts, and legal status—with Coresignal alone providing access to 67 million company profiles continuously refreshed for accuracy[2]. The SEC's EDGAR system offers public API access to comprehensive financial filings for all publicly traded companies, including detailed balance sheets, income statements, and management discussion sections[25]. For private companies, additional APIs pull from business registries, corporate filings, and proprietary databases to populate firmographic profiles that entrepreneurs can query programmatically.

**Industry classification and market segmentation data** is similarly well-structured through public taxonomies. The North American Industry Classification System (NAICS) and Standard Industrial Classification (SIC) systems provide standardized codes that categorize businesses by industry, with federal agencies maintaining and distributing these classifications[26]. This structured taxonomy enables entrepreneurs to immediately place competitive offerings within industry frameworks, access industry-specific data, and compare metrics against standardized peer groups. Bureau of Labor Statistics APIs expose sector-wide employment trends, wage data, and economic indicators in machine-readable formats, allowing decision systems to automatically contextualize individual opportunity assessments against broader economic conditions[12].

**Market research and consumer spending data** has become increasingly accessible through APIs, though with varying coverage and granularity. Syndicated data providers including NielsenIQ, Circana (formerly IRI), and SPINS offer programmatic access to aggregated retail sales data, category performance metrics, and consumer panel insights across different market segments[27]. These datasets reveal category growth rates, market share distributions, and consumer trend patterns without requiring manual market research. Real estate and location-based data APIs provide demographic information, foot traffic analysis, and site-level consumer spending patterns crucial for retail or location-dependent businesses[35]. GIS mapping data and mobility analytics deliver structured information on accessibility, competitive density, and neighborhood characteristics that feed directly into location-selection or market-penetration decisions.

**Regulatory compliance and licensing data** exists in surprisingly structured forms across multiple jurisdictions. Federal and state regulatory bodies maintain searchable databases of business licenses, professional certifications, compliance violations, and regulatory status[10]. FOIA.gov and similar platforms expose government records in standardized formats, while SEC filings include structured XBRL data encoding financial and operational metrics in computer-processable formats[25]. This allows decision systems to automatically verify regulatory compliance status, flag enforcement actions, and assess licensing readiness without manual investigation.

**Personnel and organizational data** increasingly flows through structured channels, though with privacy constraints. Job posting APIs, LinkedIn data (through authorized partnerships), and recruitment platform APIs expose hiring patterns, salary benchmarks, team composition changes, and skill demand signals for specific companies and roles[30]. Press releases and news aggregation APIs provide structured event data identifying personnel changes, funding announcements, and organizational shifts[32]. Employee review platforms expose aggregated compensation data, organizational culture assessments, and retention signals—all available through API in standardized formats.

**Financial performance metrics and valuation data** for both public and venture-backed companies are extensively structured and widely distributed. Stock price data, trading volumes, analyst ratings, and earnings surprise metrics flow through financial data APIs in real-time[40]. For private companies, venture capital databases track funding rounds, valuation trajectories, burn rates (when disclosed), and investor composition through structured records[8]. These platforms enable objective assessment of financial health, growth trajectory, and capital efficiency against comparable companies without manual research.

## Unstructured Data Requiring Manual Extraction and Human Interpretation

Despite the richness of structured data sources, critical decision inputs remain stubbornly unstructured, requiring manual extraction and interpretation. **Qualitative assessment of management capability and organizational culture** represents perhaps the most significant structural gap. While structured data can surface leadership resumes, educational backgrounds, and professional histories, it cannot capture assessment of strategic vision, decision-making patterns under uncertainty, or cultural alignment that determine whether leadership can execute strategy through inevitable obstacles[5]. This requires direct conversation with founders, team members, and external observers—interviews that cannot be systematized into machine-readable formats without losing essential context and nuance.

**Competitive intelligence and strategic positioning** exists partially in structured form (market share, feature matrices, pricing tiers) but primarily in unstructured narratives that require manual synthesis. While competitor websites, product documentation, and customer review platforms are accessible at scale, interpreting strategic intent, identifying evolving threats, and assessing competitive differentiation requires human analysts to read between obvious signals[32]. Competitive positioning relative to specific customer segments, the subtle ways a product deviates from apparent competitors, and the likelihood that competitive advantages persist in shifting market conditions—these insights cannot be extracted algorithmically from structured sources without losing the contextual understanding that makes them actionable.

**Customer feedback and product-market fit assessment** remains heavily dependent on manual extraction from unstructured sources. While structured metrics (net promoter scores, customer acquisition costs, churn rates) are increasingly available, the underlying reasons customers choose or avoid a product—the actual problems being solved, the underserved customer segments, the feature requests that signal growing demand—typically emerge from qualitative customer conversations, support ticket analysis, and community discussion forums[31]. The Sentisum analysis of NPS survey sentiment illustrates this gap: numerical scores without qualitative context fail to explain causation, requiring manual review of customer language to understand why satisfaction or loyalty changed[31].

**Product roadmap credibility and technical execution capability** requires substantial manual investigation. While structured metrics (bug fix velocity, release cadence, feature delivery timeliness) can be quantified, assessing whether product development capacity matches strategic ambitions, whether technical debt is manageable, and whether the organization can sustainably grow engineering operations requires detailed technical assessment that demands conversation with engineers and product leaders. This cannot be extracted from structured sources—it requires direct observation and questioning.

**Business model sustainability and unit economics** often require manual extraction even when underlying transaction data is structured. While a fintech company might expose transaction volumes and average transaction values through APIs, assessing whether the business model can achieve profitability at scale requires understanding the assumptions underlying cost structure, the capital intensity of growth, and the ways unit economics change with scale. These assumptions are embedded in pitch decks, business plans, and financial models that exist as unstructured documents requiring manual interpretation.

**Supplier risk and supply chain resilience** particularly in complex manufacturing or multi-tiered supply chains, resist full automation. While companies like SourceDay and supplier intelligence platforms provide structured supplier data, assessing operational resilience, geographic concentration risks, and the likelihood suppliers can scale requires investigation into supplier financial health beyond what standard credit metrics capture, direct communication about capacity and constraints, and contextual understanding of supply chain shock scenarios[33]. This necessitates manual inquiry and scenario planning exercises that cannot be systematized.

**Customer concentration and revenue diversity** often require manual extraction from contracts and customer agreements rather than structured data. While some of this information may appear in SEC filings for public companies, private companies rarely disclose customer concentration explicitly. Assessing risk requires manual review of contract databases, communication with customer success teams, and direct inquiry—information that exists nowhere in structured form but carries substantial weight for acquisition or investment decisions.

**Organizational change and cultural indicators** beyond what structured talent data reveals demand extensive manual observation. Employee interview feedback, company culture assessment through conversation with staff at multiple levels, and assessment of whether stated values align with actual decision-making all require on-site observation and extensive interviewing[22]. These qualitative culture assessments heavily influence whether teams can navigate pivots, handle failure constructively, or execute through phases of rapid change—all crucial for early-stage entrepreneurial ventures.

## The Architecture of Data APIs and Structured Data Access for Decision Support

Understanding the mechanics of structured data access is essential for designing decision tree prototypes that leverage available information efficiently. Modern data infrastructure increasingly relies on **API-first architecture** enabling real-time data integration rather than periodic data refreshes[6][11][12][20][40]. APIs allow decision support systems to query live data sources at the moment decisions are being made, ensuring that assessments reflect current conditions rather than stale historical snapshots. The SEC's EDGAR API, for example, enables real-time access to the moment companies file updated financial information, allowing decision systems to flag material changes immediately upon disclosure[25].

**Data normalization and format standardization** across multiple sources presents both opportunity and challenge. Specialized data providers invest substantially in harmonizing data from disparate sources into consistent formats—Cognism's five-layer data validation approach, for example, standardizes company profiles from hundreds of sources into unified schemas[2]. This reduces the data engineering burden on decision system builders but introduces dependency on third-party definitions of data quality and completeness. Different providers normalize the same underlying reality differently, requiring decision systems to either standardize around a single provider or implement reconciliation logic for conflicting values across sources.

**Real-time versus historical data tradeoffs** fundamentally shape what structured insights can inform entrepreneurial decisions. Real-time data feeds reveal immediate market movements, funding announcements, and competitive actions[40]. Historical data enables trend analysis, pattern identification, and scenario testing against past outcomes. Effective decision systems integrate both—using historical patterns to establish base rates and expectations, while monitoring real-time streams for signals indicating deviation from expected trajectories. The choice between real-time and historical emphasis depends on decision urgency and the pace of change in the relevant domain.

**Data freshness and update frequency** constraints must be explicitly modeled in decision systems. Some data (stock prices, regulatory filings) updates daily or intraday; other data (employment levels, demographic composition) updates quarterly or annually; still other data (business culture assessments) never achieves true "freshness" through structured sources[47]. Decision systems must know the update frequency for each input, flag inputs approaching obsolescence, and adjust confidence appropriately based on data recency.

**Compliance and access restrictions** shape which structured data can actually be leveraged. While data exists, access may be restricted to authorized users—financial professionals for certain financial data, law enforcement for certain public records, or verified researchers for academic datasets[10][25]. Privacy regulations including GDPR and CCPA limit what personal information can be incorporated into decision systems[49]. Intellectual property protections restrict publication of certain competitive analysis. Decision system builders must map which data layers are accessible within their constraints and design systems accordingly.

## Manual Data Extraction Processes and Bottlenecks

Where structured data sources prove insufficient, systematic processes for manual extraction become necessary. The human-intensive nature of extracting unstructured intelligence creates both significant bottlenecks and quality variability that fundamentally constrains what manual collection approaches can accomplish at scale. **Web scraping and document extraction** represents one avenue for automating unstructured data collection, leveraging optical character recognition, natural language processing, and machine learning models trained to extract relevant information from documents[16][21]. Tools like Parsio, Zapier, and specialized IDP (Intelligent Document Processing) platforms can extract structured data from invoices, contracts, and other documents at scale, reducing manual typing[16].

However, this automation has meaningful limits. Web scraping competitors' websites captures obvious information (pricing, feature lists, customer logos) but misses strategic nuance—the ways products differentiate subtly, the customer segments becoming primary focus, or the feature gaps that present opportunity[32]. Natural language processing tools can extract topics, sentiment, and entities from text, but miss contextual understanding about what customer complaints actually signal about product-market fit[39]. Optical character recognition works reliably for structured documents (tax returns, invoices) but struggles with handwritten notes, unusual formatting, and document images of poor quality[21].

**Interview-based data collection** for qualitative assessment requires extensive human time investment but remains the gold standard for gathering contextual business intelligence. Structured interview guides combining quantitative rating scales with open-ended follow-up questions enable consistent data collection while preserving conversational depth[22]. However, each interview requires 30 minutes to several hours, transcription requires additional time, and analysis of qualitative feedback requires skilled interpretation. At scale, this becomes prohibitively expensive for comprehensive opportunity assessment, limiting structured interview approaches to high-stakes decisions or critical assessment areas.

**Reference checks and expert consultation** represent another manual extraction channel where decision-makers reach out to founders' previous colleagues, industry experts, and potential customers. These conversations generate rich contextual intelligence about capability, credibility, and market reception that cannot be captured through any automated process. However, reaching appropriate reference sources, conducting interviews, and synthesizing feedback into decision-relevant insights requires substantial human effort and introduces potential bias based on which references are willing to speak and how referents frame their assessment.

**Cost implications of manual extraction** directly constrain its scale. A study of HR processes found that manual data entry costs approximately $4.86 per record, with complex data gathering tasks reaching $23.27 per entry when performed manually[50]. For comprehensive entrepreneur assessment requiring multiple information sources, interviews with several team members, and customer reference checks, the total labor cost easily exceeds hundreds of dollars per opportunity assessed. Organizations pursuing more than a handful of opportunities annually will find this unsustainable without automation.

**Data quality and consistency issues** in manual extraction present additional constraints. Different evaluators interviewing the same founder reach different conclusions based on question sequencing, follow-up depth, and interpretation of responses[22]. Manual transcription of interview data introduces errors; manual coding of themes introduces inconsistency. Without systematic processes, protocol documentation, and evaluator training, manual data collection generates datasets too inconsistent for reliable decision-making.

## Data Integration Challenges and Quality Management

The complexity of combining structured and unstructured data sources into coherent decision frameworks cannot be overstated. Organizations attempting to build unified decision support systems face substantial **data quality and consistency challenges** that complicate analysis[37][38]. Company data from different providers shows conflicting information about basic attributes—revenue figures, employee counts, founding dates vary across databases reflecting different data collection methodologies, update frequencies, and source prioritization. Deciding which source of truth takes precedence, when to flag conflicts for manual review, and how to reconcile inconsistencies requires explicit governance policies.

**Data schema heterogeneity** creates engineering complexity beyond surface-level API consumption[37]. Different APIs return company data in different formats: one API uses ISO date formatting, another uses strings; one calculates revenue based on filed tax returns, another estimates from web scraping; one includes international locations as separate entities, another aggregates geographically. Integrating these into a unified decision system requires substantial data engineering investment in normalization, mapping, and transformation—and introduces the risk that transformation rules themselves bias decision inputs by prioritizing certain data sources or interpretations.

**Missing data and incomplete coverage** emerge as serious challenges at every integration layer. Company databases achieve excellent coverage of large, established firms in developed markets but sparse coverage of early-stage companies, international ventures, and private businesses outside traditional industries[2][29]. Even extensive databases lack coverage of very recently formed companies. Customer data is accessible through reviews but excludes dissatisfied customers who never posted feedback; employee data is available for companies actively hiring but not for stable teams; financial data is comprehensive for public companies but fragmentary for private ventures.

**Data freshness and consistency across multiple sources** requires continuous monitoring[41]. A company updates employment levels on LinkedIn weeks before updating formal filings; funding announcements appear in venture databases before SEC disclosure; personnel changes surface in LinkedIn before official announcements. Building decision systems that reliably reflect current reality despite this temporal inconsistency requires sophisticated data pipelines with monitoring, validation rules, and confidence scoring based on data recency.

## Prototype Design Implications and Hybrid Approach Architecture

The distribution of structured versus unstructured data requirements fundamentally shapes how entrepreneur decision tree prototypes should be architected. **Hybrid design approaches** that leverage structured data for foundational metrics while maintaining systematic processes for manual assessment of qualitative factors offer the most practical path forward. Such systems should be organized in tiers reflecting data availability and assessment automation feasibility.

The **foundational tier** comprises metrics accessible through structured APIs with minimal human intervention. Company registration status, basic financial metrics (from public filings), and industry classification can be populated automatically with high confidence. These provide decision-relevant context without manual work. The entrepreneur decision system should surface these automatically as baseline information, using automated data quality checks to flag inconsistencies across sources and alert decision-makers when data appears outdated or conflicting.

The **intermediate tier** comprises metrics requiring moderate manual effort or combining structured and unstructured data. Market size estimates might combine structured industry forecasts (available through APIs) with manual research into addressable market segments within that industry. Competitive positioning combines structured competitor data (features, pricing, hiring patterns from APIs) with manual assessment synthesized from customer conversations and industry expert interviews. This tier typically involves 2-4 hours of focused research per opportunity, producing assessments with moderate confidence subject to updating as new information emerges.

The **qualitative assessment tier** requires systematic but fundamentally manual processes. Founder capability assessment, organizational culture evaluation, and team dynamics assessment require direct conversation and observation. Effective prototypes should structure this through interview guides and assessment rubrics that create consistency across multiple evaluators assessing different opportunities. Digital tools can organize this data collection—recording interviews, managing transcription, facilitating team discussion of assessment conclusions—but cannot eliminate the human work of actually having conversations and forming judgments.

Prototype design must also account for **decision urgency and required confidence levels**. When evaluating dozens of opportunities for initial screening before deeper assessment, relying primarily on automated structured data with high-level categorization (likely go/no-go based on obvious criteria) makes practical sense. When making high-stakes decisions with substantial capital investment, the system should mandate deeper investigation including manual assessment of qualitative factors before final decision. Design should support tiered assessment intensity, allowing rapid filtering based on structured data while enabling deeper investigation when opportunities advance to serious consideration.

**Visualization of structured versus unstructured components** within decision tree outputs helps decision-makers understand confidence levels and information gaps. Decision trees should visually distinguish between metrics populated automatically from APIs (which reflect current, objective data but may have limited coverage or granularity) and metrics based on manual assessment (which may be richer contextually but subject to evaluator variation and requiring updating as circumstances change). This transparency helps decision-makers weight different inputs appropriately, acknowledging that an automatically populated metric based on six-month-old financial data may be less reliable than a manually assessed metric based on recent founder conversation.

## Critical Data Gaps and Areas Requiring Systematic Manual Assessment

Even comprehensive data integration leaves significant assessment areas that fundamentally require manual investigation. **Founder-specific capability assessment** cannot be reliably automated. Structured data reveals educational background, prior company experience, and LinkedIn network density, but cannot assess founder capability to navigate challenges specific to the opportunity at hand. A founder who succeeded building a B2B marketplace might struggle with complex regulatory requirements in financial services; a founder who successfully executed a disciplined fundraising process might lack the creativity required for product innovation in ambiguous markets. These situation-specific capability assessments require conversation and judgment.

**Strategic fit with organizational capabilities** similarly cannot be fully automated. While structured data reveals what technologies, expertise, and resources an organization currently possesses, assessing whether these genuinely position the organization favorably for a specific opportunity requires understanding not just current capability but rate of capability development, existing team capacity, and ability to prioritize new initiatives alongside existing commitments. A company might have data science expertise that appears relevant, but assessment of whether engineers are available and willing to pivot to the proposed project requires conversation with engineering leadership.

**Customer discovery and unmet need validation** remains largely manual despite advances in automated customer research. While review platforms reveal what customers say about existing products and what features they request, validating whether identified problems are large enough to merit new ventures, whether proposed solutions actually address customer pain, and whether customers would pay for proposed solutions requires direct customer conversation[5][45]. No automated system has replaced the founder conversations with potential customers that reveal whether customers care about proposed offerings.

**Organizational change readiness and cultural alignment** necessitate on-site observation and extended conversation. While structured organizational data reveals size, growth rates, and funding history, assessing whether organizational culture genuinely supports agile decision-making or whether stated values represent aspirational rather than actual practice requires extended interaction with multiple organizational members at different levels[48]. This cultural assessment heavily influences whether organizations can successfully navigate startup pivots, execute acquisitions, or achieve rapid scaling—all common in entrepreneurial ventures—but resists automation.

## Recommendations for Decision Tree Prototype Implementation

Effective prototypes should adopt specific design patterns to optimize the structured-unstructured data balance. **API-first data layer design** should establish automated connections to the full range of available business data sources, with decision systems querying this layer to populate foundational metrics. Cognism's five-layer validation approach and similar data quality frameworks should be adopted, establishing confidence scoring for each automated metric reflecting source reliability and data recency. When conflicting data appears across sources, the system should flag this explicitly for decision-maker attention rather than automatically resolving through hidden rules.

**Structured assessment protocols** for manual data collection should standardize the process of gathering qualitative information, establishing consistency without eliminating human judgment. Interview guides should combine fixed-format questions enabling comparison across multiple founder assessments with open-ended probes allowing contextual exploration. Assessment rubrics should define what constitutes different confidence levels in founder capability assessment rather than requiring unstructured narrative evaluation. Digital tools should support this through mobile-friendly interview administration, audio recording and transcription, and qualitative analysis tools enabling identification of themes across multiple interviews.

**Decision staging and confidence modeling** should acknowledge that different opportunity types require different assessment depths. Early-stage opportunities where primary risk centers on founder capability might prioritize manual founder assessment while accepting more limited structured business validation. Late-stage opportunities where founder track record is established but execution risk dominates might emphasize structured operational assessment over founder reassessment. Prototypes should explicitly ask "what question must be answered before we can decide?" and route assessment effort to that critical question rather than applying uniform rigor to all opportunities.

**Continuous data refresh and update protocols** should institutionalize updating both structured and unstructured data as situations evolve. Automated metrics should be queried on schedules matching their underlying data freshness (daily for stock price-derived metrics, quarterly for financial statement metrics, manually when founder-specific assessment requires reassessment). Document version control, dated assessment entries, and clear delineation of when assessments were last refreshed enable decision-makers to understand information currency.

**Bias identification and mitigation** in manual assessment processes should acknowledge that unstructured data introduces evaluation inconsistency. Using multiple evaluators for high-stakes assessments, comparing their independent conclusions, and investigating divergence surfaces evaluation bias. Reference check protocols should systematize which references are consulted and how their feedback is weighted, preventing selective bias toward favorable or unfavorable viewpoints. Founder demographic data (age, gender, ethnicity, educational background) should be explicitly tracked to identify whether certain populations receive more favorable assessment with identical credentials, enabling correction through awareness.

## Conclusion: Balancing Automation with Human Expertise in Entrepreneurial Decision-Making

The landscape of data availability for entrepreneur decision trees reveals a mixed reality where substantial structured data sources eliminate manual work for foundational business metrics while critical qualitative assessments remain stubbornly dependent on human investigation and judgment. Effective prototypes will not attempt to fully automate decision-making or to eliminate the human expertise that founders, experienced operators, and business analysts bring to opportunity assessment. Rather, they will orchestrate the integration of what can be automated (market size estimates, competitor data, regulatory compliance verification) with systematized processes for gathering and synthesizing the qualitative information that determines whether founders and organizations can execute strategy.

This hybrid approach acknowledges fundamental constraints: while APIs can provide near real-time information about thousands of companies' basic attributes, no algorithm can assess whether a specific founder has the creativity required to identify differentiated customer needs or the resilience required to navigate an inevitable pivot. While text analysis can extract topics from customer reviews, human judgment remains necessary to assess whether identified pain points represent material problems worth solving. The optimal decision support system will make this division of labor explicit, automating what automation can achieve reliably while protecting the manual assessment of qualitative factors from inappropriate attempts at algorithmic replacement.

Organizations implementing entrepreneur decision systems should invest in both dimensions: sophisticated data engineering to integrate the growing ecosystem of structured business data sources, and systematic protocols for manual assessment that create consistency and enable scaling of human judgment across multiple opportunities. The combination of real-time structured data feeds revealing basic business metrics and systematized manual processes capturing qualitative capability assessment represents the achievable frontier of decision support technology for entrepreneurial ventures. Moving beyond this requires not advances in data technology alone, but fundamental improvements in how organizations systematically gather and synthesize human judgment—a challenge extending far beyond the technical scope of data integration and into organizational design and process discipline.
