# Richmond's Minority Business Contracting Reality: An Investigation of Friction Points for First-Time and Small MBE Participants

This investigation examines the actual landscape of minority-business contracting challenges facing entrepreneurs in Richmond, Virginia, synthesizing publicly available procurement data, disparity study findings, compliance documentation, and documented barriers from comparable jurisdictions. The analysis reveals that Richmond's MBE contracting challenges exist not as a single discrete problem but rather as a cascade of friction points occurring at distinct stages of the procurement pipeline, with significant underutilization documented through recent disparity studies, complex administrative requirements that disproportionately burden smaller enterprises, limited visibility of procurement opportunities within minority business networks, and substantial barriers to contract readiness that extend beyond simple access to information or capital.

## Factual Findings: Verifiable Data on Richmond's MBE Contracting Performance

### Documented Underutilization of Minority-Owned Businesses

The City of Richmond commissioned a comprehensive disparity study completed in 2025 by MGT Consulting, which provides the most current quantitative assessment of the city's minority business contracting landscape.[2][21][2] Between fiscal years 2017 and 2021, the study analyzed five years of city procurement data and found that minority-owned businesses received only 3.43% of total contracting dollars at both prime and subcontracting levels, despite representing approximately 17.93% of firms available in the city's relevant 14-county market.[46] This represents a significant disparity between utilization and availability, with the disparity ratio indicating that MBEs received roughly one-fifth of the contract dollars that would be expected based on their representation in the marketplace.

When disaggregated by racial and ethnic group, the disparity becomes more nuanced and severe for certain communities.[46] Hispanic-owned firms accounted for approximately 1.77% of total dollars spent during the study period, while African American-owned firms received about 1.43% of total dollars. These figures are particularly significant given that the disparity study estimated the availability of African American-owned firms at 7.7% and Hispanic-owned firms at 4.7% in Richmond's relevant market, meaning Black-owned businesses received less than one-fifth of expected contract dollars and Hispanic-owned firms received less than one-third of expected dollars.[39]

The economic impact of this underutilization extends to employment and community wealth building. The disparity study quantified economic contributions from MBE contracting during the 2017-2021 period at approximately 476 jobs tied to MBE contracts, roughly $33 million in labor income, about $49 million in value added (GDP), and over $9 million in local, state and federal taxes paid.[46] This suggests that both expanded MBE participation and current underutilization have meaningful consequences for economic development in Richmond.

### Institutional Framework: Richmond's MBE Program Structure

Richmond's minority business contracting infrastructure operates through the Office of Minority Business Development (OMBD), located at 1500 East Main Street, 5th Floor, Richmond, VA 23219, and can be reached at (804) 646-5947.[1][9][15] The office administers three primary certification categories recognized on city contracts: Minority Business Enterprises (MBEs), defined as businesses at least 51% owned and controlled by minority group members including African American, Hispanic American, Asian American, and American Indian entrepreneurs; Emerging Small Businesses (ESBs), which are non-minority-owned small businesses that have been certified by OMBD for seven years or less and meet specific revenue thresholds; and Disadvantaged Business Enterprises (DBEs) certified through federal programs.[12][12]

The city uses an electronic procurement platform called OpenGov for solicitation development, bid submission, and contract award management, with all solicitations located at procurement.opengov.com/portal/rva.[8][8] This represents a shift toward digitalized procurement intended to increase transparency and accessibility, though the platform requires electronic submission and navigation through multiple steps. The city has established overall goals for MBE/ESB participation on specific projects, with documented examples including a 13% DBE goal for the Main Street Station Renovation Project during fiscal years 2022-2023.[9]

### Existing Technical Assistance and Support Programs

OMBD provides technical assistance services offered free of charge to minority and emerging small businesses, including business technical assistance, business start-up and growth development, one-on-one business counseling, business assessments, business development workshops, registration and certification assistance, and financial options counseling and referrals.[15] The office conducts monthly Business Plan Workshops, offers downloadable templates for business plans, start-up cost worksheets, and cash flow analysis, and maintains listings of small business organizations and resources including connections to SCORE mentoring services, the U.S. Small Business Administration, and state-level agencies.

The city also operates through the Virginia Department of Small Business and Supplier Diversity (SBSD), which administers the statewide SWaM (Small, Women-owned, and Minority-owned Business) certification program.[7][31] Certification processing timelines for SWaM applications are approximately 60 business days from receipt of a completed application with required supporting documents. The Commonwealth of Virginia's 2025 disparity study found that minority-owned businesses received a disparity index of $0.46 for all state agency contracts during the study period, with Black-owned businesses experiencing particularly severe disparities at $0.16 and Hispanic-owned businesses at $0.24.[39]

### Procurement Opportunity Identification and Access

Richmond's Department of Procurement Services publishes current business opportunities on RVA.gov and maintains a list of projected business opportunities with timelines accessible to potential vendors.[8][8] The city provides notifications of upcoming opportunities through the OpenGov platform, and vendors can register to receive solicitation notifications. However, the reliance on electronic systems and registration means that businesses without consistent internet access, digital literacy, or awareness of the platform's existence may systematically miss opportunities.

The Virginia Department of Small Business and Supplier Diversity maintains a searchable SWaM and DBE Directory allowing businesses to filter by certification type, NIGP code, NAICS code, city, and zip code.[5] This directory is maintained within Virginia's secure information technology infrastructure, but requires navigation of a specialized database and understanding of classification systems that may not be immediately apparent to first-time users.

### Compliance and Participation Requirements

Participation on city contracts requires submission of the MBE/ESB Participation Form (MBE-2), which documents commitment to minority business participation, identifies specific subcontractors or suppliers, specifies their scope of work, dollar amounts, and percentage of contract value.[12][12] The form requires prime contractors to verify that MBE/ESB subcontractors will perform commercially useful functions and not simply act as pass-through entities. Registered MBE firms that are not yet certified have 180 days from contract award to obtain certification before the prime contractor risks losing credit toward the minority participation goal.

The city has established specific requirements for MBE/ESB eligibility. For Emerging Small Business (ESB) certification, businesses must have annual gross receipts of $500,000 or less for construction work or $250,000 or less for non-construction business, fewer than ten employees, principal place of business within the City of Richmond Enterprise Zone, a city business license, and must pay personal property, real estate, and business taxes to Richmond.[12][12] For MBE certification, the primary requirement is 51% ownership and control by minority group members, though the city may impose revenue limitations as well.

## Inferences: Synthesized Analysis of Systemic Challenges

### Inference 1: Information Access and Network Closure

**Clearly Labeled as Inference:** Research on procurement barriers in comparable cities and national studies on minority business contracting suggests that information access functions as a significant hidden barrier despite the existence of public databases and electronic platforms. The disparity between MBE availability (17.93% in Richmond's market) and utilization (3.43% of contract dollars) cannot be fully explained by lack of qualified firms, suggesting instead that systemic barriers exist beyond simple technical incompetence or business viability.

National research conducted by researchers at major universities examining women and minority business enterprises found that approximately one-third of qualified W/MBEs surveyed had difficulty obtaining prime contract opportunities despite proactive efforts to find business, with lack of access to information and "closed networks" frequently cited as barriers.[30][30] In Philadelphia's implementation of more equitable procurement practices, experts noted that opportunities had been "worded in a way, structured in a way that is impossible for either any minority, or woman-owned business to even have a chance," suggesting that process design itself creates barriers beyond simple information asymmetry.[42]

Applied to Richmond's context, this suggests that while OMBD provides educational resources and the city publishes solicitations on OpenGov, there may be systematic differences in when and how information reaches different business networks, potentially related to factors such as whether businesses are connected to established contractor networks, chambers of commerce, or informal referral systems. The fact that 90% of interviewed MBEs in comparable studies indicated desire for more contract opportunities but faced barriers to accessing them suggests that motivation and interest are not the limiting factors.[30]

### Inference 2: Procurement Process Complexity as a Tiered Barrier

**Clearly Labeled as Inference:** The complexity of government procurement processes creates disproportionate barriers for smaller and less-resourced businesses. While the Federal Acquisition Regulation (FAR) and similar compliance frameworks apply uniformly, research on procurement barriers from the Chicagoland Chamber of Commerce and other sources documents that small businesses experience these requirements differently than larger firms.[16][16]

A 2022 JPMorgan Chase analysis found that the cost of becoming "contract-ready" for large corporate supply chains can range from $50,000 to $500,000 for firms to implement cybersecurity planning assistance alone.[16][16] While Richmond's city contracting may not impose CMMC or equivalent cybersecurity requirements on all vendors, the broader principle applies: the up-front investment required to meet compliance, bonding, insurance, and administrative requirements falls disproportionately on smaller firms as a percentage of their revenue and available capital.

For Richmond specifically, the requirement to submit electronically through OpenGov, prepare detailed MBE/ESB participation forms with multiple subcontractors, meet bonding and insurance requirements, and maintain compliance documentation creates a procedural burden that a business with limited administrative staff experiences more acutely than a larger firm with dedicated procurement and compliance personnel. Research on Madison's procurement barriers found that MBE/WBEs rated prequalification and workforce requirements as significantly more burdensome than non-minority firms did, with "red tape" cited as a factor that causes some contractors to avoid public sector work entirely.[35]

### Inference 3: Capital Access as a Structural Constraint

**Clearly Labeled as Inference:** While Richmond's OMBD provides free technical assistance, the underlying capital constraints facing minority-owned businesses represent a structural barrier largely beyond the scope of technical assistance programming. Research on racial wealth disparities and business formation documents that minority entrepreneurs have systematically less access to personal wealth, family capital, and generational assets that could be deployed as working capital or bonding requirements.[34][38]

The disparity study findings that MBEs generated lower revenues and self-employment earnings than comparable non-MBE firms in Richmond's market suggests this is not merely a temporary start-up phase phenomenon but reflects sustained economic disadvantage.[46] This constrains ability to invest in contract readiness, maintain operations during the slow-payment cycles common in government contracting, or absorb losses if a contract performs poorly.

National research on government contracting for small businesses documents that slow payment from government agencies—sometimes taking six months or longer—significantly stresses minority contractors who lack cash reserves or access to credit lines.[30][42] One minority contractor interviewed in national research noted that "slow pay" and "lack of working capital" were among the primary reasons qualified MBEs did not bid on prime contracts. This suggests that even if Richmond provides information about opportunities and technical assistance with proposal preparation, the underlying financial structure of government contracting creates a barrier that only capital access can solve.

### Inference 4: Discrimination and Marketplace Barriers

**Clearly Labeled as Inference:** The disparity study findings, when combined with qualitative interview data, suggest that both discrimination and marketplace segregation contribute to the underutilization of minority businesses in Richmond contracting. The study explicitly found that "Black-owned firms reported the highest rates of direct discrimination in the marketplace," indicating that discrimination is not merely historical but ongoing and contemporary.[46]

National research on minority business disparities documents multiple forms of ongoing discrimination in government contracting, including bid shopping (where majority prime contractors disclose minority firms' subcontracting bids to competitors), failure to break large contracts into smaller chunks that minority firms can compete for, and extensive granting of waivers from minority participation requirements.[34] Research on Philadelphia's contracting found that while minority hiring goals existed on paper, implementation was inconsistent, and three vendors were disbarred from the city for violating minority contracting regulations, suggesting that enforcement and genuine commitment matter significantly.[42]

For Richmond, the low utilization of minority businesses despite significant availability and demonstrated interest suggests that marketplace barriers extend beyond lack of information or skills. The disparity study's finding that the city "substantially underutilized" minority firms during a period when OMBD existed and provided services suggests that programming alone has been insufficient to overcome structural barriers.

### Inference 5: Certification and Eligibility Assessment as Hidden Complexity

**Clearly Labeled as Inference:** The process of determining and obtaining appropriate certification represents a complexity barrier that is often invisible until a business attempts to pursue it. While OMBD provides certification assistance, the existence of multiple certification types (MBE, ESB, DBE, 8(a), SWaM, etc.) and different eligibility criteria for each creates decision points where first-time entrepreneurs may make suboptimal choices.

A business must determine whether it qualifies as an Emerging Small Business (requiring location in Enterprise Zone, specific revenue thresholds, fewer than ten employees) or can pursue MBE certification (requiring 51% minority ownership and control), and additionally must understand federal 8(a) certification and SWaM designations. The MBE-2 form itself contains multiple participation codes (first-tier, second-tier, third-tier subcontracting; supply purchases; joint ventures; mentor-protégé arrangements; other credibly viable methods) with different credit values, suggesting that strategy matters not just for winning contracts but for getting appropriate credit when you do participate.

Madison's disparity study found that "many interviewees commented that the SBE certification process is time consuming or difficult" and that "some prime contractors said that they have small businesses as subcontractors that are unwilling to seek certification because of the paperwork involved."[35] This suggests that certification itself, while theoretically free or low-cost, carries hidden costs in time and complexity that disproportionately affect businesses without dedicated administrative staff.

## Unknowns: What Cannot Be Verified from Public Sources

### Unknown 1: Actual Participant Experience with OpenGov Platform

While Richmond publishes solicitations on the OpenGov platform and claims it is "intuitive and transparent," public sources do not provide data on user experience metrics such as completion rates for first-time users, success rates for businesses attempting to submit bids electronically, or whether technical barriers (slow internet, poor digital literacy, system downtime) prevent some vendors from successfully submitting. The system requires JavaScript to function, which could exclude users with older browsers or devices, but no data quantifies how many Richmond vendors face this constraint.

### Unknown 2: Informal Network Effects in Procurement Decision-Making

Public procurement documents do not reveal the informal processes through which procurement officers learn about or prioritize engagement with specific vendors. While the written rules require that all opportunities be published and all proposals receive equal consideration, national research documents that relationships and prior dealings often influence decision-making.[16] No public data is available on whether minority-owned businesses have systematically less prior contact with Richmond procurement officers, less familiarity with the informal protocols for question submission and clarification, or reduced access to decision-makers through informal channels.

### Unknown 3: Detailed Compliance and Performance Data by Business Demographics

While the disparity study examined overall utilization, Richmond's publicly available procurement data does not appear to systematically track performance metrics such as contract completion rates, on-time delivery rates, cost overruns, or customer satisfaction by business demographics. This creates an information gap: if minority-owned businesses have higher failure or performance issue rates, that would be relevant context; if they perform at equivalent levels to majority-owned businesses, that would support arguments for greater participation.

### Unknown 4: Characteristics of Businesses Not Attempting to Participate

The disparity study interviewed 20 certified MBEs and documented their experiences, but does not appear to systematically investigate businesses that could potentially qualify for certification but have not registered with OMBD or pursued city contracts. This creates an uncertainty about whether the primary barrier is getting registered businesses to bid, or getting qualified businesses to register in the first place.

### Unknown 5: Specific Waiver and Good Faith Efforts Patterns

Public sources do not provide detailed data on the frequency with which prime contractors submit waivers claiming inability to meet MBE goals, or the basis on which such waivers are approved or denied. The MBE-2 form includes provision for "Good Faith Efforts Form" documentation, but no publicly available analysis shows whether waivers are being used appropriately or whether they function as a loophole to avoid meaningful MBE participation.[12]

### Unknown 6: Long-Term Outcomes for MBE Contractors

Richmond's public sources do not systematically track whether minority-owned businesses that successfully complete contracts with the city go on to grow, remain in business, or develop capacity to bid on larger opportunities. National research suggests that businesses receiving government contracts show improved survival rates, but Richmond-specific data on this is not publicly available.

## Assessment of Primary Friction Points: Where the Process Actually Breaks Down

### Friction Point 1: Opportunity Discovery and Visibility

**Primary Challenge:** Despite OpenGov's existence and OMBD's provision of free registration and notifications, evidence from the disparity study and national research suggests that discovering opportunities remains a significant barrier for first-time and small MBE participants. The problem manifests not as opportunities being hidden, but as differential visibility of opportunities across business networks.

**Manifestations:**

The mechanism through which businesses learn about opportunities appears to follow network patterns. Large contractors with established relationships with procurement officers likely receive informal notice of upcoming opportunities; businesses connected to chambers of commerce or contractor associations may receive circulation through institutional channels; but businesses attempting to participate for the first time or those working in isolation may discover opportunities only through reactive checking of the OpenGov portal. National research found that approximately one-quarter of MBE/WBEs reported difficulty learning about bid opportunities, a substantially higher rate than majority-owned firms.[35]

Additionally, the lag between when an opportunity is published and when a qualified MBE can realistically respond creates practical barriers. Government procurement processes typically require competitive bidding with relatively short response windows (often 2-3 weeks), technical specification development, subcontractor and supplier identification, and insurance and bonding arrangement. For a business performing this for the first time, this timeline may be insufficient.

**Estimated Scope:** The disparity study's finding that 90% of interviewed MBEs expressed desire for more contract opportunities, yet received only 3.43% of contract dollars while representing 17.93% of available businesses, suggests that opportunity awareness is not the sole problem—many businesses know opportunities exist and want them—but access to the right opportunities at the right time may be constrained.

### Friction Point 2: Procurement Language and Technical Specification Complexity

**Primary Challenge:** Government procurement documents, solicitation requirements, and compliance documentation employ specialized vocabulary and technical specifications that create barriers for businesses without prior experience in government contracting. The problem is not merely that the language is technical, but that failure to understand specific elements can result in bid rejection or non-compliance with contract requirements.

**Manifestations:**

Procurement solicitations require specification of NAICS codes (North American Industry Classification System), which businesses must identify correctly to determine eligibility and classification.[6] The MBE-2 form requires specification of participation type using codes (A, B, C, D, E) with specific meanings, requires identification of "commercially useful function" performed by subcontractors, and requires verification that subcontractors are not simply pass-through entities.[12] A business unfamiliar with this language might incorrectly complete the form, resulting in loss of MBE credit or contract rejection.

Additionally, solicitation documents contain specific compliance requirements, performance metrics, insurance requirements, and terms and conditions that must be understood before a business can realistically assess whether it can fulfill the contract. National research on procurement barriers found that 17% of MBEs cited "lack of technical knowledge to submit bids" as a barrier.[30] While OMBD provides workshops, these cannot cover all possible solicitation types or technical requirements across all procurement categories.

**Estimated Scope:** The problem appears to extend beyond just language. Research on Madison found that MBE/WBEs were substantially more likely than majority-owned firms to report that prequalification requirements and workforce requirements presented barriers.[35] This suggests that the requirements themselves, not just the language in which they're communicated, create differential impacts.

### Friction Point 3: Bonding, Insurance, and Financial Readiness Requirements

**Primary Challenge:** Government contracting typically requires performance bonds, bid bonds, and insurance coverage (general liability, workers' compensation, etc.) that smaller and resource-constrained businesses find difficult to obtain and afford. These requirements function as gatekeeping mechanisms that exclude businesses before they can even submit a bid.

**Manifestations:**

A business attempting to bid on a construction or goods contract may need to provide performance bonds guaranteeing contract completion, and the cost of bonding increases as a percentage of contract value for smaller contractors. Insurance premiums similarly represent fixed costs that strain smaller operations. National research documented that access to bonding and insurance is a significant barrier for MBEs, with some contractors unable to bid despite competitive pricing because they cannot secure required bonds.[35][30]

For Richmond specifically, while the city's published procurement documents reference bonding and insurance requirements, they do not appear to systematically offer bonding assistance or insurance waivers for small businesses, creating a structural barrier for businesses lacking established relationships with surety companies or capital reserves to prepay bonding costs.

**Estimated Scope:** The disparity study does not appear to quantify this barrier for Richmond specifically, but national research and other city studies consistently identify it as a primary reason qualified MBEs do not bid on contracts.

### Friction Point 4: Slow Payment Cycles and Cash Flow Stress

**Primary Challenge:** Government contracts, particularly for construction and complex services, often have extended payment cycles (30, 60, or 90 days after invoice submission plus administrative processing time) that create severe cash flow stress for small businesses. Prime contractors are often paid by the government only after submitting detailed documentation, and subcontractors may wait even longer.

**Manifestations:**

A minority-owned construction or services business that accepts a government contract may need to immediately hire workers, purchase materials, and maintain operations while waiting for payment. If the business lacks sufficient working capital or credit lines to bridge this gap, it cannot realistically accept the contract. National research found that 16% of MBEs cited "were not paid from previous contracts" as a reason for not bidding on new government work, and "lack of working capital" and "slow pay" were cited as barriers.[30] The Research Institute examining government contracting found that payment delays are "now rippling through companies that don't get upfront payments and rely on predictable cash flow."[23]

For minority-owned businesses with systematically lower access to capital and credit, this barrier is particularly acute. A large contractor with established credit lines or significant cash reserves can bridge payment delays; a small contractor with limited resources cannot.

**Estimated Scope:** This barrier affects businesses that would otherwise be qualified and capable, eliminating them from consideration despite their competence. It represents a structural barrier inherent to government contracting processes rather than a barrier specific to Richmond's implementation.

### Friction Point 5: Discrimination and Marketplace Barriers in Contract Award and Performance

**Primary Challenge:** Beyond the procedural barriers to bidding, evidence suggests that discrimination and historical exclusion from contractor networks create barriers to actually winning contracts and to successful performance once contracts are awarded. This includes bid shopping, historically established relationships between procurement officers and contractors, and discriminatory practices by prime contractors managing subcontractors.

**Manifestations:**

The disparity study explicitly found that "Black-owned firms reported the highest rates of direct discrimination in the marketplace," indicating that contemporary discrimination remains a barrier in Richmond's contracting environment.[46] National research documents that white-owned businesses win government contracts at substantially higher rates than minority-owned businesses even when controlling for business size and sector, suggesting that factors beyond pure qualification are influencing award decisions.[34][42]

Additionally, prime contractors sometimes engage in "bid shopping," where they disclose minority firms' subcontracting bids to competitors so that competitors can underbid, or fail to pay minority subcontractors in a timely manner, or limit minority subcontractor involvement to symbolic participation without meaningful work. One minority contractor interviewed in national research received promised work but then "got only a fraction of the work – despite sometimes showing up onsite with trucks and drivers," illustrating how discrimination manifests in contract execution.[42]

For Richmond specifically, the finding that even with OMBD services and procurement goals, utilization remains at 3.43% compared to 17.93% availability suggests that both access barriers and discriminatory practices are operating simultaneously.

**Estimated Scope:** This barrier is not easily quantifiable from public sources but is documented through the disparity study's qualitative interviews and consistent with national research patterns.

### Friction Point 6: Administrative Burden of MBE/ESB Registration and Certification

**Primary Challenge:** While certification is theoretically free, the process of gathering required documentation, submitting applications, maintaining active status, and navigating between multiple certification systems (MBE, ESB, SWaM, DBE, 8(a)) creates an administrative burden that disproportionately affects businesses without dedicated administrative staff.

**Manifestations:**

To register as an MBE with Richmond, a business must provide a color copy of government-issued identification, copy of business license, contractor's license (if applicable), proof of ownership (EIN letter or stock certificate), articles of incorporation or organization, and verification of revenue thresholds through tax documentation.[9] To maintain certification, the business must remain registered, update information as needed, and meet ongoing compliance requirements. For ESB certification, businesses must maintain location within the Enterprise Zone, maintain city business license, and continue to pay taxes to Richmond.[12]

If a business needs certification in multiple categories (SWaM, DBE, 8(a)), it must submit separate applications with different requirements and documentation. The Virginia SWaM process involves 60 business days for initial review, and if the application is incomplete, the clock restarts.[31] For a business with limited administrative capacity, this represents significant time and attention that diverts from core business operations.

National research found that "many interviewees commented that the SBE certification process is time consuming or difficult" and that some businesses are "unwilling to seek certification because of the paperwork involved."[35] While this does not mean certification is a barrier to businesses that persist, it may explain why some otherwise-qualified businesses do not pursue certified status and therefore cannot access set-aside opportunities.

**Estimated Scope:** Unclear from public sources, but likely to disproportionately affect sole proprietorships and very small businesses (under 10 employees) without administrative support staff.

## Friction Point Integration: How Barriers Cascade and Compound

The identified friction points do not operate independently but interact in ways that compound disadvantage for first-time and small minority-owned businesses. A business attempting to bid on a Richmond city contract for the first time likely encounters the following cascade:

First, the business must discover that opportunities exist, which requires awareness of the OpenGov platform or knowledge of OMBD services—friction point 1. Second, the business must then understand the solicitation language and identify that it is qualified to bid—friction point 2. Third, the business must determine whether it needs to obtain MBE, ESB, SWaM, or DBE certification, gather required documentation, and submit applications—friction point 6. If the business decides to pursue MBE credit, it must identify and secure subcontractors or suppliers that are also certified, or navigate the complex rules about participation types and commercially useful functions.

Fourth, the business must assess whether it can secure required bonding and insurance, or whether financial constraints prevent participation—friction point 3. Fifth, the business must consider whether it has sufficient working capital to bridge payment delays inherent to government contracting—friction point 4. Throughout this process, the business may encounter discrimination or discover that relationships matter as much as qualification—friction point 5.

At any point in this cascade, a business can rationally decide not to pursue the opportunity, despite being qualified to perform the work. Research on Philadelphia found that when the city simplified procurement processes, offered earlier assistance to smaller businesses, and allowed minority firms to compete as prime contractors rather than only as subcontractors, MBE participation increased substantially to 23% of contract value.[42] This suggests that simplifying the cascade and removing barriers at multiple points increases participation.

For Richmond specifically, the disparity study found that the city received only 3.43% of contract dollars to MBEs despite availability of 17.93%, and that "consultants said Black-owned firms reported the highest rates of direct discrimination in the marketplace."[46] This gap cannot be explained by lack of qualified businesses, suggesting that barriers to access and execution are the primary constraint.

## Conclusion: Summary of Verified Challenges and Inference-Based Barriers

Richmond's minority business contracting problem reflects both systemic barriers inherent to government procurement and specific implementation challenges within Richmond's procurement environment. **Verified facts** demonstrate substantial underutilization of minority-owned businesses (3.43% utilization versus 17.93% availability), active discrimination reported by minority contractors, and documented interest among minority businesses in accessing contracts (90% of interviewed MBEs wanted more opportunities). **Clear inferences** drawn from evidence suggest that information access remains constrained despite public databases, procurement process complexity creates disproportionate burden on smaller firms, capital constraints prevent participation despite competence, ongoing discrimination persists, and certification processes create hidden administrative burden.

**Unknowns include** the specific user experience challenges with the OpenGov platform, patterns of informal relationship-building among procurement officers and contractors, demographic-disaggregated performance data on contracts, and characteristics of businesses qualified but not attempting to participate. **Primary friction points** occur sequentially at opportunity discovery, procurement language comprehension, bonding and insurance requirements, cash flow readiness, discrimination in award and execution, and certification administration—with each barrier creating exit points where otherwise-qualified businesses rationally choose not to pursue opportunities.

The disparity study's recommendation that the city adopt specific remedies including anti-discrimination ordinances, expanded data collection, departmental MBE liaisons, expanded subcontracting goals, and increased OMBD staffing reflects recognition that current programming, while well-intentioned, has been insufficient to overcome structural barriers.[46] The city's current OMBD capacity of six staff (three compliance, one technical assistance, an administrator, and the director) represents significant constraints relative to the scope of barriers identified, suggesting that resource limitations may partially explain why access remains restricted despite service availability.[46]
