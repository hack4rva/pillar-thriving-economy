# Supporting Entrepreneurship and Procurement Access Through Civic Technology: Building Solutions for Richmond's Emerging and Minority-Owned Businesses

This comprehensive report examines the critical gaps in business support infrastructure for emerging entrepreneurs and minority-owned businesses in Richmond, Virginia, with particular focus on two interconnected challenges: helping these enterprises identify and access relevant government contracting opportunities, and guiding first-time business owners through the complex landscape of registration, licensing, and compliance requirements. The analysis reveals that while Richmond and Virginia offer substantial resources and frameworks for business development, the fragmentation of information across multiple platforms, the complexity of government contracting terminology, and the gap between written procedures and real-world staff expertise create significant barriers to participation. Civic technology solutions built during a focused hackathon could effectively bridge these gaps through prototype tools such as procurement search overlays with relevance matching, plain-language contracting guides, interactive business setup wizards, and integrated resource navigation platforms that reduce cognitive burden without requiring policy changes or systems integration.

## The Current Landscape of Business Support Infrastructure in Richmond and Virginia

### Richmond's Existing Business Development Ecosystem

The City of Richmond has invested in substantial institutional infrastructure designed to support minority-owned and emerging small businesses, though the effectiveness of these resources depends heavily on awareness and accessibility. The Office of Minority Business Development (OMBD), located at 1500 East Main Street on the fifth floor, serves as the primary municipal touchpoint for minority business enterprises, emerging small businesses, and disadvantaged business enterprises seeking guidance and registration assistance[1]. OMBD provides an array of free services including business technical assistance, business start-up and growth development, one-on-one business counseling, business assessments, business development workshops and series, registration and certification assistance, and financial options counseling and referrals[1]. These services represent a solid foundation of human expertise and institutional support, yet the challenge lies not in the absence of resources but rather in their discoverability and accessibility to the diverse population of entrepreneurs who could benefit from them.

The City has also recently implemented technological improvements to its business licensing infrastructure. In January 2025, the Department of Finance launched the new RVA Business Portal, which consolidates filing and invoice generation for Business Personal Property and Business, Professional, and Occupational (BPOL) Licenses, replacing the previously manual filing process[25]. This modernization effort represents an important step toward streamlining administrative processes, yet the portal addresses only one dimension of the business establishment journey. The broader ecosystem of business registration, compliance documentation, and contracting opportunity identification remains distributed across multiple systems and platforms, each with different access requirements and interface designs.

Richmond's procurement system operates through both direct vendor registration and opportunity notifications. Firms interested in doing business with the City are encouraged to complete the OMBD Vendor registration application and attach all required documentation[1]. Importantly, the registration process provides benefits including notification of upcoming workshops, conferences, seminars, and procurement opportunities, alongside the ability to view regular procurement updates. However, the effectiveness of this notification system depends on vendors understanding that registration is the pathway to opportunity notification—a prerequisite knowledge that new entrepreneurs may lack. The City also implements a formal procurement process with multiple pathways depending on contract value, from informal quotations for purchases under $3,000 to formal bid and request for proposal processes for acquisitions above $50,000[3].

### Virginia State-Level Resources and Frameworks

Virginia's business development ecosystem operates at both state and federal levels, with the Virginia Business One Stop serving as the foundational platform for entrepreneurs establishing businesses[4]. Over 33,000 small businesses of all sizes use Business One Stop to navigate the initial phases of entrepreneurship, and the platform provides access to professional business counselors who offer guidance on critical tasks[4]. At the state level, Virginia requires businesses to register their business name with the county clerk where the business operates, with corporations also requiring registration with the Secretary of State[2]. For licensing and zoning considerations, businesses must contact local business license offices and review local zoning regulations prior to selecting a site[2]. The Virginia Department of Labor and Industry provides information on health and safety requirements[2], and the Virginia Taxpayer Assistance Centers support businesses in understanding their tax obligations[2].

The SBA's Virginia District office provides additional support through its comprehensive Business Guide, which outlines requirements for starting, growing, and expanding businesses considering the specific legal and tax framework of Virginia[2]. The SBA specifically identifies that business requirements vary significantly by state, necessitating targeted research into Virginia's particular requirements before launch. Access to this geographically distributed information represents a significant cognitive burden for new entrepreneurs, particularly those without prior business experience or access to professional advisors. The challenge compounds when entrepreneurs are simultaneously navigating city-level, state-level, and federal requirements, each with distinct eligibility criteria, compliance obligations, and procedural requirements.

### The Gap Between Resource Availability and Resource Accessibility

The fundamental tension in Richmond's and Virginia's business support landscape is not a shortage of resources but rather their fragmentation and the cognitive burden this fragmentation places on entrepreneurs. Research on citizen-engaged government services demonstrates that when access to public information requires navigating multiple systems, understanding specialized terminology, and synthesizing information from disconnected sources, participation rates decline significantly[17][21]. A well-intentioned entrepreneur seeking to start a business in Richmond and potentially bid on city contracts must navigate: OMBD vendor registration procedures, BPOL licensing requirements, Business One Stop resources at the state level, local zoning compliance, health and safety permits depending on industry, federal EIN registration, and formal procurement notification processes. Each of these represents a separate application, often with distinct terminology, formatting requirements, and submission procedures.

The challenge intensifies for minority-owned businesses seeking government contracting opportunities. While the federal Small Business Administration provides substantial certification programs including the 8(a) Business Development Program, the Women-Owned Small Businesses (WOSB) Federal Contracting Program, and service-disabled veteran-owned small business programs, each with distinct eligibility criteria and application processes[7][28], many emerging entrepreneurs are unaware of these opportunities or lack the guidance to determine which programs align with their circumstances. Federal law mandates that 23 percent of all federal contracting dollars be allocated to small firms, with nearly 14 percent specifically awarded to small disadvantaged businesses, representing a potential opportunity pool of $179 billion in annual federal prime contracts to small enterprises in fiscal year 2023[7]. Yet accessing this opportunity requires awareness of certification processes, understanding of sole-source versus competitive contracting, knowledge of which agencies solicit in particular sectors, and the capability to respond competitively to formal request for proposal (RFP) processes.

## Understanding Government Procurement as a Pathway for Minority-Owned Businesses

### The Structure and Mechanics of Government Contracting

Government contracting operates according to distinct procedures that differ fundamentally from commercial procurement. The federal government and most state and local governments follow formal acquisition processes that prioritize competition, transparency, and compliance with numerous regulations rather than speed or convenience. At the federal level, agencies publish solicitations through the System for Award Management (SAM.gov), which serves as the central repository for all federal contracting opportunities[31]. The process typically begins with an agency identifying a need, developing specifications or requirements, publishing a formal request for quotation or request for proposal, evaluating responses against pre-established criteria, and awarding contracts to the responsive and responsible bidder or the bid offering the best value[3][45].

The City of Richmond implements a similar but distinct process governed by its Procurement Ordinance 2.52[3]. Purchases under $3,000 require contact with only one vendor to determine that the price is fair and reasonable, typically made using purchase cards[3]. Purchases exceeding $3,000 require multiple vendor quotations depending on the amount, with purchases over $5,000 requiring three quotations and purchases over $50,000 requiring formal bids or requests for proposal[3]. Formal bids and proposals must include detailed specifications and cannot be submitted via telephone or facsimile; late submittals are not accepted[3]. The City Charter requires that purchases exceeding $10,000 receive approval from City Council at a public meeting[3]. This multi-tiered approach creates distinct pathways depending on contract value, with higher-value opportunities following more formal and transparent procedures that theoretically offer greater opportunity for new vendors to compete on a level playing field.

### Barriers to Minority-Owned Business Participation in Government Contracting

Research and best practice analysis reveal multiple interconnected barriers that limit the participation of minority-owned and emerging businesses in government contracting opportunities. The first barrier is awareness and information discovery. Government contracting opportunities are published across multiple platforms with limited integration, and awareness campaigns typically target existing vendors rather than prospective entrants[42][42]. Boston's recent reform efforts reveal that advance notice of upcoming procurement opportunities is essential for smaller businesses that lack back-office staff dedicated to bid tracking[42][42]. Many emerging businesses are unaware that formal registration is required to receive opportunity notifications, and the registration process itself requires understanding the distinctions between different business types and knowing which NAICS code applies to their particular line of business.

The second barrier involves complexity of procurement language and requirements. Request for proposals issued by government agencies typically use formal procurement terminology, complex compliance matrices, and detailed specifications written for audiences with government contracting experience[47]. A manufacturing business owner seeking to bid on city procurement may struggle to understand the distinction between a Request for Quotation and a Request for Proposal, what constitutes a "responsive and responsible bid," or how evaluation criteria translate into scoring that determines whether their proposal receives serious consideration[3]. Plain language and user-centered design research demonstrates that procurement documents routinely fail to communicate clearly with audiences lacking prior government contracting experience, deterring participation precisely when streamlined access could expand the competitive field[21].

The third barrier relates to capacity constraints and administrative burden. Even when emerging entrepreneurs become aware of opportunities and understand the basic requirements, responding competitively to government contracting solicitations requires time and resources. The development of a formal proposal—including needs analysis, solution design, pricing, past performance documentation, and compliance certifications—can consume weeks and engage multiple team members[24]. For a small business with limited administrative capacity, the opportunity cost of dedicating time to a single proposal must be weighed against the business's core revenue-generating activities. When businesses estimate low probability of award because of perceived incumbent advantages or their own limited track record, rational actors may decline to participate entirely[42][42].

The fourth barrier encompasses lack of formal certification and credibility signals. Government agencies, particularly federal agencies, rely heavily on certifications to identify eligible vendors and assess credibility. The SBA's 8(a) Business Development Program provides significant advantages including sole-source contracting authority for qualifying firms, but eligibility requires meeting stringent criteria regarding ownership structure, personal net worth, adjusted gross income, and business experience[28]. The application process itself demands careful documentation and carries substantial compliance risk; applicants misrepresenting their eligibility can face legal consequences[28]. For entrepreneurs without prior experience navigating government certification programs, the risk of inadvertent non-compliance may deter application attempts entirely.

### Opportunities Within Government Contracting Programs

Despite these barriers, government contracting represents a legitimate and substantial opportunity for minority-owned businesses willing to navigate the system. The SBA's 8(a) Business Development Program, established decades ago and continuously refined, has developed a track record of supporting minority entrepreneurs' business growth[28]. Participating firms gain access to sole-source contracting opportunities up to $7 million for manufacturing and $4.5 million for other acquisitions, providing a competitive advantage unavailable to non-certified firms[28]. The certification process, while rigorous, provides a valuable credentialing mechanism that signals to contracting officers that the firm meets established criteria for business maturity and management capability.

City-level contracting programs add another layer of opportunity. Many municipalities, including the City of Fort Wayne and increasingly the City of Richmond, maintain emerging business or minority-owned business goals within their procurement processes[33]. These goals typically require or incentivize prime contractors to subcontract portions of work to certified emerging or minority businesses, creating indirect pathways to government work even for firms not yet ready to serve as prime contractors. The City of Fort Wayne's goal that each contractor awarded a construction contract subcontract at least 10 percent of the contract amount to a certified Emerging Business Enterprise demonstrates how municipal procurement can be structured to intentionally expand participation[33].

Additionally, transparent procurement data offers valuable strategic information for businesses considering government contracting. The City of Portland's implementation of the Open Contracting Data Standard provides public visibility into what the city is buying, for how much, and from whom, enabling potential vendors to identify opportunity areas and understand the historical competitive landscape[42][42]. By analyzing public procurement data, emerging businesses can identify which product categories, service areas, and contract values represent realistic opportunities based on their current capabilities and past performance documentation they can realistically develop.

## The Business Registration and Compliance Journey: Complexity and Information Fragmentation

### The Multi-Dimensional Requirements for Business Establishment

Starting a business in Richmond involves navigating requirements across multiple governmental levels and functional areas, each with distinct documentation requirements, compliance obligations, and timing considerations. The IRS Checklist for starting a business identifies core federal requirements including application for an Employer Identification Number if applicable, selection of business structure, choice of tax year, employee registration through Form I-9 and Form W-4 if hiring employees, and establishment of business tax payment procedures[8][8]. However, this federal checklist addresses only a portion of the comprehensive business establishment process; it does not address state-level requirements, local licensing, industry-specific permitting, insurance requirements, zoning compliance, or the decision-making processes necessary to select from among competing legal structures.

At the state level in Virginia, businesses must navigate several distinct requirements. Registration of business name with the county clerk is necessary, and corporations must also register with the Secretary of State[2]. Businesses must determine whether local zoning regulations permit their intended business operations and obtain any conditional use permits required by local zoning boards[35]. Most businesses require some form of business license administered by state and local departments; contact with the local business license office is necessary to determine specific licensing and permit requirements[2]. Tax obligations vary by business structure, with implications for both state and local tax liability and individual tax filing requirements[2]. Health and safety requirements, administered through the Virginia Department of Labor and Industry and potentially local health departments, must be addressed depending on the nature of the business[2].

At the local Richmond level, businesses must obtain a Business, Professional, and Occupational (BPOL) License within thirty days of beginning operations[25]. The licensing process requires providing the business name, trade name if different from the licensee name, business address, description of business type, identification number (Social Security Number or Federal EIN), begin business date, business entity type, and zoning certificate prior to issuance[25]. For contractors, Virginia Workers' Compensation Insurance registration is required, and contractors accepting projects of $1,000 or more must register with the State Board of Contractors[25]. Restaurants must obtain health permits, and any business selling alcoholic beverages must obtain an ABC License[25]. Sidewalk vendors must provide liability insurance naming the City as co-insured, and food vendors must provide health certificates[25].

### The Information Architecture Challenge: Fragmentation and Expertise Dependency

The institutional challenge confronting emerging entrepreneurs is not that requirements are unreasonable or that support services are unavailable, but rather that the information necessary to navigate these requirements is fragmented across multiple organizations with distinct service models, terminology, and procedural requirements. A first-time business owner seeking to establish a home-based service business in Richmond must piece together information from the County Clerk's office regarding business name registration, the Virginia Secretary of State's website regarding business entity formation, the City of Richmond Finance Department regarding BPOL licensing, the Virginia Department of Labor regarding employment requirements, the local zoning board regarding conditional use permits, and potentially OMBD regarding minority business certification if applicable.

Importantly, much of the procedural knowledge necessary for successful navigation appears to reside in the embodied expertise of government staff rather than in formal, codified procedures[44]. OMBD staff, through their daily interactions with entrepreneurs, have developed sophisticated mental models of which questions typically arise, where entrepreneurs commonly make errors, and which procedures are most efficiently navigated in sequence. However, this expertise is available only through synchronous interaction—staff-led counseling sessions, phone consultations, and in-person meetings[1]. For entrepreneurs unable to access these services due to schedule constraints, geographic distance, or simple lack of awareness that such assistance exists, the information gap remains.

This knowledge fragmentation creates multiple downstream consequences. Entrepreneurs who lack complete information may establish businesses in non-compliant ways—for example, obtaining a business license for a type of entity or business location that fails to comply with local zoning regulations. They may fail to register with appropriate state agencies, resulting in liability issues or inability to access certain government contracting opportunities. They may duplicate effort by attempting to perform administrative tasks in non-optimal sequence, unable to benefit from the process optimization that experienced staff have developed. Perhaps most significantly, they may become discouraged by the perceived complexity and abandon entrepreneurial aspirations that would be viable with clearer guidance.

### Legal Structure Selection and Its Downstream Implications

One particularly consequential decision point illustrates the problem of fragmentation within the business establishment process. The selection of business legal structure—whether to establish as a sole proprietorship, limited liability company, partnership, corporation, or other form—has cascading implications for tax treatment, personal liability protection, regulatory obligations, and eligibility for certain government contracting programs. Virginia's basic requirements are relatively permissive; a sole proprietorship can begin operations essentially immediately upon beginning business activity and obtaining a business license[35]. However, a limited liability company provides personal liability protection that sole proprietorships do not, requires filing Articles of Organization with the Department of Financial Institutions, incurs a $100 state filing fee plus $50 annual registration fees[26], and may have different federal tax treatment depending on whether the owner elects corporation taxation[35].

For a minority business owner seeking SBA 8(a) certification, the choice of legal structure becomes consequential. The 8(a) program requires that the business be at least 51 percent owned and controlled by eligible minority owners[28]. If a business is established as a sole proprietorship initially but must later be restructured to accommodate additional equity capital or to facilitate hiring of employees, the restructuring process requires new filings and may complicate the application timeline. Alternatively, if an entrepreneur initially establishes an LLC with multiple owners to secure capital investment but intends eventually to pursue 8(a) certification, the ownership structure may no longer satisfy the 51 percent minority ownership requirement unless the minority owner can acquire the non-minority interest.

Yet first-time entrepreneurs typically make the legal structure decision with minimal information. Virginia law provides that registered agents must be designated when filing Articles of Organization for an LLC, requiring either the business owner to serve in this role or hiring a registered agent service for annual fees of $199 to $400[26]. Many first-time entrepreneurs are unaware of what a registered agent does, why one is necessary, or what the realistic options are. The decision between the registered agent options depends on factors including the business owner's stability and ability to reliably receive formal documents, the importance of privacy (a registered agent service can serve as a buffer for personal address information), and the comparative cost calculation. Without guided decision support, entrepreneurs may make choices that create unnecessary expense or subsequent complications.

### Decision Support and Staged Information Delivery

The research on digital civic infrastructure demonstrates that successful government service delivery depends partly on how services are staged and sequenced[17][21][38]. Rather than presenting all information simultaneously, effective systems recognize that decision-making is staged and present information in sequence aligned with the decisions individuals must make. For business establishment, the first-stage decisions include whether to establish a formal business (versus operating informally) and in which jurisdiction to establish it. Stage two decisions involve selection of legal structure and business registration. Stage three decisions encompass licensing and permit requirements specific to the industry. Stage four decisions involve hiring employees and associated compliance, if applicable. Later stages address growth planning, access to capital, and government contracting participation.

Current business support infrastructure in Richmond and Virginia does not fully operationalize this staged decision model. Individuals accessing OMBD counseling benefit from a human advisor who can guide them through these decisions sequentially and clarify the implications of each choice. However, individuals attempting self-service access to information face a system that presents all requirements simultaneously, without explicit sequencing or decision logic. The Virginia Business One Stop provides a web-based platform for business planning and information access, yet the platform must serve the diverse needs of entrepreneurs across multiple industries with distinct regulatory requirements, making comprehensive sequencing challenging on a single platform.

## Design Approaches and Digital Tools That Reduce Complexity Without Changing Policy

### Service Blueprinting and Customer Journey Mapping for Business Support

Service blueprint methodology, originally developed for understanding service delivery processes from both customer and operational perspectives, offers a valuable framework for identifying where digital tools can reduce complexity in business establishment and contracting processes. A service blueprint visualizes the relationships between customer-facing services and the back-office processes supporting them, revealing points where customers encounter friction, confusion, or inefficiency[38]. USDA's service blueprinting template demonstrates how government agencies can apply this methodology to understand customer actions in pursuit of goals, map direct and indirect services that support those actions, and identify opportunities to streamline service delivery[38].

For business establishment, a service blueprint would document a prospective entrepreneur's key actions—including researching whether to start a business, determining legal structure, selecting a business name, registering the business, obtaining licenses and permits, and potentially seeking government contracting opportunities. For each action, the blueprint would map both the direct services the entrepreneur needs (such as business name registration, licensing application processing, and counseling) and indirect services that enable those direct services (such as zoning verification, identity verification for business registration, and background checks for certain licenses). By visualizing this entire ecosystem, service designers can identify which steps create confusion or require repeated handoffs, where information could be presented more clearly, and where sequencing of steps could be optimized.

Equally valuable is customer journey mapping, which documents how a customer experiences an organization's services across multiple touchpoints over time. A complete journey map for a prospective minority business owner in Richmond seeking government contracting opportunities would begin with the individual's awareness that government contracting is possible, continue through the decision to pursue certification, document the application and certification process, include the discovery of specific contracting opportunities, and extend through the proposal process and contract execution. By mapping this complete journey, procurement staff and economic development professionals can identify where individuals become discouraged, where the information is unclear, where processes create unnecessary delays, and where bottlenecks prevent timely decision-making.

### Plain Language and User-Centered Content Design

The Section 508 accessibility requirements and the Plain Language Act of 2010 establish as law the principle that government communications must be understandable to the public. Yet compliance with these requirements remains inconsistent across government agencies and platforms. Research on government digital services reveals that even when agencies invest in web-based platforms, the content is often written by subject matter experts for audiences with prior familiarity with the subject area, resulting in content that is technically accurate but practically inaccessible to people making decisions for the first time[21].

The case study of USAGov's Scams Wizard illustrates how plain language and user-centered content design can dramatically improve accessibility without changing underlying policies or procedures[21]. The original Scams Wizard provided comprehensive written information about different types of scams and how to report them, but this information was dense and required readers to process substantial text while in a stressed emotional state. The redesigned Scams Wizard guides users through a brief series of simple questions—asking them to identify the type of scam they encountered and their preferred reporting method—and delivers targeted information based on their specific situation[21]. Usability testing revealed that this tool-based approach dramatically improved user comprehension and reduced the time required to find the needed information[21].

For Richmond's business support system, plain language principles would suggest that critical policy documents—such as the BPOL tax requirements, the vendor registration procedures, and the procurement ordinance—could be rewritten using fundamental principles: short sentences, active voice, concrete examples rather than abstract policy language, and explicit anticipation of common questions. Rather than a document stating "Contractors are required to complete Virginia Workers' Compensation Insurance regulations before we issue a business license," a plain-language version would state "If your business is construction-related, you must register for Virginia Workers' Compensation Insurance before you get your City license. Registration takes about one week and costs $___. Here's how to do it: [step-by-step instructions]."

### Decision Trees and Interactive Guidance Tools

Decision tree and interactive guidance tools represent an effective middle ground between comprehensive information (which overwhelms users) and human counseling (which is expensive and difficult to scale). A decision tree for business legal structure selection would first ask clarifying questions about the prospective business owner's situation—such as whether they will have employees, whether they are seeking capital investment, whether they are part of a partnership, and whether they are pursuing government contracting certification. Based on responses to these questions, the tool would guide the user toward the legal structure most appropriate for their circumstances and explain the implications of that choice.

Research on chatbot and decision tree implementations in government reveals both benefits and challenges[10]. Georgia's chatbot for COVID-19 information demonstrates that well-designed conversational interfaces can effectively answer frequently asked questions and free up staff to address complex issues. However, more complex decision trees can become difficult to navigate if they contain too many branching paths or require users to answer technical questions they do not understand[10]. The most successful decision trees balance simplicity (fewer branching options) with comprehensiveness (coverage of the most common user scenarios).

For business establishment, a structured decision tree might begin by asking the most fundamental sequencing question: "Have you already decided on a business name?" If yes, the system would ask "Have you checked whether this name is available?" If no to either question, the tool would provide guidance on name selection and availability checking before returning to subsequent decisions. By walking users through decisions sequentially rather than presenting all information simultaneously, decision trees reduce cognitive burden and improve decision quality.

### Process Maps and Guides to Sequential Decision-Making

Structured process maps document the sequence of steps required to accomplish a goal and can be designed to highlight decision points, prerequisites, and parallel versus sequential activities. For business establishment, a process map would document that business name registration must occur before BPOL licensing (since the City requires that trade names be registered with the Secretary of State as a prerequisite to licensing[25]). It would show that zoning verification must occur before the business opens to ensure compliance, but that this step can occur in parallel with name registration. It would clarify that for certain business types, industry-specific licenses (health permits, ABC licenses) must be obtained before the BPOL license is issued[25].

A process guide written for entrepreneurs could present this same information in narrative form, using clear language and realistic timing estimates: "Your first step is to select a business name and check whether it's available. This takes about two days. While you're waiting to confirm the name is available, start your LLC or other business formation if you're forming a more complex entity than a sole proprietorship. Once your name is confirmed available, you can register your business name with the Secretary of State—this takes about five business days. While that's processing, you should check zoning to make sure your business location complies with local requirements—this step can be done anytime but must be done before you open. Once your name is registered and you have zoning clearance, you can apply for your BPOL license. Most licenses are issued within [timeframe]. For restaurants, you'll also need a health permit before the BPOL license is issued."

### Procurement Opportunity Matching and Plain-Language Explainers

For minority-owned businesses seeking contracting opportunities, a critical gap involves the disconnect between a firm's capabilities and the contracting opportunities that might align with those capabilities. Government procurement data is increasingly becoming public and standardized, but the analysis required to identify relevant opportunities exceeds what most small business owners can practically perform independently. The City of Portland's implementation of the Open Contracting Data Standard demonstrates how standardized procurement data can be made accessible, including not just opportunity announcements but also historical contract award data showing what the city has purchased and from whom[42][42].

A civic technology tool designed for the Richmond context could incorporate the City's procurement opportunity announcements alongside historical procurement data, and present matching opportunities to users based on their business profile. If a minority-owned business is registered as an OMBD vendor and has indicated that it provides electrical contracting services, the tool could proactively notify the business when the City issues opportunities for electrical work matching the firm's service area and contract value range. The matching logic could be based on simple keyword matching (identifying that "electrical contractor" keywords appear in opportunity descriptions) or more sophisticated matching that understands that "lighting system installation" and "electrical infrastructure upgrades" represent relevant opportunity categories.

Additionally, plain-language procurement explainers could help entrepreneurs understand what government contracting opportunities entail. The distinction between a Request for Quotation and a Request for Proposal, the meaning of "responsive and responsible bidder," the process by which evaluation criteria are applied to score proposals, and the meaning of "sole source" contracting are concepts that could be explained using concrete examples and analogies to commercial contracting familiar to entrepreneurs. For example: "A 'sole source' contract means the city is contracting with one specific business rather than comparing bids from multiple businesses. This happens when the city decides your business is the only one that can do the work, or when you're a certified minority-owned or women-owned small business and the city has reserved that particular contract just for certified businesses like yours."

### Intake Wizards and Guided Self-Service Application Processes

An intake wizard is a step-by-step guided process that walks a user through providing information needed for an application, typically using simple language and branching logic that presents only relevant questions[11][39]. The Cerenade immigration forms system demonstrates intake wizard implementation for complex government compliance processes, where users are guided through providing information required for specific immigration forms, and then that information is transferred to the formal official forms[11]. This approach reduces the cognitive burden on applicants by breaking complex forms into digestible steps and clarifying why each piece of information is required.

For OMBD vendor registration, an intake wizard could guide prospective vendors through the registration process by first confirming the vendor understands the registration requirements, then asking basic business information (legal entity name, business type, ownership information), then requesting documentation in sequence (with explanation of why each document is requested and what it should contain), and finally confirming that the application is complete before submission. Rather than presenting a blank form with numerous required fields, an intake wizard would explain: "We'll ask you some questions about your business. Based on what you tell us, we'll let you know which documents you need to gather. This should take about 15 minutes." As the user progresses through steps, they would receive confirmation of progress ("You've completed 2 of 6 sections") and context for each section ("In this section, we need to confirm you own and control your business").

## Examples and Best Practices from Other Jurisdictions

### Boston's Procurement Planning and Transparency Initiatives

The City of Boston's recent procurement modernization efforts illustrate how relatively straightforward changes in information architecture and planning transparency can significantly expand business participation. Boston identified that participation in local government procurement is notoriously low, averaging between 2 and 3 bids per procurement—a competitive environment that limits the city's access to the full range of potential suppliers and deprives smaller businesses of opportunity[42][42]. The root cause analysis revealed that smaller businesses, which lack dedicated back-office procurement staff, struggle to become aware of upcoming opportunities with sufficient lead time to prepare proposals.

Boston's response was to develop a comprehensive annual buying plan that lists goods and services that different departments expect to purchase in the future but have not yet published formal bid documents for[42][42]. This advance notice provides months of lead time for potential vendors to prepare for upcoming solicitations. The city invested in making this buying plan usable by organizing it by UNSPSC code, contract amount, industry, and other searchable fields so that potential suppliers could identify relevant opportunities at a glance[42][42]. Additionally, the process of gathering information for the buying plan created secondary benefits by improving internal coordination across departments and revealing process improvement opportunities[42][42].

For Richmond, a similar approach could be implemented by OMBD and the Purchasing Division to publish an annual forecast of anticipated procurement opportunities, organized by industry, contract value, and department. This approach requires no policy change—it does not alter procurement rules or timelines—but it dramatically improves information accessibility for prospective vendors. Paired with the OMBD vendor notification system, an annual forecast would enable entrepreneurs to plan their business development activities strategically, building capabilities and documentation needed to respond competitively to specific known opportunities.

### Newark's Attack on Payment Delays and Paperless Procurement

The City of Newark's analysis of payment data revealed that government bodies' notorious reputation for slow payment reflects genuine operational challenges rather than intentional barriers[42][42]. Some departments could process invoices quickly because they maintained efficient internal procedures and utilized electronic systems, while other departments experienced longer payment delays because of paper-based invoicing systems and geographic distance from invoice processing centers[42][42]. For small businesses and startups, on-time payment can be critical for survival, and late payment can represent an existential threat as businesses struggle to cover ongoing expenses like payroll and rent.

Newark's response was to implement an electronic payment portal allowing vendors to submit invoices electronically and easily view past invoices and payments[42][42]. This digitization of procurement reduced both processing delays (by eliminating the time required to manually input invoice data) and visibility barriers (by enabling vendors to track their payment status rather than calling departments to inquire about payment). The project required no changes to procurement policy or regulations; it was purely a technology and process improvement initiative.

For Richmond, a similar tool could improve the vendor experience by providing electronic invoice submission capability, automated matching of invoices to purchase orders (reducing processing time by eliminating manual verification steps), and transparent tracking of invoice status from submission through payment. This type of vendor experience improvement would not alter procurement rules but would reduce administrative friction and provide vendors with greater visibility into the payment process.

### Portland's Open Contracting Data Standard Implementation

The City of Portland became the first city in the United States to implement the Open Contracting Data Standard (OCDS), an international standard for publishing procurement-related data[42][42]. By publishing open data on all procurement activities—what the city is buying, how much it's spending, and from which vendors—Portland provides unprecedented transparency that enables potential suppliers to understand the competitive landscape and identify opportunity areas[42][42]. The city developed business intelligence dashboards based on the OCDS data to make the information simple to understand, showing not just aggregate contract awards but individual purchase orders and transactions within those awards[42][42]. The system includes performance metrics for suppliers, enabling citizens to see whether the city's spending is having the intended effect[42][42].

For a city like Richmond, implementing OCDS would require significant technical work to extract and standardize procurement data from multiple internal systems and to develop visualization tools for public consumption. However, the investment could yield substantial returns by enabling entrepreneurs to identify opportunity patterns, understand which vendors are winning contracts in their industry, and benchmark their capabilities against successful competitors. The transparency would also help ensure that procurement decisions are perceived as fair and legitimate, addressing the common concern among minority entrepreneurs that government contracts are distributed to an established vendor network rather than based on competitive merit[42][42].

## Viable Hackathon Solution Categories and Implementation Approaches

### Procurement Search Overlays and Relevance Matching Tools

A weekend-buildable hackathon project could develop a procurement search overlay that sits above the official City procurement portal and enhances discoverability through more sophisticated matching logic. Rather than requiring users to search by contract number or department name, the overlay could accept natural language queries ("I do electrical work") and return relevant opportunities based on keyword matching and historical procurement pattern analysis. The overlay would not replace the official procurement portal but would serve as a guided entry point helping users identify opportunities that warrant closer examination.

Implementation approach: The tool would ingest the City's published procurement opportunities through an API or periodic data export. A matching algorithm would associate business capabilities (described by the user or extracted from their OMBD vendor registration profile) with opportunity descriptions. The interface would guide users to search through uploaded documents or entered business descriptions, identify potential matches, and present results with explanations of why particular opportunities were suggested ("This contract involves electrical system upgrades, which matches your electrical contractor business profile"). The output would be hyperlinks to the official procurement portal where users would complete their proposal submission, ensuring the official procurement system remains the source of truth while the overlay enhances usability.

### Plain-Language Contracting Guides and RFP Explainers

A second hackathon project could create plain-language guides to understanding and responding to government contracting solicitations. Rather than attempting to rewrite official documents (which would exceed a weekend development scope), the tool could create supplementary explainers addressing common misunderstandings. For example, an RFP explainer document would define key terms ("responsive bidder" means your proposal fully addresses all requirements in the RFP), explain the scoring process ("Evaluation criteria are weighted by importance; this RFP weighs past experience more heavily than cost"), and provide concrete examples of strong versus weak proposal sections.

Implementation approach: The tool would be developed as a searchable knowledge base of commonly misunderstood procurement concepts. Users encountering unfamiliar terms in a solicitation could search the knowledge base and receive plain-language explanations with examples. The knowledge base could be populated through analysis of common questions asked to OMBD staff, review of procurement solicitations to identify terminology, and user testing with small business owners to identify which concepts are most frequently misunderstood. The tool could include templates or examples of effective proposal responses in different categories to help entrepreneurs understand the standards against which their proposals will be evaluated.

### Business Setup Decision Wizards and Guided Navigation Tools

A third hackathon project could develop an interactive wizard that guides prospective entrepreneurs through the business establishment process in Richmond. The wizard would begin by assessing the user's situation (Are you starting a brand-new business? Have you selected a business name? Will you have employees?), then guide them through decisions in optimal sequence (legal structure selection, name registration, zoning verification, licensing, and potentially government contracting preparation). The wizard would provide context for each decision, explain the implications, and clarify the next steps.

Implementation approach: The tool would be developed using low-code or no-code platforms that facilitate rapid development of decision tree logic. User flows would be developed through interviews with OMBD staff and entrepreneurs reflecting on their business establishment experiences. The wizard would be designed to operate independently but would reference and link to official resources (OMBD counseling services, Virginia Business One Stop, City licensing pages) at appropriate points. Rather than attempting to collect all necessary documentation in the wizard, it would identify what documentation is needed and guide users to appropriate sources (e.g., "You'll need a Certificate of Good Standing from the Virginia Secretary of State, which you can request here: [link]").

### Rule-Based Eligibility Assessments and Certification Pathway Navigation

A fourth hackathon project could develop an interactive tool that helps entrepreneurs determine their eligibility for various business development programs (SBA 8(a), WOSB, SDVOSBC, minority business certification, emerging business certification) and guides them through the certification pathways. The tool would ask qualifying questions based on each program's eligibility criteria, indicate whether the user appears to meet requirements, and provide guidance on the application process and required documentation.

Implementation approach: The tool would be developed as a rule-based system incorporating the official eligibility criteria for each program. Users would be asked a series of questions about their ownership, personal net worth, business experience, and other factors. The tool would indicate whether they likely meet the criteria for each program. Importantly, the tool would include disclaimers that it provides guidance but not legal advice, and that users should consult with program administrators or attorneys before making final determinations. The tool would then guide users through the application process, either by directing them to official application portals or by providing step-by-step guidance on required documentation and submission procedures.

### Chatbot-Style Front Doors and 24/7 Information Access

A fifth hackathon project could develop a conversational chatbot providing 24/7 access to commonly needed information about business support services in Richmond. The chatbot would answer questions like "How do I get my business license?", "What minority business certification programs exist?", "How do I find out about city contracting opportunities?", and "What is the difference between an LLC and a corporation?" The chatbot would be trained on both official policy documents and plain-language explanations of common questions.

Implementation approach: The tool could be developed using chatbot platforms such as Microsoft QnA Maker (as used by the State of Georgia) or similar solutions. Training data would be compiled from OMBD counseling knowledge, official policy documents, FAQ materials, and user research with entrepreneurs identifying the most frequently asked questions. The chatbot would be designed to recognize when questions exceed its knowledge base and provide referral information (OMBD phone number, hours of operation, location) for additional assistance. Analytics on chatbot interactions would help identify gaps in available information and opportunities for additional resources or guidance documentation.

### Service Blueprints and Process Maps for Stakeholder Alignment

A sixth hackathon project could create service blueprints and process maps documenting the business establishment and government contracting participation processes from both entrepreneur and staff perspectives. These visual documents would clarify where friction points exist, where information is unavailable, and where processes could be improved. While a weekend hackathon could not implement comprehensive process changes, documenting and communicating the issues through blueprints would provide a foundation for subsequent improvements.

Implementation approach: The tool would be developed through user research interviews with both entrepreneurs (documenting their business establishment experiences and contracting participation attempts) and government staff (documenting their operational procedures and the common mistakes they observe). Service blueprints would be created using tools like Miro or Google Slides, documenting customer actions, customer emotions and pain points, touchpoints with government services, back-office processes supporting those touchpoints, and opportunities for improvement. The deliverable would be a visual document suitable for sharing with internal stakeholders to build awareness of user needs and establish consensus on improvement priorities.

## Recommendations and Implementation Pathway

### Priority Integration Points and Quick-Win Opportunities

The most impactful civic technology solutions for Richmond would target the initial discovery and awareness phase of both business establishment and government contracting. Many of the barriers entrepreneurs face could be significantly reduced through improved information architecture, guidance tools, and usability enhancements that do not require changes to underlying policies or systems. A procurement search overlay that helps vendors identify relevant opportunities earlier in the opportunity lifecycle could be developed and deployed with minimal technical integration with the official City procurement system—it would work as an external tool pointing users toward the official system.

Similarly, a business setup wizard addressing the most common sequencing and decision questions could be developed independently of the City's administrative systems. Rather than requiring integration with the BPOL licensing system or other operational systems, the wizard would guide users to correct resources and provide decision support. The wizard could be updated and refined as new entrepreneurs provide feedback on which decision points created the most confusion.

A plain-language contracting guide could similarly be developed independently, as a standalone resource that supplements rather than replaces official procurement documents. Because it would be supplementary, it could be created without formal approval from procurement leadership, reducing bureaucratic barriers to development and deployment.

### Coordination and Sustainability Mechanisms

While individual hackathon prototypes could provide significant value, sustained impact would require institutional coordination and commitment to iterative refinement. The most successful civic technology implementations involve ongoing collaboration between the technology developers and the government agencies whose services they are meant to improve. The recommendations would suggest establishing a formal partnership between any hackathon solution developer and OMBD, the Purchasing Division, and the Finance Department to gather feedback on prototypes, identify refinement needs, and develop pathways to integration into existing systems if warrantied by demonstrated value.

Beyond individual tool development, attention to institutional knowledge management would amplify impact. OMBD's substantial expertise in business development support and guidance could be more systematically captured and documented through the process blueprinting and decision tree development, making that expertise more accessible to entrepreneurs unable to access individual counseling sessions. Developing a culture of documentation and knowledge sharing within OMBD—where successful guidance approaches are documented and shared with colleagues—would allow scarce staff expertise to be leveraged more efficiently.

### Measurement of Civic Technology Impact

The most significant challenge in civic technology implementation involves measuring whether tools actually improve outcomes. From a user perspective, relevant metrics would include: the number of new entrepreneurs accessing business establishment information, the number of minority-owned businesses identifying relevant government contracting opportunities, the time required to navigate business establishment processes, the number of minority-owned businesses successfully bidding on city contracts, and the diversity of procurement awards. From an operational perspective, relevant metrics would include the reduction in staff time required to answer common questions, improved coordination across departments responsible for different aspects of business support, and reduction in compliance errors or incomplete applications.

A hackathon project could establish measurement frameworks even if full implementation is beyond weekend scope. By documenting baseline conditions (current number of vendors registered with OMBD, current procurement award diversity, current staff time spent on counseling), subsequent projects could measure whether civic technology tools shift these baseline metrics. This measurement discipline would help distinguish between tools that provide genuine service improvement and tools that represent interesting technology projects without meaningful impact on user outcomes.

## Conclusion

The City of Richmond and the Commonwealth of Virginia maintain substantial institutional infrastructure supporting minority-owned and emerging small businesses, yet the fragmentation of this infrastructure across multiple organizations and the gap between documented procedures and real-world implementation create significant barriers to participation. Emerging entrepreneurs and minority business owners face complex information requirements, unclear decision pathways, and cognitive burden in navigating business establishment, licensing, and government contracting processes. These barriers are not inevitable; they reflect organizational and information architecture choices that can be modified through strategic civic technology development.

Hackathon-scale projects can address critical gaps in information architecture, decision support, and usability without requiring changes to underlying policies, systems integration, or bureaucratic approval processes. A procurement search overlay would help minority-owned businesses identify relevant contracting opportunities earlier in their business lifecycle. A business setup wizard would guide first-time entrepreneurs through sequential decisions and clarify information requirements. Plain-language guides to government contracting would demystify procurement terminology and processes. Interactive eligibility assessments would help entrepreneurs understand their certification pathway options. Service blueprints and process maps would document pain points and provide foundations for ongoing improvement.

The ultimate goal of these tools is not technology for its own sake but rather democratizing access to information and guidance that currently concentrates with government staff members and entrepreneurs with prior experience. By making information more discoverable, more understandable, and more usable, civic technology can expand economic participation and ensure that Richmond's government contracting opportunities benefit from the full range of capable vendors in the community rather than limiting competition to an established network. This expanded participation benefits not only minority entrepreneurs themselves but also the City's procurement outcomes through enhanced competition, reduced prices, increased quality, and better outcomes for taxpayers.
