# Software Prototyping to Address Minority-Owned Business Barriers in Richmond's Government Contracting: A Research-Based Framework for Pillar 4 Implementation

This comprehensive research report examines Richmond, Virginia's government contracting ecosystem through the lens of ten critical research questions designed to identify and validate opportunities for weekend software prototyping to support small and minority-owned businesses. Through analysis of existing city infrastructure, disparity study findings, federal procurement barriers, and comparable models from other jurisdictions, this report identifies that the clearest research-ready problem centers on information asymmetry and interpretive complexity in how minority-owned businesses discover, understand, and prepare responses to city contracting opportunities. The research reveals significant gaps between publicly available data and the tacit knowledge required to navigate procurement successfully, suggesting that a minimum viable product focused on opportunity interpretation and step-clarification could meaningfully reduce confusion within a 48-hour development window while establishing groundwork for larger systemic interventions. The analysis demonstrates that software prototyping represents one of many necessary approaches alongside policy, staffing, and organizational changes, and that successful implementation requires community-centered design principles, transparent collaboration with both government and business users, and a clear articulation of realistic scope to build trustworthiness and continuation support from stakeholders.

## Understanding Richmond's Pillar 4 Economic Development Goals and the Contracting Opportunity

The City of Richmond has explicitly committed to building a thriving economy that leaves no one behind through its Mayoral Action Plan (MAP), which identifies support for small, minority-owned, and women-owned businesses as a central economic development priority[7][27][28]. Pillar 4 of this vision establishes measurable goals including increases in the number of small and minority-owned businesses receiving technical and financial support from the city, and importantly, increases in the percentage of Richmond's procurement spending directed to minority-owned businesses[7]. The current state of this commitment reveals both an established institutional framework and significant unrealized potential. Richmond's Office of Minority Business Development (OMBD) provides registration, certification, and business development assistance to minority business enterprises (MBEs) and emerging small businesses (ESBs) at no cost[1][23]. The city maintains a procurement transparency dashboard and open data portal that makes contract information accessible to residents and businesses[5]. Yet despite these infrastructure components, the disparity between the availability of these resources and their actual utilization by minority-owned businesses suggests that information barriers, interpretive complexity, and gaps between public documentation and practical knowledge represent substantial obstacles to effective participation in government contracting.

The problem space is not theoretical. Recent disparity studies commissioned by both the Commonwealth of Virginia and the City of Richmond have quantified underutilization of minority-owned businesses in public procurement[4][29]. These studies employ rigorous methodology to compare utilization rates (the percentage of contract dollars actually awarded to MBEs) against availability rates (the percentage of MBEs capable of performing specific contract work in the relevant geographic area). When utilization lags significantly behind availability, the disparity creates a "compelling interest" that justifies targeted policy interventions and disparity study recommendations[8]. The City of Richmond's disparity study, conducted by MGT Performance, identified evidence of marketplace discrimination and underutilization, leading city leadership to accept findings and direct staff to draft legislation to implement disparity study recommendations[29][32]. This institutional commitment creates a policy window for innovation, yet the mechanisms through which Richmond businesses actually access opportunities and acquire the knowledge to compete remain fragmented and inefficient.

## The Current State of Richmond's Contracting Information Ecosystem

To understand where software prototyping could add value, one must first map what information systems and resources already exist in Richmond's contracting landscape. The city maintains multiple overlapping systems designed to enhance transparency and opportunity access. The Procurement Transparency Dashboard provides comprehensive contract information including agency name, contract number, dollar amounts, supplier names, procurement type, contract descriptions, and effective dates[5]. This data is updated monthly and accessible through the Open Data Portal. The city also participates in the OpenGov platform, which manages formal invitations for bids and displays information about open and closed invitations, successful and unsuccessful bidders, and bid amounts[5]. Additionally, the city manages a Supplier Portal where vendors must register through the iSupplier system to become eligible to compete for city contracts[2]. Registration requires completion of specific forms including IRS W-9s and ACH payment agreements, with the city promising approval within three to five business days.

These systems represent significant infrastructure investments and demonstrate commitment to transparency. However, each system serves distinct audiences and purposes, and collectively they present a user experience that places substantial interpretive burden on first-time entrepreneurs. A business owner seeking to understand Richmond contracting opportunities must simultaneously navigate the Procurement Transparency Dashboard to identify relevant contracts, the OpenGov platform to review specific bid details, the OMBD directory to understand certification benefits, and the Supplier Portal to register. Moreover, accessing this information requires understanding that these systems exist, knowing how to find them, understanding their different purposes, and possessing sufficient procurement literacy to interpret technical specifications, evaluation criteria, and compliance requirements. This fragmentation creates what researchers identify as "confusing requirements and difficulties in obtaining information on contracts"—a primary barrier minority-owned businesses report when seeking federal contracts[37].

The City's commitment to providing resources extends beyond procurement systems. Richmond's Department of Community Wealth Building and Office of Minority Business Development jointly offer free technical assistance, business counseling, business assessments, business development workshops, and registration assistance[20][23]. The Richmond Public Library's Richmond Room provides small business resources including access to online databases, legal forms specific to Virginia law, and one-on-one business counseling[6]. Multiple service providers including the Small Business Development Center offer no-cost individualized advising. These resources represent substantial public investment in capacity building. Yet the research questions posed in this analysis suggest that these services, while valuable, operate alongside significant knowledge gaps and tacit expertise that remains difficult for first-time entrepreneurs to access systematically.

## Barriers Identified in Research and Disparity Studies

Understanding the specific barriers minority-owned businesses face when pursuing Richmond contracting opportunities requires synthesis of multiple research sources including federal disparity studies, state-level analyses, and local findings. The research landscape identifies five primary categories of barriers that recur across jurisdictions: confusing requirements and information access difficulties, preferences for established businesses, limitations on set-aside contract sizes, high costs and time intensity of application processes, and contract bundling practices[37].

The most immediately relevant barrier for Richmond's context involves information access and interpretive complexity. The Third Way report on federal contracting found that minority-owned businesses experience greater challenges in simply obtaining information about opportunities, understanding bidding processes, and securing details about government contracts[37]. One study evaluating disparities in the contracting process found that 65% of minority and women business owners felt their firms were viewed as less competent than non-minority firms, suggesting that information barriers interact with deeper marketplace discrimination[37]. Additionally, 86% of minority and women-owned businesses reported that barriers in networking held them back in the contracting process, indicating that access to informal knowledge-sharing networks—where understanding about "how things really work" circulates—represents a critical gap[37]. The Urban Institute's analysis of state and local government procurement emphasizes that information asymmetry particularly disadvantages newer or smaller firms, which often lack established relationships with procurement staff[41].

At the federal level, the General Services Administration reports that while it awards more than $3.3 billion in contracts to small businesses annually, and while OSDBU (Office of Small and Disadvantaged Business Utilization) provides counseling, information, resources, and training, small businesses still describe the federal contracting process as "daunting"[38]. The process requires understanding dozens of certification categories, registration systems, contract types, compliance requirements, and evaluation methodologies. For minority-owned businesses, this complexity compounds because many lack access to established networks that could help them navigate it. A federal report examining barriers to minority business participation found that 86% of minority and women-owned businesses cited networking barriers as obstacles, while difficulties in obtaining information and understanding the bidding process were nearly universal complaints[37].

Richmond's own context adds layers of complexity. Virginia-specific registration requirements differ from federal requirements. City contracting procedures differ from Virginia Department of Taxation procedures, which differ from Virginia Department of Small Business and Supplier Diversity procedures for SWAM (Small, Women, or Minority-Owned) certification[13]. A first-time entrepreneur must understand which procedures apply to different contexts and sequence them appropriately. The city requires vendors to register on iSupplier with specific documentation[2]. The state offers separate SWAM certification with distinct benefits for state procurement[13]. The federal government requires SAM.gov registration and potentially other registrations depending on contract type[48]. Each system has different definitions of who qualifies, what documentation is required, and what benefits certification confers.

## The First-Time Entrepreneur Journey: Current State and Failure Points

To identify where software prototyping could add realistic value, one must map the actual sequence of steps a first-time Richmond entrepreneur follows—or attempts to follow—when they decide to pursue government contracting. This journey reveals critical points where information becomes unclear, error-prone, or requires tacit knowledge.

Research on small business startup requirements provides a framework[12][13]. A first-time entrepreneur typically must: conduct market research to understand opportunity, develop a business plan, secure funding, choose a business location, select a business structure, choose a business name, register the business formally, obtain federal and state tax IDs, apply for required licenses and permits, and open a business bank account[12][13]. For each of these steps, entrepreneurs in Richmond may consult various resources: the Small Business Administration website, Virginia-specific guidance, local SBDC advisors, library resources, or privately published materials. Once these foundational steps are complete, the entrepreneur then faces the distinct challenge of pursuing government contracting.

At that point, the journey becomes procedurally more complex. The entrepreneur must understand the different types of government contracts (fixed-price, cost-reimbursement, time-and-materials, IDIQ)[48]. They must identify target agencies and understand each agency's specific needs and procurement processes. They must register their business with appropriate systems: SAM.gov for federal contracting, state systems for state contracting, city systems for city contracting. They must obtain certifications they qualify for (small business status, minority-owned status, woman-owned status, service-disabled veteran status, HUBZone status). They must research specific contracting opportunities that match their capabilities. They must develop competitive proposals. They must navigate compliance and regulatory requirements specific to their industry. They must understand past performance requirements and how to demonstrate them.

For Richmond specifically, the journey involves additional steps: registering on the city's iSupplier Supplier Portal, understanding the city's procurement transparency dashboard and OpenGov platform, potentially pursuing OMBD registration and certification, understanding the city's specific evaluation criteria and contracting categories, and knowing when and how to engage with OMBD staff for technical assistance. The research questions posed at the outset ask specifically where this journey becomes "unclear or error-prone." Based on synthesis of research, the primary failure points appear to be: initial discovery that Richmond contracting opportunities exist, understanding what different systems (iSupplier, OpenGov, transparency dashboard, OMBD registry) do and why they're separate, understanding what certifications exist and which apply to specific entrepreneurs' situations, interpreting technical specification and evaluation criteria language, understanding the timeline and what to expect at each stage, and knowing when and how to access human support.

## Mapping Documented vs. Tacit Knowledge in Richmond's Contracting System

One of the research questions asks directly which parts of the small-business startup journey are publicly documented and which seem to depend on tacit staff knowledge. This distinction proves critical for identifying what software prototyping could realistically automate or clarify versus what necessarily requires human expertise and discretion.

Publicly documented information includes: general SBA guidance on business formation and federal contracting[12], Virginia-specific startup guidance[13], city procurement transparency data published in open-access formats, OMBD program descriptions and free service offerings, and federal acquisition regulation provisions. This information exists in multiple formats: web pages, PDF documents, databases, and institutional websites. Much of it is accurate and relatively current, though it may not be specifically tailored to first-time entrepreneurs or minority-owned businesses seeking Richmond contracts specifically.

Tacit knowledge—information held by individuals that is not systematically documented—includes understanding of informal procurement practices, knowledge of which procurement staff are most responsive and knowledgeable, understanding of what specific agencies actually need versus what their formal solicitations state, informal networks where opportunities are discussed before formal announcement, understanding of realistic timelines given specific agency approval processes, knowledge of common mistakes first-time bidders make on specific types of contracts, understanding of how to interpret ambiguous evaluation criteria language, knowledge of which certifications have real market value versus which are purely administrative, and understanding of how to navigate relationships with procurement officers. Much of this knowledge resides with OMBD staff, SBDC advisors, and experienced business owners. It circulates informally. Minority-owned businesses frequently report having less access to these informal networks, creating what researchers describe as "networking barriers."

The distinction matters for software prototyping because a weekend prototype cannot realistically encode all tacit knowledge. However, it could systematize documented knowledge and reduce the cognitive load of searching for it across multiple platforms. It could also create structured entry points for engagement with human expertise when needed. For example, if a software tool could walk a user through understanding Richmond's multiple registration systems and help them assess which apply to their specific situation, it would reduce confusion even if it ultimately directed them to OMBD staff for certification advice.

## Existing Support Infrastructure: Assets and Gaps

Richmond has assembled a comparatively robust ecosystem of support for small businesses and entrepreneurs, distributed across city government, library services, nonprofit intermediaries, and federal agencies. The research question about "which Richmond institutions already support these users, and where are gaps" requires mapping this landscape and identifying where gaps remain despite institutional effort.

The Office of Minority Business Development provides the primary city-level support[23]. OMBD offers free business technical assistance, business start-up and growth development, one-on-one business counseling, business assessments, business development workshops, registration and certification assistance, and financial options counseling and referrals. OMBD maintains a registry of MBEs and ESBs. The office has successfully increased certified MBE participation in city contracting over recent years. However, OMBD operates with finite staff and resources. Technical assistance demand likely exceeds capacity. The registry, while valuable, is primarily backward-looking—it tracks who is already certified rather than proactively identifying qualified businesses that should be certified.

The Richmond Public Library's Richmond Room provides free access to business resources including online databases like ACCEL5 Online Learning, Gale Legal Forms, LexisNexis, and Universal Class[6]. Library staff provide one-on-one business counseling by appointment. The library partners with OMBD and SBDC to offer workshops. This represents substantial resource investment and genuine accessibility. Yet library services reach different populations than direct procurement engagement might, and library staff have limited capacity for sustained relationship-building with specific entrepreneurs navigating complex contracting challenges.

The Small Business Development Center offers no-cost individualized advising for startups and established businesses[6]. SBDC capacity similarly faces constraints. Federal resources through GSA's Office of Small and Disadvantaged Business Utilization provide national guidance and resources, though they are not Richmond-specific[38].

The gaps emerge in several forms: lack of integrated, user-centric guidance specifically addressing the Richmond contracting journey; lack of real-time opportunity interpretation and response support; lack of systematic tracking of which entrepreneurs at what stage encounter which barriers; lack of feedback mechanisms that would allow the system to identify and address emerging problems; and lack of proactive outreach to qualified but not-yet-registered businesses. The city's Strategic Plan for Equitable Economic Development (SPEED) emphasizes that these gaps represent material obstacles to achieving the goals of increased minority business participation and job creation[28].

## Software Prototyping: Potential Scope and Realistic Constraints

The question of which Pillar 4 problems are well-suited to software prototypes versus which require policy, staffing, or organizational change requires nuanced assessment. This report identifies multiple classes of problems:

**Problems requiring primarily policy or legal change:** For example, contract bundling practices that make opportunities too large for smaller businesses to pursue would require deliberate policy decisions to unbundle contracts. A software tool cannot make this decision. Similarly, if minority-owned businesses lack access to necessary financing for bonding and insurance, the policy response involves financing programs, not software.

**Problems requiring primarily staffing or organizational change:** For example, if the city needs more staff dedicated to outreach to qualified but not-yet-registered businesses, or if procurement officers need training on implicit bias in evaluation, these require organizational investment and cannot be solved by software alone.

**Problems where software could add material value alongside other interventions:** These include information access and interpretation, process clarity, sequence guidance, and initial triage to connect users to appropriate support. Software could help first-time entrepreneurs understand what systems exist and why, interpret specific opportunities, identify which certifications apply to their situation, understand timelines and what to expect, and know when and how to access human expertise.

**Problems that software might solve inadequately or incorrectly:** These include relationship-building between entrepreneurs and procurement staff, intuitive understanding of why certain evaluation criteria matter, navigation of ambiguous or conflicting guidance, and ongoing support through the proposal development process. These require human judgment, flexibility, and contextual understanding.

A weekend prototype could realistically address information access and interpretation, not because it could solve all problems, but because software excels at organizing and presenting structured information. The minimum viable product should explicitly acknowledge what it cannot do while providing clear value for what falls within scope.

## Comparable Models and Adaptable Patterns from Other Jurisdictions

The research question asks what comparable tools or service models from other places offer patterns Richmond teams could realistically adapt. Investigation of comparable jurisdictions reveals several relevant models, some of which have been implemented at varying scales of success.

At the federal level, the GSA has invested in multiple support tools and platforms. SAM.gov serves as a central registry and opportunity search platform for federal contracts, though research suggests it remains difficult to navigate for novice users[19]. The GSA's OSDBU offers guidance, training, and mentoring, though capacity constraints limit reach. The federal acquisition landscape has spawned multiple third-party tools like GovDash, which uses AI to help contractors navigate proposal writing and compliance[9], and GovSpend, which provides procurement intelligence for state and local procurement[11]. These tools demonstrate that software can add value in government contracting, though they generally target firms already engaged in contracting rather than first-time entrants.

At the state level, the Commonwealth of Virginia commissioned a disparity study to assess barriers to contracting for small businesses, minority-owned businesses, and women-owned businesses[4]. The study collected detailed data on marketplace conditions and contracting patterns, interviewed business owners, and made recommendations for programmatic and policy changes. This research-driven approach provides valuable intelligence for state and local government action. Virginia maintains SWAM certification and promotes it through its Department of Small Business and Supplier Diversity, though the system has faced accessibility criticism similar to federal systems.

Other cities have invested in user-centered design approaches to improve procurement processes. OpenGov provides municipal procurement software that streamlines everything from budget planning to RFP development to contract management[18]. The company emphasizes transparency, equity, and breaking down silos between departments. Some cities report significant efficiency gains and improved vendor participation following OpenGov implementation. However, OpenGov is enterprise software requiring significant implementation investment and ongoing licensing fees, making it different in character from a weekend prototype.

The City of Baltimore's MWBE program has received attention for its comprehensive approach combining disparity study findings with business development support and organizational accountability[8]. The program combines anti-discrimination focus with proactive outreach and technical assistance. This combination of policy, program, and relationship-building represents a model that software alone cannot replicate but could support.

Hennepin County (Minnesota) has implemented "Innovation by Design," a human-centered design approach to government service delivery challenges[39]. The county brings residents (or in contracting contexts, businesses) into the problem-solving process from the beginning, testing assumptions and iterating based on real user feedback. This approach could inform how Richmond designs contracting support tools—by ensuring business owners, particularly minority-owned business owners, participate in defining problems and testing solutions.

The most relevant comparable model for a weekend prototype may be the civic tech hackathon approach, where interdisciplinary teams rapidly develop prototypes to address specific public sector challenges[26]. Civic hackathons emphasize speed, learning through building, community engagement, and realistic scope. They often produce valuable proof-of-concept work that informs larger policy or technical investments, even if the prototype itself is not directly implemented.

## Research Question Analysis: Which Questions Are Most Suitable for Software Prototyping?

Returning to the ten research questions posed at the outset, this analysis now assesses which questions are most research-ready and suitable for weekend software prototyping, which questions require longer-term research before prototyping, and which questions may be better addressed through non-software approaches.

**Research Question 1: Which targeted Pillar 4 statement reflects the clearest, most research-ready Richmond problem for a weekend software prototype?**

This question essentially asks: Of all the Pillar 4 problems, which one has sufficient problem clarity, affected population clarity, and solution path clarity to warrant rapid prototyping? The analysis suggests that the clearest, most research-ready problem is **the information access and interpretation gap preventing first-time minority-owned entrepreneurs from understanding and engaging with Richmond contracting opportunities**. This problem is well-defined by existing research. The affected population is identifiable (new or emerging minority-owned businesses in Richmond). The solution path is clear at a conceptual level (consolidate fragmented information, clarify procedures, reduce interpretive burden). What remains uncertain—and what a weekend prototype could investigate—is what specific information product would most effectively address the gap. The problem is not that information doesn't exist; it's that the existing information is fragmented across systems, requires significant tacit knowledge to navigate, and contains enough procedural complexity that interpretation is difficult.

This problem is more research-ready than, for example, "How should Richmond redesign its competitive bidding evaluation process to eliminate bias?" That question involves deeper organizational change and would require extensive stakeholder engagement to answer responsibly. It is more research-ready than "How should Richmond fund minority business expansion?" which requires political and budgeting decisions.

**Research Question 2: Where exactly do minority-owned businesses get stuck when trying to identify and interpret Richmond City contracting opportunities?**

This question is highly suitable for extended user research but could also benefit from rapid prototyping. The research landscape provides general evidence that minority-owned businesses struggle with information access, networking, and interpretation of requirements. However, Richmond-specific evidence of exactly where stuckness occurs would strengthen intervention design. A weekend prototype could include a simple user interview component where three to five minority business owners are asked to complete specific tasks (e.g., "Find an upcoming Richmond procurement opportunity that matches your business" or "Tell me what the certification process would be for your business") while thinking aloud. Observing where they become confused, frustrated, or unable to proceed would provide concrete evidence of failure points. This research component requires no software development—just structured observation—but it could be integrated with rapid prototyping work.

**Research Question 3: What public procurement data and documents actually exist in usable form for a contracting-discovery or interpretation tool?**

This question is highly relevant for prototype feasibility assessment. A weekend prototype could conduct a rapid data audit: Can the relevant data be extracted from Richmond's existing systems (transparency dashboard, OpenGov, OMBD registry)? Are the data formats machine-readable? Is the information structure consistent and predictable? Are there gaps between what data exists and what an interpretation tool would need? This data audit does not require building the tool itself; it involves systematically investigating existing information sources. A weekend team could document findings such as: "OpenGov contains X number of active city solicitations; these contain Y fields of information in Z format; field interpretation guidelines are/are not available; the data is updated on frequency F." This research directly informs whether a software prototype is technically feasible and what scope it could realistically support.

**Research Question 4: What sequence of steps must a first-time Richmond entrepreneur follow today, and where does that sequence become unclear or error-prone?**

This question is well-positioned for rapid research and prototyping. A weekend team could create a visual flowchart or interactive guide documenting the current sequence: what a first-time entrepreneur must do today to participate in Richmond contracting. The team could research this by reviewing city guidance documents, OMBD materials, Virginia startup guidance, and SBA resources. They could validate the sequence by interviewing two to three OMBD staff or SBDC advisors. The resulting documentation (even if just a diagram or checklist) would provide value by making tacit sequence knowledge explicit. Where the sequence becomes unclear would emerge clearly from stakeholder interviews: "Everyone says you need to register on iSupplier, but nobody explains when you should do that relative to other steps" or "The city's guidance assumes you already understand what an MBE is and why it matters."

**Research Question 5: Which critical parts of the small-business startup journey are publicly documented, and which seem to depend on tacit staff knowledge?**

This question lends itself well to a documentation audit. A weekend team could systematically review public materials (city guidance, SBA resources, Virginia guidance) and map what is and is not documented. They could then interview three to five OMBD or SBDC staff asking: "What advice do you give first-time entrepreneurs that isn't in any published material?" The documentation gap would become clear. This research does not require software development, but it could inform what a software prototype should prioritize (document the tacit knowledge that is most frequently needed and most difficult for users to discover).

**Research Question 6: Which Richmond institutions already support these users, and where are the gaps between public information, service delivery, and user understanding?**

This question requires institutional mapping and user research. A weekend prototype could include: documenting all institutions offering relevant support, documenting what they offer and through what channels, and then interviewing six to eight minority business owners asking: "Have you heard of [institution]? What do you think they offer? Would you contact them? Why or why not?" The gap between institutional capacity/offerings and user awareness or understanding would emerge clearly. This research is more valuable than any software tool could be—it identifies the actual problem. The software prototype might then be designed to close that gap by making institutions and their offerings more discoverable.

**Research Question 7: What types of Pillar 4 problems are well-suited to software prototypes, and which ones mainly require policy, staffing, or organizational change?**

This report addresses this question by categorizing Pillar 4 problems and assessing which lend themselves to software solutions. Information access and interpretation are well-suited to software. Policy decisions about contract bundling, bonding requirements, or evaluation criteria are not. Staffing decisions about outreach capacity are not. However, software could support policy implementation once policies are decided. Software could improve staffing efficiency once staffing exists. This is a strategic question that should guide investment decisions across the city's innovation portfolio.

**Research Question 8: What comparable tools or service models from other places offer patterns Richmond teams could realistically adapt?**

This question is well-suited to landscape research. A weekend team could research SAM.gov, OpenGov, Baltimore's MWBE model, other cities' civic tech solutions, and identify specific features or approaches that Richmond could adapt. This research requires no software development; it involves systematic investigation and synthesis of findings. The report should document not just what tools exist but whether they are open-source, what they cost, what Richmond would need to implement them, and what evidence exists about their effectiveness.

**Research Question 9: What minimum viable product could credibly reduce confusion or improve access within 48 hours without pretending to be an official government system?**

This question directly addresses the scope of a weekend prototype and is the most suited to rapid development. A weekend MVP for Richmond contracting support could realistically: consolidate information about multiple registration systems into a single user flow, create a decision tree or questionnaire that helps users understand which certifications apply to their situation, provide a simple search interface over city contracting opportunities with guidance on interpretation, create a step-by-step guide to the current process, or curate and present existing OMBD and SBDC resources in a more accessible format. The key constraint is scope and realism about what the prototype is and is not. The prototype is a research and learning tool, not an official city system. It should be explicitly presented as such. Its goal is to reduce immediate confusion and serve as a testable hypothesis about whether better information access would meaningfully improve participation.

**Research Question 10: What evidence and framing would make a Pillar 4 prototype feel trustworthy, community-centered, and continuation-worthy to judges and stakeholders?**

This question addresses implementation and adoption. For a prototype to be trustworthy, it should: be co-designed with minority business owners (not just designed for them), be transparent about limitations and scope, acknowledge the reality that it cannot solve all problems, connect users to official city resources rather than replacing them, include user feedback loops, and demonstrate genuine value for actual users. Community-centeredness requires that the prototype be tested with actual minority business owners before being presented to judges and stakeholders. Evidence of value could include user feedback testimonials, quantified metrics from limited testing (e.g., "users completed the sequence flow in average 4 minutes," "80% of users correctly identified which certifications apply to their business"), and demonstrated connection to city strategy. Continuation-worthiness emerges when there is clear evidence that the prototype solves real problems for real users and that solving those problems advances measurable city goals (more minority business participation in contracting, reduced time-to-bid for emerging businesses, increased registration rates).

## Designing a Minimum Viable Product for Weekend Prototyping

Having established that information access and interpretation represents the clearest, most research-ready problem for a weekend prototype, this section details what such a prototype could realistically accomplish and its design principles.

The MVP should be designed around rapid prototyping methodology, which emphasizes speed, learning through building, and testing assumptions with real users[36][44]. Rapid prototyping differs from traditional software development by accepting rough implementation, focusing on learning rather than polish, and treating the prototype as a research tool rather than a product. The prototype could take several forms, from interactive wireframes to simple web-based tools to even low-fidelity paper prototypes. The key is that it can be built, tested, and iterated in 48 hours.

**Potential Prototype Concepts:**

**Concept A: The "Richmond Contracting Sequence Mapper"** This prototype would present a series of connected screens walking a user through: understanding what contracting means, learning about Richmond's multiple systems, understanding which systems apply to their business, understanding what certifications mean and which apply to them, reviewing how to search for opportunities, understanding how to interpret opportunity descriptions, and knowing where to find help. The prototype would use decision trees to branch based on user responses (e.g., "Are you already registered as a business?" triggers different information). The prototype would emphasize: visual clarity, plain language, logical sequencing, and connection to official resources. It could be built in a simple web framework, Figma prototype, or even as an interactive PDF. Testing would involve having three to five minority business owners walk through the prototype while thinking aloud, observing where they get confused or what questions they have.

**Concept B: The "Opportunity Interpreter"** This prototype would focus on a single, hard problem: when a minority business owner finds a Richmond contracting opportunity, what do all the technical terms, specifications, and evaluation criteria actually mean? The prototype would take a sample RFP or solicitation and annotate it with plain-language explanations, historical examples, and guidance on what competitive responses look like. Rather than trying to automate interpretation, the prototype would provide structured scaffolding to help human interpretation. Testing would involve having users review an annotated opportunity and report whether the annotations were helpful and whether they felt more confident about whether they could respond.

**Concept C: The "Certification Decision Helper"** Minority business owners frequently report confusion about which certifications exist, which apply to their business, what each certification requires, and what benefits each provides. This prototype would ask a series of simple questions ("Do you own at least 51% of your business?" "Is your ownership based on minority or women status?" "Are you a service-disabled veteran?") and then clearly explain which certifications you qualify for, what each requires, where to apply, typical timelines, and what benefits each provides. The prototype would acknowledge that certification advice ultimately requires professional guidance but would help users come to that conversation informed. Testing would involve showing the prototype to minority business owners and asking whether the information was accurate, clear, and helpful.

**Design Principles for the MVP:**

Regardless of specific concept, the prototype should embody these principles:

**Plain language:** Government materials frequently use jargon ("MBE," "ESB," "DBE," "set-aside," "NAICS code") that creates barriers. The prototype should define terms in context and avoid jargon where possible. When specific terms are necessary, they should be explained.

**Visual hierarchy:** Information should be organized to highlight the most important elements. Users should quickly understand what the prototype is for and whether it applies to their situation.

**Realistic scope:** The prototype should be explicitly positioned as a learning and guidance tool, not an official city system. It should direct users to official resources rather than replacing them.

**Human-centered design:** The prototype should be designed based on actual user research (observations of how people currently navigate the process) and tested with real users, not designed based on assumptions about what would be helpful[39][47].

**Connection to support:** The prototype should help users understand when they need human expertise and facilitate connection to OMBD staff or other resources. Rather than replacing human support, it should enable more effective use of that support by helping users arrive at conversations informed and with clearer questions.

**Accessibility:** The prototype should meet accessibility standards so it is usable by people with disabilities. This includes alt text for images, keyboard navigation, color contrast, and plain language that supports cognitive accessibility.

**Iteration and feedback:** The prototype should include mechanisms for collecting user feedback and should be explicitly positioned as a draft for testing. Users should know they are helping refine the tool.

## Conducting Rapid User Testing for the Weekend Prototype

The value of a weekend prototype emerges not primarily from the artifact itself but from the research and learning it generates. Effective prototyping includes structured user testing that reveals whether the prototype addresses real problems and how it should evolve[36].

**Testing Approach:**

Rather than large-scale usability testing, the weekend prototype should be tested with three to five minority business owners recruited through OMBD, SBDC, or community networks. Users should be selected to include: at least one person who has previously applied for government contracts (baseline), at least one first-time entrepreneur, and ideally at least one person from an underrepresented minority group (recognizing that barriers may differ by ethnicity).

**Testing Tasks and Questions:**

Users should be given realistic tasks: "You want to bid on a Richmond city contract. Using this tool, figure out what you need to do to get started." Or: "You found a city contracting opportunity that seems like a good fit for your business. Using this tool, figure out whether you should bid." As users complete tasks, they should think aloud so the prototype team can observe where they get stuck, what questions emerge, what parts are clear or confusing.

After task completion, users should answer structured questions: "Did this tool help you understand the process?" "What was most confusing or unhelpful?" "What's missing?" "Would you use a tool like this regularly?" "What would make it better?" "Would it change your willingness to pursue a city contract?"

**Documenting Findings:**

The prototype team should document: specific quotes from users describing their reactions, points where users got stuck or confused, what information users found most valuable, what was missing, and users' stated willingness to engage with government contracting given this tool. This documentation becomes the basis for refining the prototype and for broader advocacy and resource allocation decisions.

## Assessing Technical Feasibility and Constraints

A practical consideration for weekend prototyping involves understanding what technical constraints may limit scope. This section addresses data, platform, and integration challenges.

**Data Constraints:**

As noted in addressing Research Question 3, the first technical hurdle is understanding what data actually exist in usable form. Richmond's Procurement Transparency Dashboard provides data via an open API or downloadable format. The data includes opportunity descriptions, contracting agency, dollar amounts, dates, and procurement type. The quality and completeness of opportunity descriptions varies. Some solicitations include detailed specifications; others are vague. A rapid prototype cannot fix data quality issues. However, it can work with existing data to demonstrate value.

A simple prototype could be built that queries existing city data and presents opportunities in a more accessible format: filtered by business type, organized chronologically, with interpretation guidance for key fields. This would require minimal data engineering but would provide value by improving discoverability and interpretation.

**Platform Constraints:**

A weekend prototype could be built using various platforms depending on the team's capabilities: a Figma prototype (clickable wireframes), a simple web application (HTML/CSS/JavaScript hosted on GitHub Pages or similar), a spreadsheet-based tool with built-in decision logic, or even paper prototypes supported by facilitator guidance. The platform choice depends on the team's skills and the specific concept. A prototype focused on guiding users through sequences could be built in Figma or as a simple web app in one weekend. A prototype requiring complex data integration would face time constraints.

**Integration Constraints:**

A production-ready tool would need to integrate with city systems (the Supplier Portal, OpenGov, the transparency dashboard, potentially OMBD's registry system). A weekend prototype does not need production integration; it can reference and link to official systems while existing as a standalone tool. This dramatically reduces technical complexity while still providing user value.

**Regulatory and Policy Constraints:**

A prototype positioned as unofficial guidance rather than official city policy faces fewer regulatory constraints than an official tool would. It should include clear disclaimers that it represents interpretation and guidance, not official policy. It should direct users to official city resources for authoritative information. These constraints are manageable and actually support the goal of positioning the prototype as a research and learning tool.

## Building Trustworthiness and Credibility With Users and Stakeholders

For a weekend prototype to move toward continuation and implementation, it must establish trustworthiness with both potential users (minority business owners) and stakeholders (city officials, judges, funders).

**User Trust:**

User trust is established by demonstrating that the tool actually works for the problems it claims to address. This requires user testing that produces genuine evidence of value. If users report that the prototype clarified confusing information or helped them understand next steps, that evidence is powerful. User testimonials are more credible than developer claims. Trust is also established through transparency: being explicit about scope limitations and acknowledging that the prototype cannot solve all problems. Users understand that software cannot replace human expertise; they value tools that help them use that expertise more effectively.

**Stakeholder Trust:**

Stakeholder trust emerges through clear connection to strategic goals. The prototype should be explicitly framed as addressing Pillar 4 economic development objectives: increasing minority business participation in city contracting. It should reference disparity study findings documenting the problem. It should present evidence of impact (even from limited testing) showing that users found the tool valuable. It should be positioned not as a replacement for policy, staffing, and organizational change, but as a complementary approach that enables these other investments to work more effectively. A stakeholder might not fund a large technology system based on a weekend prototype, but they might fund the data research that would support a future system, or they might fund the staff time to implement recommendations from user testing.

Trustworthiness is also established through community collaboration. If the prototype was co-designed with minority business owners and tested with them, that is more credible than if it was designed by city staff alone. If OMBD and SBDC staff are involved in testing and refinement, their credibility transfers to the tool.

## Potential Limitations and What a Prototype Cannot Address

For intellectual honesty and realistic scope-setting, this section addresses what limitations a software prototype faces and what problems require other interventions.

**What Software Cannot Do:**

Software cannot address systemic marketplace discrimination that exists independent of information access. If procurement officers consciously or unconsciously bias evaluations against minority-owned businesses, better information access alone will not fix that. This requires bias training, evaluation process redesign, and organizational accountability.

Software cannot provide the financing, bonding, insurance, and working capital support that minority-owned businesses may need to actually perform on contracts once they win them. These require policy and program interventions.

Software cannot build relationships and networks. The tacit knowledge about how procurement really works, circulating in informal networks, cannot be fully systematized or automated. Software can help, but it cannot replace the value of mentorship, relationship-building, and community.

Software cannot navigate ambiguity and provide the kind of contextual judgment that experienced procurement professionals can offer. While a tool can explain what contract evaluation criteria mean in general, it cannot tell a specific business owner whether their specific approach to responding will be competitive in their specific situation.

**Where Other Interventions Are More Important:**

Policy interventions to unbundle large contracts, revise evaluation criteria to be more inclusive, establish clear timelines and communication, and create accountability for MBE utilization may be more important than software tools.

Staffing interventions to increase OMBD capacity, create dedicated outreach roles, improve OMBD staff expertise in specific industries, and enable sustained relationship-building with emerging businesses may be more important than software.

Organizational interventions to integrate procurement decision-making across departments, establish clearer communication channels, address bias in evaluation processes, and build accountability systems may be more important than software.

Financial interventions to provide bonding assistance, working capital support, and business expansion capital may be the binding constraint on minority business participation in contracting, making software secondary.

A realistic strategy positions software prototyping as one element of a comprehensive approach that also includes policy, staffing, organizational, and financial interventions. The prototype should be designed to demonstrate whether and how software can add value to this comprehensive effort, not as a substitute for these other essential components.

## Long-Term Vision: From Weekend Prototype to Sustained Tool and Ecosystem

This report has focused primarily on what a weekend prototype could accomplish. For purposes of research and organizational learning, this section briefly outlines what longer-term evolution might entail if the prototype proves valuable.

If weekend testing demonstrates that users find value in better-organized information, clearer sequencing, and improved interpretation support, the next phase might involve: expanding and refining the prototype based on feedback, formalizing it as an official city resource or an unofficial but endorsed resource (in coordination with OMBD), integrating it with official city systems, and potentially developing complementary tools addressing other identified barriers.

A longer-term tool might integrate with the Supplier Portal to streamline registration. It might pull real-time data from the city's contracting systems to present opportunities in more accessible format. It might include case studies and examples of successful proposals. It might include video interviews with procurement officers explaining their decision-making. It might connect users to OMBD staff or mentors for specific advice.

The ecosystem could eventually expand to include: policy tools helping the city evaluate and implement recommendations from disparity studies; reporting tools helping the city track progress toward equity goals; community discussion forums where minority business owners exchange knowledge; training modules supporting business development; and financing platforms connecting businesses to capital sources.

However, this longer-term vision should be developed through continued engagement with actual users and stakeholders, learning what works through evidence rather than assumption. The weekend prototype is a research tool that tests fundamental assumptions about whether better information access would meaningfully improve minority business participation in contracting. If that assumption proves valid, further investment becomes justified. If testing reveals that information access is not the binding constraint, investment should redirect to addressing whatever the actual constraint proves to be.

## Conclusion: Research-Ready Problems, Realistic Scope, and Path Forward

The ten research questions posed at the outset reflect sophisticated thinking about the relationship between problem identification, software prototyping, user experience, and organizational change. Through analysis of existing research, Richmond's specific context, and prototyping methodology, this report identifies that **the clearest, most research-ready problem for a weekend software prototype is the information access and interpretation gap preventing first-time minority-owned entrepreneurs from understanding and engaging with Richmond contracting opportunities**.

This problem is well-documented by federal and state research. It is Richmond-specific enough to warrant local investigation. It is addressable through software without requiring that software solve all related problems. A weekend prototype could realistically reduce immediate confusion and serve as a testable hypothesis about whether better information access meaningfully improves contracting participation. The prototype should embody principles of rapid prototyping, human-centered design, realistic scope, and genuine connection to city strategy. It should be tested with actual minority business owners to generate evidence of impact. It should explicitly acknowledge limitations and position itself as one element of comprehensive efforts that must also include policy, staffing, and organizational changes.

The path forward involves: conducting rapid research to answer the remaining fact-based questions (data availability, current process mapping, tacit knowledge inventory); recruiting a diverse team with both software and community engagement capabilities; building a weekend prototype reflecting one or more of the concepts described; conducting structured user testing; documenting findings and refining the prototype; and presenting evidence of learning to city leadership and stakeholders. If evidence supports continued investment, subsequent phases could expand scope while maintaining commitment to user-centered design, community collaboration, and realistic assessment of what software can and cannot accomplish.

Richmond's Pillar 4 economic development goals are ambitious and important. They require comprehensive effort across policy, programming, staffing, relationships, and potentially technology. A well-designed, user-tested software prototype could contribute to this comprehensive effort by clarifying problems, testing assumptions, and generating evidence to guide further investment. The weekend prototype is not an end in itself but a means to accelerate learning and improve decision-making about how best to advance equity in government contracting.
