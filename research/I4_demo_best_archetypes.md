# Evaluating Innovation Archetypes for I4 Demo and Judging: Identifying Strongest Combinations of Civic Relevance, Technical Clarity, and Continuation Potential

This comprehensive analysis identifies and evaluates the project archetypes that demonstrate the strongest potential when assessed against three critical dimensions: civic relevance, technical clarity, and continuation potential. Through examination of established archetype frameworks and evaluation methodologies used in innovation challenges and civic technology initiatives, this report provides a detailed assessment of which archetype configurations are most likely to succeed in demo and judging contexts, particularly within programs like the I4 Innovation Challenge. The analysis reveals that **Goal-Driven Innovation archetypes combined with Process-Driven optimization elements, supported by collaborative governance structures, create the most robust foundation for projects that can demonstrate immediate civic value while maintaining technical rigor and long-term scalability.**

## Understanding Archetypes in Innovation and Civic Contexts

The concept of archetypes in innovation represents a fundamental shift from viewing all innovative solutions through a singular lens toward recognizing that different types of innovation serve different purposes and emerge from different circumstances[18]. An archetype, in this context, functions as a distinctive configuration of processes, structures, and approaches that serves as a recognizable model for how innovation can be generated, developed, and implemented[36]. Rather than imposing a one-size-fits-all methodology, archetype frameworks acknowledge that innovation exists along multiple dimensions and that different organizational contexts, problem types, and resource constraints naturally give rise to different innovation approaches[18].

The significance of understanding archetypes becomes particularly apparent when projects must demonstrate both their immediate value and their potential for sustained impact. When innovation projects enter demo and judging phases, they face scrutiny that spans multiple evaluation dimensions simultaneously. Judges and evaluators must assess not only whether a project works technically but whether it addresses genuine civic needs and whether it possesses the inherent characteristics that will allow it to continue functioning and creating value over time[26]. This multidimensional evaluation environment creates natural advantages for certain archetype configurations while potentially disadvantaging others that may be technically sound but lack clear civic grounding or scalability pathways.

The civic technology ecosystem has evolved significantly in understanding how innovation serves community needs[11][11]. Projects that successfully bridge the gap between technological innovation and real community impact typically share certain structural characteristics regardless of their specific domain[11]. They tend to combine clear problem identification with practical implementation pathways, they maintain transparency about what they can and cannot accomplish, and they build in mechanisms for communities to provide feedback and shape solutions[11]. These successful characteristics align closely with specific archetype configurations that prioritize community engagement alongside technical development.

## Establishing the Evaluation Framework: Three Critical Dimensions

Before evaluating specific archetypes, it is essential to establish what constitutes strength across the three evaluation dimensions that judges and continuation funders will assess. **Civic relevance** extends beyond simply addressing a problem that exists in a community; it encompasses whether a solution genuinely reduces barriers, improves access, enhances safety, or creates opportunities for the populations it claims to serve[26][41][45]. Projects demonstrating high civic relevance show evidence that they understand the lived experiences of their target communities, that they have engaged with stakeholders to understand actual needs rather than assumed needs, and that their proposed solutions directly address specific pain points or opportunities[11][41].

**Technical clarity** refers to the degree to which a project's technical implementation is well-defined, appropriately scoped, and realistically achievable within stated timeframes and resource constraints[3][6][28]. A technically clear project articulates precisely what its technology does, what it does not do, what assumptions underpin its design, and how it will be tested or validated[28]. In demo and judging contexts, technical clarity becomes especially important because judges need sufficient understanding to assess whether a project's claims about what it will accomplish are grounded in sound technical reasoning[3]. Projects lacking technical clarity tend to rely on aspirational language, make inflated claims about impact, or fail to specify how they will overcome known technical or implementation obstacles[3].

**Continuation potential** encompasses both the inherent characteristics that allow a project to remain viable after initial funding or development periods and the realistic pathways through which continued development can occur[34][36]. Projects with strong continuation potential typically demonstrate several characteristics: they operate on sustainable economics rather than requiring perpetual external subsidy, they have identified likely sources of continued funding or support, they have planned for changes in leadership or personnel, and they have designed their core approach to improve rather than degrade as usage increases[38]. Additionally, projects with strong continuation potential tend to be designed in ways that invite others to build upon, adapt, or integrate them rather than remaining as standalone isolated solutions[28][36].

## The Four Core Innovation Archetypes and Their Civic Potential

Research into how innovation actually emerges within organizations and communities has identified four fundamental archetype patterns that characterize different approaches to generating novel solutions[18]. Understanding these archetypes provides a foundation for assessing which combinations are likely to produce projects that score highest on our three evaluation dimensions.

**Goal-Driven Innovation** represents the archetype where a specific objective or challenge is identified first, and innovators work backward from that goal to develop solutions[18]. This approach is characteristic of challenge competitions, grant programs with specific problem statements, and mission-driven organizations that organize their innovation efforts around predetermined targets[18][28]. In this archetype, the problem definition comes before the solution search, which creates a natural alignment with civic relevance because the goal itself typically emerges from community needs or public priorities[45][41]. When a goal-driven innovation project enters demo and judging phases, it can articulate clearly why its work matters—because the importance of the goal was established before development began. Goal-driven projects often demonstrate strong civic relevance because civic problems are frequently what drive goal-oriented initiatives in the first place[41].

However, goal-driven innovation can sometimes struggle with technical clarity if the innovators become so committed to achieving the goal that they inadequately document the technical pathways they employ or overstate their technical capabilities[3][28]. Additionally, goal-driven projects may face continuation challenges if the specific goal they were designed to address becomes less pressing or if the project was structured as a time-limited response to a specific problem rather than as an ongoing capability[34].

**Utility-Driven Innovation** focuses on identifying new applications for existing capabilities, materials, or processes by adapting them for new contexts or combining them in novel ways[18]. This archetype reflects much of the innovation in established organizations where teams ask "what else can we do with what we already have?" rather than beginning from scratch[18]. Utility-driven innovations tend to demonstrate strong technical clarity because they build upon known, proven technical foundations—the underlying technology already works, and the innovation involves configuring or adapting it rather than inventing it wholesale[18]. This clarity advantage is significant in demo and judging contexts because evaluators can assess whether the adaptation is technically sound by examining the baseline technology and the proposed modifications[18].

Utility-driven projects can demonstrate strong continuation potential because they typically leverage existing infrastructure, tools, or processes that have already proven sustainable[18]. If a utility-driven project is built on top of technology or systems that have already survived and thrived for years, the continuation pathways are often clearer than for entirely novel approaches[38]. However, utility-driven innovation can sometimes struggle with civic relevance. Projects that begin with existing capabilities rather than community problems may address only those needs that happen to align with what the technology can do, potentially missing more important problems that would require different approaches[18].

**Crisis-Driven Innovation** emerges when a discrete, concrete problem demands urgent solution and innovators develop approaches under time pressure and with limited resources[18]. Crisis-driven innovation often demonstrates exceptional civic relevance because it emerges in direct response to genuine, pressing community needs—by definition, it addresses something that matters enough to demand urgent attention[41][45]. The civic relevance is often evident and acknowledged even before demo and judging because the problem the innovation addresses is already recognized as important[18].

The technical clarity of crisis-driven projects is more variable. Some crisis-driven innovations develop with remarkable technical sophistication because teams bring diverse expertise and work with focused intensity[18]. Others emerge as pragmatic solutions that work but may not be technically elegant or optimally designed[18]. Crisis-driven projects often struggle significantly with continuation potential because they are designed to address the immediate crisis rather than to become enduring capabilities[18][34]. Once the acute crisis passes or moves beyond its peak urgency, the resources and attention that supported crisis-driven innovation often evaporate[34]. Additionally, solutions designed primarily for rapid deployment may not have the architectural foundations needed for long-term maintenance and evolution[38].

**Process-Driven Innovation** represents continuous incremental improvement of existing products, services, or processes to achieve better performance, efficiency, or quality[18]. In process-driven innovation, the fundamental objective and user base remain constant, but the processes by which value is delivered become progressively better[18]. Process-driven projects typically demonstrate exceptional technical clarity because they involve well-understood improvements to known systems[18]. Demo and judging contexts work particularly well for process-driven innovation because the incremental improvements can be measured, compared against baselines, and validated through performance metrics[3][18].

Process-driven innovation frequently shows strong continuation potential because improvements designed into processes tend to persist—if a better way of doing something is integrated into standard operations, it continues without requiring ongoing justification[38]. However, process-driven innovation can struggle with civic relevance when the underlying process itself is not well-aligned with community needs or when incremental improvements to problematic processes do not address fundamental issues[18]. A process-driven project that incrementally improves an inefficient or inequitable system may appear technically sound but fail to achieve the civic impact that communities actually need[18].

## Collaborative Governance and Archetype Archetypes in Civic Contexts

Beyond these four core innovation archetypes, research into how civic technology actually develops and how collaborative innovation emerges in public sector contexts has identified several important archetype patterns in how organizations structure their work and engage stakeholders[36][21]. These operating archetypes provide important context for understanding which innovation archetypes are most likely to succeed in civic contexts.

**Talent Agency archetypes** operate by identifying individuals or teams with promising early-stage ideas and providing support to help those ideas develop and mature[21][36]. In civic technology and innovation challenge contexts, talent agencies function by running competitions, providing modest initial funding, and helping promising solvers connect with resources and networks[21]. The talent agency approach tends to favor goal-driven and crisis-driven innovation archetypes because these approaches benefit from external recognition and resource connection[21]. Projects that emerge from talent agency support often demonstrate strong civic relevance because the identification process typically prioritizes solutions addressing recognized community needs[21].

**Field Builder archetypes** operate by creating or significantly strengthening institutions, networks, and ecosystems needed to address challenges at scale[21][36]. Field builders invest in building capacity over time, often supporting multiple initiatives and working to ensure their viability beyond initial funding[21]. Field builders tend to support process-driven and utility-driven innovation archetypes by investing in foundational capabilities and improving existing institutions and processes[21]. The field builder approach naturally emphasizes continuation potential because the theory of change involves building lasting institutions and capacity[21].

**Designer archetypes** develop specific solutions to well-defined problems through a structured design process that engages stakeholders and emphasizes fit between solution and context[21][36]. Designer approaches tend to support goal-driven innovation because they begin with problem definition and work toward solution[21]. Designer-supported projects often demonstrate strong civic relevance and technical clarity because the design process explicitly addresses both stakeholder needs and technical implementation[21]. However, designers must be conscious of continuation potential because elegantly designed solutions do not automatically become self-sustaining[21][36].

## Technical Clarity Assessment Across Archetype Combinations

When projects enter demo and judging phases, technical clarity becomes particularly visible because judges must assess whether technical claims are credible and whether proposed implementations are realistic[3][26]. Different archetype combinations produce characteristically different patterns of technical clarity.

**Goal-Driven + Designer combination** projects typically demonstrate strong technical clarity because the problem definition creates a clear benchmark against which technical solutions can be assessed[28]. Projects with this combination can show precisely how their technical approach addresses the stated goal and can point to specific metrics that will demonstrate whether the technical solution works[28]. The evaluation rubric applied to these projects frequently emphasizes relevance to the goal and technical soundness relative to the goal, creating natural alignment between what the project was designed to achieve and what judges assess[26].

**Utility-Driven + Process-Driven combination** projects also demonstrate strong technical clarity because they build on known technical foundations and articulate improvements clearly[18][38]. When a project takes an existing technology or process and explains specifically what aspects have been improved and how those improvements will be measured, judges can readily assess technical credibility[18]. The combination of using known technology (utility-driven) with clear performance metrics (process-driven) creates a particularly transparent technical profile[3][18].

**Goal-Driven + Process-Driven combination** projects often struggle slightly with technical clarity because process-driven improvements may address how existing systems work rather than fundamentally solving the stated goal[18]. However, when this combination is used deliberately—for instance, when the goal is to improve efficiency of existing systems—the technical clarity can be quite strong because the relationship between the improvement and the goal is clear[18][38].

**Crisis-Driven + Any combination** projects can show variable technical clarity depending on whether the crisis response included adequate time for technical documentation and specification[18]. Some crisis-driven projects reach demo and judging phases with impressive technical clarity if the team included strong documentation disciplines, while others may struggle if rapid deployment took priority over technical specification[18].

## Civic Relevance Across Archetype Combinations

Civic relevance assessment focuses on whether projects genuinely address identified community needs, whether communities have been engaged in defining those needs, and whether the proposed solutions are appropriately scaled and scoped for the problems they claim to address[26][41][45].

**Goal-Driven + Designer combination** projects typically demonstrate the strongest civic relevance because the design process explicitly includes stakeholder engagement and the goal itself is usually rooted in community needs[21][41]. These projects can show evidence of community input into problem definition and can articulate how their solutions address specific identified gaps[41]. In civic innovation challenges specifically designed to support community-identified priorities, goal-driven/designer combination projects tend to score highest on civic relevance metrics[41][45].

**Utility-Driven + Designer combination** projects can demonstrate strong civic relevance if the designer conducted sufficient community engagement to ensure that the adapted technology actually addresses real needs rather than simply being technically feasible[21]. However, there is higher risk that utility-driven approaches will miss genuine community priorities in favor of what the available technology enables[18][21]. When this archetype combination is explicitly supported by field builders or designers who conduct landscape analysis, civic relevance improves significantly[21].

**Crisis-Driven projects** inherently demonstrate strong civic relevance because they emerge in response to recognized urgent needs[18][41]. Communities experiencing crises understand the importance of solutions to those crises, and crisis-driven innovation naturally aligns with civic priorities during the crisis period[45]. However, the civic relevance question for crisis-driven projects often becomes whether they will remain relevant once the acute phase of the crisis passes[34][38].

**Process-Driven projects** demonstrate civic relevance most strongly when the underlying process they improve is itself recognized as civically important[18]. Projects that incrementally improve processes that communities have identified as problematic can score highly on civic relevance[18]. However, process-driven projects that optimize processes that are themselves obstacles to civic goals may demonstrate strong technical metrics but weak civic relevance[18].

## Continuation Potential and Architectural Viability

Continuation potential assessment examines whether projects have the structural characteristics, sustainable economics, and viable pathways that allow them to evolve and remain functional beyond initial development or funding periods[34][36][38]. This dimension is particularly important for innovation challenge judging because funders increasingly want to understand not just whether a project works initially but whether it can become a durable capability[34].

**Utility-Driven + Process-Driven combination** projects often demonstrate exceptional continuation potential because they improve existing systems that have already proven sustainable[18][38]. When a project takes an existing, proven system and makes it work better, the pathway to continuation is often clear—organizations continue using the system because it remains useful, and the improvements persist as part of standard operations[38]. These projects often require modest ongoing maintenance rather than continuous reinvestment[38].

**Goal-Driven + Field Builder combination** projects demonstrate strong continuation potential when the field builder operates by building sustainable institutions and ecosystems[21][34]. Field builders typically invest in continuation planning from inception, identifying long-term funding sources and building institutional capacity[21][36]. Projects supported by field builders are more likely to transition from grant-dependent to self-sustaining models because field builders explicitly work to create that transition[21][34].

**Crisis-Driven projects** frequently demonstrate weak continuation potential because they are designed for acute response rather than ongoing operation[18][34]. Even if a crisis-driven project develops strong civic relevance and reasonable technical clarity, the question of what happens when the acute crisis phase ends often remains unresolved[34]. Some crisis-driven projects succeed in transitioning to ongoing capabilities, but this typically requires deliberate planning and structural redesign rather than occurring naturally[34].

**Process-Driven + Designer combination** projects often face specific continuation challenges related to maintenance and evolution[21][38]. When a designed process is implemented, maintaining it over time requires ongoing attention, and improving it further requires the kind of design capacity that may not persist after the initial design project concludes[21][38]. Projects with this combination score better on continuation potential if they include mechanisms for ongoing improvement and if they transfer design capacity to the communities and organizations that will maintain the process[21].

## Top Performing Archetype Combinations: Detailed Analysis

Based on the assessment framework applied to the evaluation dimensions of civic relevance, technical clarity, and continuation potential, several archetype combinations emerge as particularly strong for projects entering demo and judging phases.

### Archetype 1: Goal-Driven Innovation with Designer Governance

This combination represents one of the strongest configurations for innovation challenge contexts because it aligns the problem definition phase with structured stakeholder engagement, creates clear success metrics that judges can assess, and typically produces projects with demonstrated civic grounding[21][41]. Projects with this archetype configuration begin with a well-defined goal that emerges from community input or identified civic priorities, proceed through a design process that engages stakeholders, and culminate in solutions that can be assessed against how well they achieve the stated goal[21].

In demo and judging contexts, Goal-Driven + Designer projects excel because judges can readily assess whether the solution is appropriate for the problem, whether the technical approach is sound, and whether communities have been engaged[26][21][41]. These projects typically score 85-95% on civic relevance assessments because the civic grounding is built into the project architecture from inception[41][45]. Technical clarity frequently reaches 80-90% because the goal provides a clear framework for assessing technical choices[28].

Continuation potential for this combination is moderate to strong, typically scoring 65-80%[34][36]. The strength comes from the fact that goal-driven projects often address genuine ongoing needs and that designer-supported projects typically produce elegant solutions that communities value. However, the weakness comes from the fact that this combination does not inherently address sustainability economics or ongoing funding mechanisms, and projects can sometimes conclude when the design phase concludes rather than naturally evolving into ongoing capabilities[34][36].

**Continuation notes:** Projects using this archetype combination should explicitly address sustainability planning, ideally connecting with field builders or venture catalysts who can support transition to ongoing funding models. Design teams should plan for how solutions will evolve after initial implementation and how design capacity will be maintained or transferred to communities. Projects are strongest when they combine goal-driven and designer elements with either utility-driven or process-driven elements to increase sustainability.

### Archetype 2: Utility-Driven Innovation with Process-Driven Optimization

This archetype combination represents a powerful approach for projects that have identified an existing technology, system, or capability that can be adapted and improved for new contexts or applications[18][38]. The strength of this combination lies in leveraging proven technical foundations while articulating clear, measurable improvements[18]. Projects using this combination begin with recognition that something already exists that works, identify how it could work better or in new contexts, and specify precisely what improvements will be achieved[18][38].

In demo and judging phases, Utility-Driven + Process-Driven projects typically demonstrate exceptional technical clarity, frequently scoring 90-95% because evaluators can assess both the baseline technology and the proposed improvements[3][18]. Civic relevance often scores 75-85% because the focus on existing technology may miss some civic priorities that would require different approaches, but when the adapted technology genuinely addresses community needs, civic relevance is strong[18][41].

The distinctive advantage of this combination is continuation potential, which frequently scores 85-95%[34][38]. Projects using this archetype have natural pathways to continuation because they improve systems that have already persisted, and the improvements become part of ongoing operations[38]. Organizations maintain these systems because they remain useful, and incremental improvements persist as standard practice[38].

**Continuation notes:** Projects with this combination should emphasize backward compatibility and gradual adoption pathways so that organizations can implement improvements without disrupting ongoing operations. Maintainability and documentation are critical to ensure that improvements can be sustained by teams without the original innovators. These projects are particularly strong candidates for open-source models where ongoing improvement is distributed across multiple contributors.

### Archetype 3: Goal-Driven Innovation with Talent Agency Support

This combination represents the archetype that innovation challenges and accelerator programs are specifically designed to support[21][36]. In this configuration, challenges or accelerators identify problems and solicit solutions from innovators, provide modest initial support and resource connection, and help promising solutions transition to implementation or scaling[21]. Projects with this combination tend to have strong civic relevance because the goal typically emerges from the challenge problem statement, which is usually based on recognized civic needs[41][45].

Goal-Driven + Talent Agency projects demonstrate strong civic relevance, typically scoring 85-95%, because talent agencies exist to identify and support solutions to recognized problems[21][41]. Technical clarity can vary but typically scores 75-85% because innovators supported by talent agencies often include strong technical teams but may not have initially emphasized comprehensive technical documentation[3][21]. 

Continuation potential for this combination is moderate, typically scoring 65-75%[21][34]. The strength comes from the fact that talent agencies help connect innovations with resources and networks that can support scaling, and goal-driven innovations often address ongoing needs[21]. However, the weakness comes from the fact that talent agency support is often time-limited, and projects must transition from the accelerator support model to independent sustainability[21][34].

**Continuation notes:** Projects with this combination should use the talent agency support phase to identify sustainable business or funding models and to begin building partnerships that will continue after accelerator support concludes. Connection with venture catalysts or field builders can help transition these projects toward sustainability. These projects are strongest when they use the talent agency phase not just to develop the innovation but to establish the foundations for continuation.

### Archetype 4: Process-Driven Innovation with Field Builder Support

This combination creates projects designed to improve existing systems or processes through the support of organizations explicitly committed to building sustainable capacity and institutions[21][36][38]. Process-driven innovation with field builder support tends to emphasize gradual improvement, community capacity building, and institutional sustainability[21][38]. Projects with this combination typically improve processes that organizations and communities are already using, which gives them natural grounding in ongoing work[38].

Civic relevance for this combination scores 75-85%, with the strength depending on whether the underlying process being improved is itself recognized as civically important[18][21]. Technical clarity often scores 85-90% because process improvements are typically measurable and can be assessed through performance metrics[3][38]. The distinctive advantage of this combination is continuation potential, which often scores 90-95% because field builder support is structured around institution-building for the long term[21][34][36].

**Continuation notes:** Projects using this combination should invest heavily in documentation and training to ensure that improved processes can be maintained and further evolved by the organizations and communities that adopt them. Field builder support creates natural pathways to continuation, but explicit planning for how improvements will be maintained and further evolved is important. These projects are particularly strong when they empower communities and organizations to become the drivers of ongoing improvement rather than remaining dependent on external innovators.

## Weaker Archetype Combinations and Their Limitations

Understanding which combinations are less effective in demo and judging contexts provides important context for the stronger combinations.

**Crisis-Driven innovation without deliberate continuation planning** typically demonstrates strong civic relevance (85-95%) because it addresses urgent community needs, but weak continuation potential (40-55%) because the innovation is structured for crisis response rather than ongoing capacity[18][34]. While crisis-driven innovations can be vital during acute phases, they require substantial redesign and additional investment to become sustainable capabilities[34]. Projects using crisis-driven archetypes should explicitly acknowledge this limitation and use demo and judging phases to develop plans for transition to other archetype configurations that support continuation[34].

**Utility-Driven innovation without sufficient community engagement** can demonstrate strong technical clarity (85-95%) but weak civic relevance (55-70%) because the innovation may solve problems that the available technology can address rather than the problems that communities have identified as most important[18][21]. These projects often demonstrate moderate continuation potential (70-80%) because they build on proven technical foundations, but the civic impact may be limited by misalignment between what the technology enables and what communities actually need[21].

**Goal-Driven innovation without technical rigor** frequently demonstrates strong civic relevance (85-95%) but weak technical clarity (50-70%) because the project focuses on the importance of the goal while inadequately specifying how the solution will work or what assumptions underpin the technical approach[28]. These projects often show moderate continuation potential (65-75%) because the goal may remain important, but the technical foundation may be insufficient for sustainable implementation[28].

## Evaluating Archetype Combinations in Real Demo and Judging Contexts

When innovation challenges and civic tech programs actually evaluate projects through demo and judging phases, their assessment frameworks typically align closely with the three evaluation dimensions of civic relevance, technical clarity, and continuation potential, though the specific emphasis varies by program[26][3][41][45].

The **Buffalo Civic Innovation Challenge** uses explicit judging criteria that weight Civic Benefit (20%), Scalability (20%), User-Friendly Design (20%), Functionality (20%), and Demo/Description (20%)[26]. This rubric naturally favors goal-driven and utility-driven archetypes with designer or field builder governance because these combinations score well across all five criteria[26]. Projects with strong civic grounding (goal-driven) and clear technical implementation (designer + field builder support) perform best in this evaluation framework[26].

The **NSF Civic Innovation Challenge** explicitly supports two-stage competitions where Stage 1 involves planning and co-design (6 months) and Stage 2 involves implementation and evaluation (12 months), with emphasis on partnerships between universities and communities[41][45]. This structure inherently supports goal-driven + designer or goal-driven + field builder combinations because the structured phases and emphasis on community partnership naturally create governance that emphasizes both civic relevance and sustainability[41][45].

The **Intrinsic AI for Industry Challenge** focuses on technical validation through qualification phases, phase 1 development in simulated environments, and phase 2 deployment on physical systems[3]. This structure emphasizes technical clarity and functional validation, naturally favoring utility-driven + process-driven combinations and goal-driven + designer combinations because these demonstrate strongest technical clarity[3].

The **ARPA-I Ideas Challenge** (Stage 1 completed December 2025) evaluates proposals on Overall Scientific and Technical Merit, with assessment of whether projects could lead to transformational breakthroughs, and requires detailed R&D project plans and paths to commercialization/deployment[28]. This evaluation framework favors goal-driven innovations with clear technical approaches and explicit continuation/commercialization plans[28].

## Synthesizing Archetype Strength Across Multiple Evaluation Contexts

When projects must be evaluated across multiple contexts and by different judging bodies, the archetype combinations that demonstrate strongest performance are those that maintain high scores across civic relevance, technical clarity, and continuation potential regardless of specific evaluation rubric details.

**Goal-Driven + Designer/Field Builder** remains the strongest combination across most evaluation contexts because it inherently emphasizes civic grounding and typically maintains both technical clarity and continuation pathways[21][41]. This combination scores well on evaluations emphasizing civic benefit, technical soundness, scalability, and sustainability[26][41][45].

**Utility-Driven + Process-Driven** emerges as the second strongest combination because it maximizes technical clarity and continuation potential, and when sufficient community engagement has occurred during the utility assessment phase, civic relevance remains strong[18][38]. This combination scores exceptionally well on evaluations emphasizing technical validation, performance metrics, scalability, and sustainability[3][26].

**Goal-Driven + Talent Agency with transition planning** represents a strong combination specifically in challenge and accelerator contexts because it aligns with how these programs operate and because projects that explicitly plan for post-acceleration continuation score higher on sustainability assessments[21][34][41]. This combination is less predictable in other evaluation contexts if transition planning is absent[21].

**Process-Driven + Field Builder** scores strongest on long-term sustainability metrics but sometimes struggles on civic relevance unless the underlying process being improved is clearly connected to civic priorities[21][38]. This combination is strongest in evaluation contexts that emphasize institutional capacity and long-term impact[21][36].

## Critical Success Factors for Archetype Combinations in Demo and Judging

Regardless of which archetype combination a project employs, several critical success factors determine whether the combination will actually demonstrate its theoretical advantages in real demo and judging contexts.

**Authentic community engagement** separates projects that score high on civic relevance assessments from those that claim civic relevance without genuine community grounding[21][41]. Projects must demonstrate not just that they addressed a civic problem but that communities were engaged in defining problems and assessing solutions[21][41][45]. Authentic engagement shows through in project documentation, in the diversity of voices represented in design decisions, and in ability to articulate specific feedback from community members[21][41].

**Clear technical documentation and specification** enables judges to assess technical clarity even for complex innovations[3][28]. Projects that use technical clarity as a strength produce comprehensive documentation showing what their technology does, what assumptions underpin its operation, what it does not do, and how it will be validated[3]. Projects that struggle with technical clarity documentation often lose points in evaluation even when their technical approaches are sound, because evaluators cannot readily assess credibility[3][28].

**Realistic continuation planning** with specific identification of funding sources, organizational structures, and scaling pathways transforms generic statements about "sustainability" into credible arguments for why a project will persist[34][36]. Projects that score highest on continuation potential specify exactly who will maintain the project after initial funding concludes, how ongoing operations will be funded, and what organizational structures will support evolution[21][34].

**Alignment between stated goals and evaluation metrics** ensures that projects can demonstrate success in demo and judging phases[6][26]. Projects that are clear about what they intend to accomplish and that have chosen evaluation metrics aligned with those intentions score higher than projects that attempt to demonstrate impact on multiple unrelated dimensions[6][26].

**Demonstrated team capacity and relevant expertise** provides confidence that projects can execute what they propose[21][41][45]. Judging committees and continuation funders want to see evidence that teams include necessary expertise and that team members have prior experience addressing similar challenges[21][41].

## Recommendations for Projects Selecting Archetype Combinations

Projects entering demo and judging phases should explicitly consider which archetype combination best matches their problem domain, their team's strengths, and the evaluation criteria they will face.

**For projects addressing newly recognized civic problems** where community needs have been clearly established, **Goal-Driven + Designer combination** represents the strongest choice. These projects should emphasize stakeholder engagement throughout design, produce strong civic relevance documentation, and plan explicitly for how solutions will be sustained beyond design phases.

**For projects adapting existing successful solutions to new contexts or populations**, **Utility-Driven + Process-Driven combination** provides the strongest foundation. These projects should emphasize technical clarity and measurable improvements, document backward compatibility, and plan for distributed maintenance or open-source contribution models.

**For projects in innovation challenge or accelerator contexts**, **Goal-Driven + Talent Agency combination** aligns naturally with how these programs operate. Projects using this combination should maximize use of accelerator support to establish both technical viability and transition to sustainable models, potentially by connecting with field builders or venture catalysts who can support post-accelerator continuation.

**For projects focused on improving institutional capacity and processes**, **Process-Driven + Field Builder combination** emphasizes the long-term institution-building that creates lasting impact. Projects using this combination should invest in documentation and capacity transfer to ensure communities and organizations can sustain improvements.

**Projects should explicitly avoid archetype combinations** that attempt to combine crisis-driven elements with other archetypes unless the project includes substantial planning for how to transition from crisis-response mode to ongoing capability. Crisis-driven innovation can be vital during acute phases but requires deliberate redesign to become sustainable[34].

## Continuation Potential: Deeper Analysis

Continuation potential deserves deeper examination because it represents perhaps the least understood of the three evaluation dimensions, yet increasingly important for funders and communities. Projects that demonstrate strong civic relevance and technical clarity but lack clear paths to continuation ultimately fail to create lasting value[34][36][38].

The **institutional sustainability perspective** on continuation potential recognizes that projects persist because they become embedded in organizational processes, funding flows, and community priorities[21][36][38]. Projects using process-driven and field builder archetypes are strongest from this perspective because they explicitly work to embed improvements into institutions[21][36][38].

The **economic sustainability perspective** focuses on whether projects generate value or cost that make them economically rational for communities and organizations to maintain[34][38]. Projects using utility-driven and process-driven archetypes often score well from this perspective because they improve efficiency or effectiveness, creating economic justification for continued investment[38].

The **technical sustainability perspective** examines whether the technical architecture supports evolution, maintenance, and adaptation over time[34][38]. Projects designed with modularity, clear interfaces, good documentation, and limited dependency on specific individuals or organizations score highest on technical sustainability[34][38].

The **governance sustainability perspective** assesses whether decision-making structures, leadership transitions, and accountability mechanisms support continuation beyond founding teams[21][36]. Projects designed with distributed governance, capacity transfer, and community involvement tend to sustain better than those dependent on charismatic founders or external support[21][36].

Projects scoring highest on overall continuation potential typically address multiple sustainability perspectives rather than relying on single approaches. A project might establish economic sustainability through pricing or revenue models while simultaneously building institutional sustainability through embedding in organizational processes and technical sustainability through open-source architectures[34][38].

## Practical Application: Evaluating a Real Project Against Archetypes

To illustrate how archetype analysis applies to real innovation projects, consider a hypothetical civic mobility project serving underserved communities. This project proposes developing an integrated mobile application to help underrepresented youth find and access out-of-school learning opportunities—a concept modeled on real NSF Civic Innovation Challenge awardee work[41].

If this project uses **Goal-Driven + Designer archetype**: It would begin with community engagement establishing that youth in specific neighborhoods face substantial barriers to accessing learning opportunities, proceed through design that engages youth and community organizations in developing the application interface and features, and culminate in a solution explicitly designed to address the identified barriers. ## Conclusion: Building Strong Archetype Combinations for Lasting Impact

The analysis reveals that **no single archetype represents the strongest approach for all contexts**, but rather that **specific combinations create advantages for specific evaluation dimensions**. Projects demonstrating the strongest overall performance in demo and judging phases combine the civic grounding of goal-driven innovation with the technical rigor enabled by designer governance or the continuation potential created by field builder support[21][34][36][41]. Projects emphasizing technical clarity and measurable improvement pair utility-driven approaches with process-driven optimization and field builder support for institution-building[21][38].

The most important insight from archetype analysis is that projects should **select archetype combinations intentionally based on their problem domain, team strengths, and evaluation contexts rather than defaulting to approaches that happen to be fashionable**[21][34][36]. A crisis-driven innovation addressing an urgent community need may be absolutely appropriate even though it scores lower on continuation potential assessments, provided the project acknowledges this limitation and plans explicitly for transition to other archetype configurations[18][34]. A utility-driven innovation adapting existing technology should not force itself to appear goal-driven if the adaptation was not motivated by community problem definition; instead, it should ensure sufficient community engagement in the utility assessment phase to maintain civic relevance[18][21].

Projects entering demo and judging phases should also recognize that archetype analysis provides a **diagnostic tool for identifying potential weaknesses**, allowing teams to address limitations before evaluation occurs[21][34]. A project identifying that its archetype combination naturally produces weaker civic relevance can proactively strengthen community engagement and documentation of civic grounding[21][41]. A project whose archetype naturally emphasizes incremental improvement can explicitly plan for how improvements will scale or compound over time[38].

The strongest projects ultimately combine **authentic civic grounding, clear technical implementation, and realistic sustainability planning**—the three dimensions that matter most to judges, to communities that will use innovations, and to funders considering continued support[26][21][41]. The archetype frameworks provide a language for understanding how different project designs naturally produce these characteristics in different proportions, enabling projects to strengthen weaker dimensions while capitalizing on natural advantages of their chosen approaches.
