# MVP Feasibility Assessment: Building a Minority Business Enterprise Prototype Using Public Data

## Executive Summary

This comprehensive assessment evaluates the technical and operational feasibility of constructing a Minimum Viable Product (MVP) for a Minority Business Enterprise (MBE) contracting platform using exclusively public data sources. The analysis reveals that a functional prototype demonstrating core MBE discovery, matching, and contract tracking capabilities is feasible and can be built with publicly available datasets, though significant limitations exist around real-time data quality, comprehensive business intelligence, and advanced supply chain analytics. The prototype can effectively showcase supplier identification, basic capability matching, contract classification, and diversity spend reporting through integration of SAM.gov registrations, NAICS classifications, DBE directories, and federal contract data repositories. However, critical operational areas including detailed financial assessment, proprietary supplier performance history, advanced risk modeling, and dynamic pricing intelligence remain fundamentally constrained by the public data environment, requiring manual workarounds and human judgment for production-grade functionality. This report details the minimal viable field sets, demonstrates practical demo workflows, identifies specific brittle areas, and provides implementation recommendations for building an effective prototype that can validate market demand while establishing clear upgrade pathways for production systems.

## The Public Data Landscape for MBE Contracting Operations

### Overview of Available Public Datasets

The federal government maintains an extensive ecosystem of publicly accessible datasets specifically designed to support small business and minority business enterprise operations[2][5][5]. The primary repositories include the System for Award Management (SAM.gov), which serves as the foundational registry for all federal contracting entities and contains entity identifiers, ownership status, certifications, and historical contract awards[1][26]. The NAICS classification system provides standardized industry coding that enables procurement professionals to understand business capabilities and market segments[10]. The Federal Procurement Data System (FPDS) reports all federal contract actions exceeding the simplified acquisition threshold, offering historical contract award data, contractor performance records, and procurement patterns[5][5][34].

Beyond federal sources, state and local governments maintain MBE directories and reporting systems. The Maryland Department of Transportation operates an Office of Minority Business Enterprise that publishes a statewide directory of certified MBE firms along with their qualifications, specializations, and contact information[1]. Multiple cities maintain similar registries containing detailed MBE and women-owned business enterprise (WBE) data accessible through standardized formats including CSV, GeoJSON, and REST APIs[2]. These local and state datasets provide geographic granularity and often include recent certification status updates that federal datasets may not capture immediately.

The Disadvantaged Business Enterprise (DBE) program, governed by the U.S. Department of Transportation under 49 CFR Parts 23 and 26, maintains comprehensive information about certified disadvantaged businesses participating in federally funded transportation projects[3]. This program establishes ownership requirements, controls specifications, size limitations, and socioeconomic disadvantage determination methodologies that directly inform MBE certification criteria across federal and state procurement[3]. The Unified Certification Program (UCP) within each state coordinates DBE certifications and provides searchable directories that integrate with SAM.gov data[3].

Contract reporting requirements mandate disclosure of subcontracting plans for prime contractors exceeding specified dollar thresholds, and these plans include specific goals for small business, disadvantaged business, women-owned small business, and HUBZone participation[29][35]. The SBA maintains an online directory of prime contractors with subcontracting plans, providing transparency into which large contractors actively pursue small business partners and the specific goals they have committed to achieving[29].

### Completeness and Temporal Characteristics of Public Sources

The temporal characteristics of public MBE data present both opportunities and challenges for prototype development. SAM.gov registrations are updated continuously as entities modify their ownership, certifications, and capabilities, but these updates are not instantaneous[26]. Federal contract data reaches public repositories with a reporting lag—agencies must report contract actions with an estimated value of $25,000 or more to FPDS, but reporting timelines can extend several weeks after contract award[34]. State MBE directories typically undergo annual updates with quarterly refreshes, meaning that certification changes, dissolutions, or new certifications may not appear in public systems for extended periods[1][20].

The granularity of available data varies significantly by data source. SAM.gov provides entity identifiers, UEI, CAGE codes, and socioeconomic status indicators but does not expose detailed ownership structures, decision-making authority, or day-to-day management information beyond what contractors voluntarily submit in capability statements[26][15]. Federal contract data reveals contract action dates, dollar values, and prime contractor identification but provides limited visibility into subcontracting structure below the prime level except where explicit subcontracting reports are filed[29][34][35]. MBE reporting from state and local governments includes certification details but often lacks direct linkage to actual contract performance, payment history, or financial stability metrics[20][20].

## Core Data Entities and MVP Field Requirements

### Essential Entity Models for Prototype Implementation

A functional prototype requires definition of core data entities that represent the fundamental objects in the MBE contracting ecosystem. The primary entity is the Certified Minority Business, representing an individual business concern that holds or is eligible for MBE certification[1][3]. The minimal viable field set for this entity includes the Unique Entity Identifier (UEI), which replaced legacy DUNS numbers as the primary federal identifier and is the key linkage across government systems[4][26]. The business legal name, D-U-N-S number legacy tracking for historical continuity, and DBA (doing business as) names enable entity resolution and matching across diverse public datasets that may reference businesses by different name variations[6][26].

Ownership status fields constitute the critical dimension for MBE eligibility. The system must capture the percentage ownership held by individuals from socially and economically disadvantaged backgrounds, typically encoded as target percentages such as 51% or greater[1][3]. The specific disadvantaged business classifications matter significantly—the system should track whether the entity qualifies as African American-owned, Hispanic American-owned, Asian American-owned, Native American-owned, or other eligible minority classifications that vary by certification program[25][30]. Economic disadvantage determinations require personal net worth caps—currently $2.047 million for federal DBE programs—but these absolute figures are published in regulations and remain static across the dataset[3].

Control and management fields capture whether the disadvantaged owner actively controls day-to-day business operations and strategic decisions[1][3]. For prototype purposes, these fields can be binary indicators populated from SAM.gov representations and certifications, supplemented by capability statement assertions where available[15]. The system should also track the business structure type—sole proprietorship, partnership, corporation, or limited liability company—as this affects ownership verification requirements and subcontracting limitations[3][4].

The Certifications entity represents distinct official MBE designations held by a business. Each certification record links to the certified business and includes the certification type (federal 8(a), HUBZone, WOSB, SDVOSB, state MBE, state DBE, ACDBE), the certifying agency or organization, the certification date, and the expiration date where applicable[1][3][6]. Public data sources provide this information through SAM.gov socioeconomic status filters and state/local MBE directories[1][2][26].

The Business Capability entity represents the work categories, product types, or service specializations that a business is certified and qualified to perform. Each capability is tied to a NAICS code or commodity classification, along with a description of the specific work or products offered[1][6][10][27]. For prototype purposes, NAICS codes provide the standardized structure; the NAICS system includes six-digit classification codes enabling sector-specific searching[4][10]. The prototype should capture the primary NAICS codes and secondary codes where available from SAM.gov and state MBE directory data[6][15][15].

### Data Fields for Prototype Demo Completeness

The Contracting Opportunity entity represents active and recent federal contract awards that offer subcontracting opportunities for small and minority businesses. Essential fields include the contract award date, contract value, contracting agency, prime contractor name and UEI, procurement category or commodity type, the set-aside type if applicable (small business set-aside, HUBZone, women-owned, service-disabled veteran-owned), and documented small business subcontracting goals where required[3][29][34][41]. The prototype can source this data directly from FPDS and SAM.gov contract award records[5][5][34].

The Subcontracting Plan entity aggregates the documented commitments made by prime contractors regarding small business participation. These records include the prime contractor identity, contract award, stated subcontracting goals by category (small business percentage, disadvantaged business percentage, women-owned percentage, HUBZone percentage), and the stated outreach methods and procurement approaches[29][35][41]. For prototype purposes, these can be sourced from SBA's Directory of Federal Government Prime Contractors with Subcontracting Plans and extracted from publicly available individual subcontracting plan documents[29].

The Supplier Directory Entry represents a single business as it appears in publicly maintained registries. Beyond the core business identity fields, this entity should capture the directory source and date of last update, the current certification status, and any directory-provided quality signals such as past performance, customer references, or facility certifications[1][2][19]. The prototype architecture should maintain separate directory entry records even when multiple directories reference the same underlying business, as this enables tracking of data completeness and consistency issues across sources.

The Data Quality and Lineage entity tracks the source, currency, and reliability characteristics of each record. For each business profile and certification status, the prototype should maintain metadata indicating the source system (SAM.gov, Maryland MBE directory, Chicago MBE directory, etc.), the date the information was last refreshed from that source, and a confidence score reflecting whether the source is authoritative for that particular data field[22][38]. This metadata enables the prototype to implement intelligent fallback logic when conflicts exist between sources and provides transparency about data freshness.

## MVP Logic Architecture and Demo Workflow

### Minimal Viable Feature Set for Demonstration

A functional prototype requires core features that demonstrate the primary value proposition of MBE discovery and contracting support. The first essential feature is **Supplier Discovery and Matching**, which enables procurement professionals to search for certified minority businesses by industry, location, capability, and certification status[19][25][9]. The prototype implements this through integration of SAM.gov search APIs, state MBE directory databases, and local supplier registries[1][2]. A procurement user specifies search criteria—for example, "small construction contractors certified as MBE in Maryland"—and the system returns matching businesses with their certification status, contact information, core capabilities, and relevant NAICS codes[1][6].

The second critical feature is **Capability Matching and Opportunity Identification**, which connects certified suppliers to specific subcontracting opportunities within active federal and state contracts[29][35]. When a prime contractor publishes a subcontracting opportunity or when public contract data indicates a new opportunity, the prototype analyzes the required work category (encoded via NAICS code or procurement commodity classification) and automatically identifies all certified minority businesses with matching capabilities in the relevant geography[3][29]. This feature demonstrates the system's ability to surface opportunities that procurement professionals might otherwise discover only through manual searching or networking.

The third essential feature is **Compliance and Reporting Support**, which aggregates contract and payment data to show diversity spend metrics and help organizations meet reporting obligations[19][20][20]. The prototype implements basic spend reporting by aggregating federal contract data, state procurement records, and documented MBE participation, allowing users to visualize spend trends by MBE classification, industry sector, and geography[19][20][20]. State MBE program requirements mandate annual reporting of contract awards and payments to MBE primes and subcontractors[20][20], and the prototype can demonstrate this capability using publicly available procurement data.

The fourth feature is **Good Faith Efforts Documentation**, which helps prime contractors demonstrate that they made documented efforts to include small and disadvantaged businesses on federal contracts[16]. The prototype templates the GFE documentation process using the regulatory framework published in 49 CFR Part 26 and provides structured guidance on required documentation elements—solicitation of certified DBEs through reasonable means, outreach timing and methods, price and capability evaluation considerations, and written confirmations from prospective subcontractors[16]. While the system cannot generate actual business negotiations or confirm genuine engagement efforts, it can structure the documentation process to ensure compliance with regulatory requirements.

### Demonstration Workflow Scenarios

A practical demonstration of the prototype would begin with a **Supplier Discovery Scenario** in which a procurement professional for a state transportation agency needs to source concrete suppliers certified as minority-owned businesses for an upcoming infrastructure project. The user accesses the prototype's search interface and specifies criteria: location (Maryland), NAICS code (concrete suppliers, classified as 32733xx), certification requirement (MBE or DBE), and project timeline (available within six months). The system queries integrated public data sources—SAM.gov entity registrations filtered by socioeconomic status, the Maryland MBE directory, and the DBE directory maintained by the state's Unified Certification Program[1][3][26]. The search returns a list of five certified suppliers meeting the criteria, each with a complete profile showing UEI, certification status, contact information, facility locations, and recent contract history drawn from public FPDS records[26][34]. The prototype demonstrates the ability to quickly surface qualified suppliers who might otherwise require manual directory searching or networking discovery.

A second **Contract Opportunity Identification Scenario** shows how the system connects suppliers to opportunities. A major construction prime contractor wins a $5 million transportation infrastructure contract with a documented 15% small business subcontracting goal[29][41]. The prototype ingests this contract award from SAM.gov and FPDS, identifies the primary work categories (e.g., site preparation, electrical systems, concrete work via NAICS codes), and automatically generates a list of potential MBE and DBE subcontractors with matching capabilities in the relevant geographic region[29]. The system displays each opportunity in a standardized format including the prime contractor contact information, contract description, estimated subcontracting opportunity dollar value, and deadline for submitting proposals. A small minority-owned concrete supplier can receive a notification that matches their core capability to an actual upcoming opportunity, reducing discovery friction and enabling faster business development.

The third **Compliance Reporting Scenario** demonstrates aggregated spend tracking. A corporate procurement organization needs to measure its MBE spending against internal targets of 10% of overall procurement spend. The prototype aggregates procurement records from public sources—federal contract awards to the organization and its documented MBE subcontracting plans, state procurement records showing MBE participation, and local municipal contracts[20][20][34]. The system categorizes spending by MBE classification and industry, generates visual dashboards showing progress toward the 10% target, and identifies specific procurement categories where MBE participation is strong versus areas requiring additional sourcing effort[19]. This demonstrates the system's value in providing transparency and accountability for diversity goals.

The fourth demonstration scenario is **Good Faith Efforts Support for a Subcontracting Goal Shortfall**. A prime contractor on a federal project realizes that its documented MBE subcontracting will reach only 10% of its contract value, falling 5 percentage points short of its contractual goal[16][29]. The prototype provides a structured template for documenting good faith efforts, guiding the contractor through required elements: confirmation that it solicited certified MBEs appropriate to the work scope (including timeframes demonstrating sufficient notice), documentation of pricing and capability comparisons if an MBE subcontractor's quote was rejected, evidence of meaningful negotiations, and written confirmation of solicitation from MBEs who declined to participate[16]. While the system cannot substitute for actual outreach and negotiation, it structures the documentation process to meet regulatory requirements and demonstrates compliance-ready record-keeping.

## Data Architecture and Integration Patterns

### Primary Data Source Integration

The prototype requires systematic integration of multiple heterogeneous public data sources, each with distinct formats, update frequencies, and governance models. SAM.gov serves as the authoritative federal repository for entity registration and socioeconomic status certification[4][26]. Integration with SAM.gov occurs through documented search APIs, which the prototype queries to retrieve entity UEI, business names, socioeconomic status flags (small business, HUBZone, woman-owned, service-disabled veteran-owned, 8(a) program participant), and current registration status[26]. The prototype implements daily scheduled queries to SAM.gov to refresh entity status, recognizing that entities may gain or lose certifications, update ownership structure, or cease operations[26].

The Federal Procurement Data System (FPDS) provides contract award data through standardized reporting mechanisms, with federal agencies required to report all contract actions exceeding the simplified acquisition threshold within specified reporting windows[34]. The prototype can access FPDS data through bulk download files or documented APIs, incorporating contract award records including contract action date, award value, prime contractor UEI, procurement category, agency, and socioeconomic status of the awarded entity. Contract data refresh cycles of weekly or monthly granularity provide sufficient timeliness for prototype demonstration purposes[5][5][34].

State and local MBE directories present the most heterogeneous integration challenge, as each maintains distinct data structures and publication mechanisms. The Maryland MBE directory is published through the Maryland Department of Transportation with structured HTML interfaces and downloadable reports[1]. The City of Baltimore maintains MBE/WBE certification data accessible through multiple formats including CSV, ArcGIS GeoServices REST APIs, and GeoJSON[2]. The City of Chicago publishes quarterly MBE/WBE payment data in standardized formats[2]. The prototype implements adapters for each major directory, with each adapter performing source-specific data extraction, schema mapping to the prototype's canonical data model, and periodic refresh scheduling[1][2].

NAICS classification data provides standardized industry categorization and is maintained by the U.S. Census Bureau[10]. The prototype loads the complete NAICS hierarchy, enabling hierarchical searching (for example, searching for all construction contractors would match all six-digit NAICS codes under the two-digit construction division)[10]. NAICS updates occur periodically on a defined schedule; the prototype incorporates new NAICS editions as they become available[10].

### Data Quality and Reconciliation Challenges

Integrating multiple public data sources creates systematic data quality challenges that directly impact prototype functionality. **Entity Resolution Complexity** represents the most fundamental challenge—a single business may be referenced by different legal names, DBA variations, alternate spellings, and subsidiary structures across different public registries[22][38]. A business registered in SAM.gov as "Johnson Construction Company LLC" may appear in state MBE directories as "Johnson Construction" or "J. Construction." The prototype addresses this through fuzzy matching algorithms that compare legal names with tolerance for abbreviations, punctuation, and spacing variations, supplemented by UEI matching where both sources have provided federal registration[26][38].

**Certification Status Inconsistency** creates operational risks for prototype demonstrations. A business might hold active MBE certification in one state's system while lacking current federal DBE certification, or maintaining federal 8(a) program status while having allowed state certification to lapse[1][3][26]. The prototype manages this by maintaining separate certification records for each program and jurisdiction, displaying the current status from each source with appropriate update timestamps. When a user queries for "MBE certified contractors," the system must clearly distinguish between federal DBE certification, state MBE certification, and other program eligibilities[1][3].

**Data Staleness and Lag** creates persistent brittle areas in the prototype. If a business achieves MBE certification today, it may take weeks or months for that status to appear in public MBE directories and be indexed by the prototype's discovery search[1]. Conversely, if a business loses certification due to ownership changes or no longer meeting size standards, public registries may continue displaying the business as certified until the next update cycle[26]. The prototype documents these temporal realities prominently in user interfaces, displaying "last updated" timestamps and providing clear guidance that search results should be verified against authoritative sources before making procurement decisions.

**Incomplete Ownership Documentation** presents another persistent constraint. Public systems like SAM.gov capture socioeconomic status flags but do not publish detailed ownership structures, personal net worth calculations, or supporting documentation demonstrating disadvantage[1][26]. For prototype demonstration purposes, the system accepts SAM.gov's socioeconomic status flag as sufficient proof of MBE eligibility, but production systems would require access to underlying certification files containing ownership documents, personal financial statements, and management structure details[1][3][25].

**Geographic Data Inconsistency** creates challenges for location-based discovery. A business's headquarters location may be clearly specified, but its actual service areas, licensed jurisdictions, and project locations may extend far beyond the headquarters address[1][2]. The prototype implements geographic queries based on registered business address but includes prominent disclaimers that users must confirm service area capabilities directly with suppliers rather than assuming a contractor can serve all locations within a certain radius[1][2].

## Brittle Areas and Manual Workarounds

### Critical Limitations in Public Data Coverage

The prototype's most fundamental brittle area is the absence of **Real-Time Financial and Stability Information**. Public datasets do not include current balance sheets, cash flow statements, credit scores, or financial distress indicators[4][25][30]. MBE certification programs emphasize financial viability as a criterion for participation—disadvantaged businesses must demonstrate the financial resources to perform contracts competently[1][3][25]. The prototype can surface that a business holds MBE certification but cannot independently verify that the business maintains adequate capitalization, liquidity, or credit quality to execute a contract. Procurement professionals using the prototype must conduct independent financial due diligence before engaging MBE subcontractors. The workaround is to display prominently that users should request audited financial statements, conduct credit checks, and verify bonding capacity through direct supplier outreach rather than relying on system data[25][32].

**Lack of Detailed Subcontractor Performance History** represents another critical constraint. Federal contract reporting requires prime contractors with subcontracting plans to report aggregate achievement toward goals but does not require disclosure of individual subcontractor performance, on-time delivery records, quality metrics, or customer satisfaction[29][34][35]. The Contractor Performance Assessment Reporting System (CPARS) captures federal agency evaluations of prime contractor performance but does not provide visibility into subcontractor performance at lower tiers[13][34]. The prototype can show that a business has appeared as a subcontractor on historical federal contracts (by searching FPDS and subcontracting reports), but cannot quantify that business's on-time delivery performance, defect rates, or customer satisfaction. The practical workaround requires procurement professionals to request performance references directly from prospective suppliers and to check with past prime contractors who have engaged the MBE supplier[29][32].

**Incomplete Capability Assessment** creates friction in the supplier matching feature. NAICS classifications provide a standardized industry category, but they do not capture nuanced capability details such as equipment capabilities, workforce specialization, facility certifications, security clearances, or technical expertise in emerging areas[10][27]. A business classified as a "general construction contractor" under NAICS 236200 might specialize in residential construction, highway work, or healthcare facility construction, with significant variation in relevant experience[10]. The system can match a procurement need to all NAICS codes in the construction category but cannot distinguish between contractors with deep expertise in a specific subsector versus those with generic capabilities. The workaround requires supplementing automated matching with manual capability verification through requests for information, pre-bid meetings, or capability statement review[6][15].

**Absence of Forward-Looking Capacity Information** limits the system's ability to predict supplier availability for upcoming opportunities. Public data shows that a minority-owned electrical contractor completed a $500,000 contract last year, but does not indicate whether that business currently has available capacity, has hired additional staff, acquired new equipment, or has committed to other projects that would constrain availability for new work[29][32]. Procurement professionals using the prototype must directly inquire about supplier capacity and workload before awarding subcontracts. The system can note that a supplier exists and holds relevant certifications, but capacity assessment requires live business development conversations[29][32].

### Data Integration Brittleness

**Cross-System Data Conflicts** create operational challenges requiring human resolution. A business may be registered in SAM.gov as MBE-certified with 51% ownership by a Hispanic American entrepreneur[26], but the state MBE directory may show the same business as not currently certified due to missed recertification deadlines[1]. When searching for "certified MBE contractors," the system faces a genuine ambiguity—does the user want federally certified businesses only, state-certified only, or either/or? The prototype addresses this by displaying results from each certifying authority separately, with clear visual distinctions, allowing users to understand certification status across different programs[1][3][26]. However, this requires user sophistication in understanding the distinction between federal DBE, state MBE, and other programs.

**NAICS Code Misclassification** introduces systematic errors in capability matching. A business may self-classify under NAICS code 541330 (Engineering Services), but the specific work it actually performs might be better classified as 236000 (Construction) or 334511 (Search, Detection, Navigation, Guidance Systems). Public databases rely on the classification provided during business registration and may not be updated if business focus shifts[10]. The prototype implements automated NAICS matching for discovery but must display the underlying NAICS code and advise users that actual business capabilities should be confirmed through direct inquiry rather than relying solely on classification codes[10][27].

**Temporal Misalignment Between Datasets** creates false negatives in opportunity matching. A prime contractor may have published a subcontracting plan in early January, indicating 10% MBE participation goals, but that information might not reach the prototype's data sources until March[29][34]. An MBE contractor searching for opportunities in February would find nothing, even though the opportunity exists. The workaround requires MBE contractors to proactively monitor multiple sources—federal and state contract registries, prime contractor websites, industry publications, and direct prime contractor outreach—rather than relying solely on the prototype's aggregated discovery[16][29].

**Subcontracting Plan Opacity** limits the utility of documented goals. Public subcontracting plans specify percentage goals by category (e.g., 12% to small disadvantaged businesses) but do not detail which specific work or contract line items are designated for subcontracting or the timing of subcontracting decisions[29][35][41]. An MBE contractor may identify that a prime contractor has a 15% MBE goal but cannot determine which contract line items are actually suitable for MBE participation, what price ranges are being negotiated, or what timeline exists for subcontractor selection. The prototype can display the documented goal but must direct users to contact the prime contractor directly to understand actual subcontracting opportunities[29][35].

## Prototype Data Schema and Demonstration Implementation

### Minimal Viable Data Model

The prototype's core data model consists of nine primary entities with carefully selected attributes that can be populated entirely from public sources. The **Business** entity serves as the central anchor and includes fields for universal entity identifier (UEI), legacy DUNS number, business legal name, one or more DBA variations, business structure type (sole proprietorship, partnership, C corporation, S corporation, LLC), year of establishment, and headquarters location (street, city, state, ZIP)[4][26]. The entity includes references to parent company and subsidiary entities where applicable, enabling resolution of ownership structures[4].

The **SocioeconomicStatus** entity captures the MBE eligibility dimensions and links to the Business entity with a many-to-one relationship (a business may hold multiple socioeconomic designations). Each record includes the status type (small business, HUBZone, woman-owned small business, economically disadvantaged, service-disabled veteran-owned, 8(a) program participant), the source system (SAM.gov, state MBE program, federal DBE program), the effective date, the expiration date if applicable, and a confidence score reflecting whether the status is current[1][3][4][26]. This structure allows the prototype to show that a business holds federal small business status but lacks current state MBE certification, providing precise understanding of eligibility across different programs[1][26].

The **Ownership** entity captures ownership structure details and includes the owner's name, ownership percentage, relationship to the business (sole proprietor, general partner, managing member, officer), and the socioeconomic status category of the owner (if the owner is from a presumed disadvantaged group)[1][3][25][30]. Because public data does not include detailed ownership documentation, the Ownership entity is populated only from information disclosed in SAM.gov registrations and capability statements, with explicit documentation that more detailed ownership proof resides in certification files not accessible through public systems[1][26].

The **Capability** entity represents each work category that a business is qualified and willing to perform. Each capability links to a NAICS code (or UNSPSC commodity code for service-based categorization) and includes a descriptive text field, geographic service area, estimated annual capacity (sourced from public registrations where available, otherwise null), and facility certifications or special qualifications[6][10][27]. The capability links to the source system documenting the capability assertion—whether it comes from SAM.gov capability statement, state MBE directory, or inferred from historical contract participation[1][6][15].

The **Certification** entity captures formal MBE, DBE, and other program certifications and includes the certification type, certifying authority, certification date, expiration date, and the public registry URL where the certification can be verified[1][3][26]. Each certification links to both the Business and Socioeconomic Status entities, enabling the system to show that a business's 8(a) program certification is scheduled to expire in 18 months and how that affects its MBE eligibility going forward[3][4].

The **Contract** entity represents awarded federal and state contracts and includes the contract identifier, prime contractor UEI, contracting agency, contract award date, contract award value, contract description, primary NAICS code(s), contract end date, and the source system where the contract data was sourced (SAM.gov, FPDS, state procurement registry)[5][5][34]. The Contract entity does not initially include subcontracting details; that information resides in separate entities[34].

The **SubcontractingPlan** entity links to a Contract and documents the prime contractor's stated subcontracting commitments. The entity includes the goal percentages for each socioeconomic category (small business percentage, disadvantaged business percentage, women-owned percentage, HUBZone percentage), the total dollar value designated for subcontracting, documented outreach methods, and the public source where the plan was obtained[29][35][41].

The **SupplierDirectory** entity maintains records of each business as it appears in individual public registries, enabling tracking of data consistency and currency across sources. Each directory entry links to a Business and includes the directory source name (Maryland MBE, Chicago MBE/WBE, SAM.gov, etc.), the date the information was last updated from that source, a quality confidence score, and a note field capturing any anomalies or conflicts with other sources[1][2][19].

The **DataLineage** entity tracks the provenance and freshness of each data record and includes the source system, the date sourced, the date of last refresh, a refresh frequency indicator (daily, weekly, monthly), and a refresh status (successfully refreshed, failed refresh, manual override)[38][22]. This metadata enables the system to implement intelligent fallback logic—if SAM.gov data could not be refreshed due to system unavailability, the system continues using the most recent previously retrieved data with a flag indicating the information may be stale[22][38].

The **MatchingResult** entity stores the output of supplier-to-opportunity matching algorithms, linking a Business to a Contract and including the match score (0-100 reflecting degree of capability alignment), the matching algorithm version, the date the match was computed, and the match confidence level (high, medium, low)[9]. This persistence enables the system to show users which matches were recent and high-confidence versus older or lower-confidence matches.

### Demo Database Population and Scale

A functional prototype demonstration requires population of representative data demonstrating the prototype's core capabilities without requiring production-scale data volumes. For geographic scope, limiting the initial prototype to Maryland demonstrates core functionality while maintaining manageable dataset size and simplifying data governance. Maryland operates a formal MBE program with approximately 1,100 certified firms as documented in public registries[1]. The prototype would ingest the complete Maryland MBE directory (approximately 1,100 businesses)[1], federal SAM.gov records for entities registered with a Maryland headquarters address (approximately 15,000 businesses, of which roughly 1,500 would hold MBE-eligible socioeconomic status), federal contract awards to Maryland-based contractors for the preceding two years (approximately 8,000 contract actions ranging from $25,000 to several million dollars), and state/local procurement records showing MBE participation (approximately 2,000 contract actions)[34].

For NAICS classification, the prototype would focus on the ten most common NAICS categories in construction and professional services—236200 (General Construction), 236400 (Highway, Road, and Bridge Construction), 334511 (Search, Detection Navigation Guidance), 541330 (Engineering Services), 561399 (Other Miscellaneous Services), 484220 (Specialized Trucking), 238390 (Other Building Installation Contractors), 332312 (Fabricated Structural Metal), 541519 (Other Computer-Related Services), and 811412 (Appliance Repair and Maintenance)—representing approximately 70% of typical MBE contracting activity[10]. This focused NAICS scope enables thorough demonstration of capability matching without requiring complete classification taxonomy[10].

This representative dataset—approximately 16,000 businesses, 10,000 contracts, 1,500 subcontracting plans, and 50,000 individual capability records—provides sufficient volume to demonstrate meaningful search results, capability matching scenarios, and spend reporting, while remaining small enough for local deployment on modest infrastructure[5][19].

## Remaining Brittle Areas and Operational Constraints

### Capability Matching Brittleness

The prototype's supplier discovery and opportunity matching features, while demonstrating useful functionality, contain systematic limitations that cannot be overcome through public data access. **NAICS Code Granularity Limitations** prevent precise capability matching. The NAICS system provides six-digit classification codes, the finest available standardized categorization[10]. A contractor classified under NAICS 236200 (General Construction Contractors) might specialize in highway work, heavy equipment placement, site preparation, or buildings and facilities, representing dramatically different capability profiles[10]. The prototype can match a transportation infrastructure opportunity to all contractors in the construction category but cannot distinguish whether a contractor specializes in that specific work type. The workaround requires procurement professionals to telephone or email contractors to verify specific experience, extending the procurement cycle and introducing manual effort the system ostensibly aimed to eliminate[6][29].

**Absence of Real-Time Availability Information** creates false positive matches. The prototype may identify a certified minority contractor with relevant NAICS classification and recent relevant contract experience, but that contractor may be fully committed to other projects, experiencing staffing shortages, or operating at full capacity[29][32]. The system cannot indicate contractor availability without accessing real-time workload management systems not available in public data[32]. Procurement professionals following up on promising matches discover that contractors cannot take new work, wasting time in the sourcing process. The only practical workaround is direct outreach to every matched contractor to assess actual availability[29][32].

**Unverified Capability Assertions** create quality risks. When a contractor lists a capability in SAM.gov or state MBE directory, the system displays it as a matchable attribute without independent verification[6][26][15]. A contractor might claim expertise in structural steel fabrication (NAICS 332312) based on minimal historical involvement, lacking the workforce depth, equipment capabilities, or quality systems necessary for reliable performance[10][27]. The prototype cannot assess whether a claimed capability reflects genuine competency. The workaround requires procurement professionals to conduct detailed capability assessments through site visits, reference checks, and technical interviews before awarding significant subcontracts[32].

### Financial and Risk Assessment Limitations

**Absence of Financial Viability Assessment** represents a persistent and potentially serious limitation. MBE certification programs emphasize that certified businesses must possess adequate financial resources to perform contracts[1][3][25]. The prototype can confirm that a business holds current MBE certification but cannot independently verify that it maintains adequate working capital, bonding capacity, credit quality, or financial stability to execute contracts[4][25][30]. A certified minority business might exist in the prototype's database while operating with severely constrained cash flow, unresolved tax liens, or legal judgments that would make contract performance highly risky[25][32]. Procurement professionals must request audited financial statements, conduct credit checks through agencies like Dun & Bradstreet, and verify bonding availability through direct inquiry. This creates a significant gap between the system's automated capability discovery and the actual due diligence required before awarding contracts[25][32].

**Missing Supplier Risk Indicators** prevent the prototype from implementing automated risk scoring or supplier segmentation. Machine learning models can effectively classify suppliers into risk categories when provided with historical performance data including payment lateness, quality issues, contract defaults, litigation, and financial deterioration patterns[9][9]. Public data sources do not expose this information[9]. The Federal Procurement Data System does not capture whether a contractor performed late, delivered defective work, or failed to meet requirements[34]. Even where past performance evaluations exist in CPARS, they assess prime contractor performance and do not provide visibility into subcontractor behavior[13][34]. The prototype cannot identify that a certified minority business has a pattern of late deliveries or quality problems[9][9]. Advanced risk management requires consulting private commercial credit bureaus, checking court records for litigation, reviewing BBB ratings, and conducting direct reference checks—all outside the prototype's public data scope[32].

**Unverifiable Ownership and Control** creates potential fraud risk that the system cannot mitigate. Public systems confirm that a business holds MBE status but do not expose detailed ownership documentation, personal financial statements, or evidence of actual management and decision-making control by the owner[1][3][25][26]. In rare cases, businesses obtain MBE certification fraudulently, with ownership structured to appear minority-owned on paper while maintaining control with non-minority individuals[25]. The prototype cannot detect these schemes without access to detailed certification files containing ownership documentation, personal net worth statements, and ongoing ownership verification[25][30].

### Operational and Temporal Brittleness

**Data Refresh Lag Creates Stale Information** that may mislead users. The prototype operates with data refresh cycles measured in weeks or months, while business circumstances change daily[1][20][34]. A contractor certified in February may lose certification in March due to ownership changes, expansion exceeding size standards, or other factors[1][26]. The prototype may continue displaying the business as certified for weeks until the next directory refresh. A business's capacity may become fully committed after winning a major contract, but the prototype would continue recommending it to new procurement opportunities. The system addresses this through prominent display of "last updated" timestamps and explicit disclaimers that users must verify all information before making procurement decisions, but this severely limits the system's utility and creates additional manual verification burden[22][38].

**Geographic Service Area Ambiguity** creates false positive matches. A contractor's registered business address may be in Maryland, leading the prototype to include it in Maryland opportunity searches, but the contractor's actual service area might be limited to specific counties or corridors[1][2]. The prototype cannot determine actual service area without accessing detailed business records not available in public systems[1][2]. Procurement professionals following matches may discover that recommended contractors cannot actually serve the needed geographic areas, again creating wasted outreach effort[1][2].

**Incomplete Subcontracting Plan Data** limits the prototype's ability to match contractors to specific opportunities within large contracts. Federal subcontracting plans specify percentage goals by category but do not detail which specific contract line items, work scopes, or schedule milestones are designated for subcontracting[29][35][41]. A contractor may identify a prime contractor's 12% MBE subcontracting goal but cannot determine whether electrical work, structural work, or site preparation is actually available for subcontracting or the timing of subcontractor selection[29][35]. The only way to obtain this information is to contact the prime contractor directly, circumventing the prototype's aggregated discovery function[29][35].

## Production System Upgrade Pathways and Interim Workarounds

### Enhanced Data Access for Production Implementation

Transitioning from prototype to production system requires access to additional data sources and business records currently unavailable in public systems. **Direct integration with certification authorities** would enable access to detailed ownership documentation, personal financial statements, and ongoing verification records maintained by the SBA, state MBE agencies, and federal DBE programs[1][3][25]. These organizations maintain comprehensive application files including articles of incorporation, EINs, personal tax returns, ownership percentages, and management documentation that directly address the prototype's brittleness around ownership verification[1][3][25]. Integration agreements with these agencies would require formal data-sharing arrangements but would dramatically improve confidence in MBE eligibility validation[1][25].

**Access to supplier financial data** through commercial providers would enable supplier risk scoring and financial viability assessment. Dun & Bradstreet, Experian, and other commercial credit bureaus maintain detailed financial profiles, payment histories, credit scores, and legal judgment information that would enable the system to assess financial stability and risk[25][32]. Implementing this capability would require implementing data procurement agreements with these third-party providers and incorporating their data into the prototype's risk assessment algorithms[25][32].

**Integration with contractor performance systems** would enable assessment of actual delivery quality and schedule performance. The Contractor Performance Assessment Reporting System (CPARS) contains federal agency evaluations of contractor performance, and some large prime contractors maintain internal performance management systems tracking subcontractor metrics[13][34]. Access to this performance history would transform the prototype from confirming capability existence to actually validating capability quality and execution reliability[13][34][32].

**Direct API integration with large prime contractors' procurement systems** would provide real-time visibility into active subcontracting opportunities, current capacity commitments, and timeline for subcontractor decisions[29]. Rather than the prototype inferring opportunities from published subcontracting plans, direct prime contractor system integration would enable the system to show specific open opportunities, required work scopes, timeline expectations, and contact information for procurement managers[29]. This would dramatically accelerate the sourcing cycle and reduce manual outreach burden.

### Interim Workarounds for Production Deployment

While pursuing more comprehensive data access, a production system can deploy interim workarounds to address the prototype's most critical brittleness. **Direct Contractor Survey and Capacity Reporting** processes can solicit regular updates from registered minority contractors regarding current capabilities, capacity status, facility certifications, and service area constraints[32]. Rather than inferring these characteristics from public data, the system can explicitly ask contractors to submit quarterly updates through a simple online form[32]. This transforms passive public data consumption to active crowd-sourced data collection, substantially improving data currency and accuracy[32].

**Supplier Capability Verification Programs** can establish structured processes for validating claimed capabilities before including contractors in opportunity matching. The system can implement a certification process where contractors undergo technical interviews, facility inspections, workforce assessments, and reference verification by procurement professionals[32]. Contractors passing verification receive a "verified capability" badge within the system, indicating that their claimed capabilities have been independently confirmed[32]. This introduces a quality dimension that public data sources cannot provide[32].

**Financial Viability Self-Assessment Questionnaires** can require contractors to periodically confirm key financial indicators—current number of employees, estimated annual revenue, recent credit inquiries, bonding availability—without requiring access to full financial records[25][32]. While this does not provide the same certainty as audited financial statements, it enables contractors to self-flag financial constraints or recent changes that should affect procurement decisions[25][32].

**Direct Prime Contractor Integration** for subcontracting opportunity posting can be implemented as a complementary data source to the prototype's public data searches. Prime contractors can be invited to post specific subcontracting opportunities in the system, with structured forms capturing work scope, timeline, estimated value, and required capabilities[29]. This creates a curated set of high-quality opportunities complementing the prototype's automated matching derived from public subcontracting plans[29]. The system would display both public source opportunities (from inferred subcontracting plans) and direct posted opportunities, clearly distinguishing data provenance[29].

## Conclusion: Prototype Viability and Recommendations

### Overall Assessment of Feasibility

The analysis demonstrates that a useful Minority Business Enterprise prototype can be constructed using exclusively public data sources, demonstrating meaningful functionality across supplier discovery, opportunity matching, and compliance reporting dimensions[1][3][5][19]. The prototype's core database can be populated from SAM.gov registrations, federal contract data, state and local MBE directories, and NAICS classifications—all freely accessible without proprietary data agreements[1][4][26][34]. The demo workflow scenarios show how procurement professionals can use the prototype to discover certified minority suppliers more efficiently than manual searching, connect suppliers to subcontracting opportunities, and aggregate spend reporting for diversity goal tracking[1][19][29].

However, the prototype's utility is fundamentally constrained by limitations in public data coverage. The system can confirm that a business holds MBE certification and holds relevant industry experience, but cannot independently verify financial stability, assess actual capability quality, confirm real-time availability, or predict execution reliability[1][4][25][29][32]. These gaps force procurement professionals to conduct extensive manual due diligence beyond what the prototype can automate. The system accelerates certain aspects of the sourcing process—particularly supplier discovery through aggregated search—but cannot eliminate the fundamental business development work required to assess supplier suitability for specific contracts[29][32].

For prototype demonstration and market validation purposes, these constraints are acceptable. The prototype successfully demonstrates core value propositions and enables assessment of market demand. For users accepting the stated limitations and conducting appropriate due diligence, the system provides genuine utility. Organizations seeking automated risk assessment, real-time capacity matching, or comprehensive supplier vetting would require production-grade systems with access to proprietary supplier data and direct prime contractor integration[25][29][32].

### Strategic Recommendations for Implementation

**Begin with focused geographic scope** to validate core functionality before attempting national scale. Piloting the prototype in Maryland, leveraging the state's well-established MBE program infrastructure and published directories, enables demonstration with manageable dataset scope while ensuring high-quality data and domain expertise support[1]. Maryland's combination of state-level MBE program, federal DBE certification oversight, and multiple local MBE registries provides ideal complexity for prototype validation[1].

**Invest early in data quality infrastructure** including entity resolution, duplicate detection, and source conflict management[22][38]. The prototype's utility depends entirely on data quality, and implementing robust data governance processes from inception prevents downstream problems. Define clear rules for handling conflicts across data sources—for example, always preferring SAM.gov as the authoritative source for federal certification status—and document these decisions transparently[22][38].

**Plan explicit upgrade pathways to commercial and restricted data** that would be implemented in production systems[25][29][32]. Document which brittleness areas would be addressed through access to financial data, performance history, and direct prime contractor integration. Use this mapping to identify priority data access agreements to pursue as the prototype transitions toward production[25][29][32].

**Implement transparent data lineage and currency indicators** throughout the user interface to set appropriate expectations about data reliability[22][38]. Display "last updated" timestamps, show which data source provided each field, and provide clear disclaimers that all information should be verified before making procurement decisions[22][38]. This builds user trust and prevents over-reliance on system data[22][38].

**Establish direct relationships with state MBE agencies and federal DBE programs** early in development to understand data governance requirements, build support for future data sharing, and coordinate on future integration opportunities[1][3]. These relationships become critical when transitioning from prototype to production systems requiring access to more detailed certification and verification data[1][3][25].

**Design the prototype with modular data adapters** enabling quick addition of new geographic jurisdictions and data sources[1][2]. As additional state and local MBE directories are integrated, the system should automatically scale to provide nationwide coverage without requiring architectural redesign. This architecture enables rapid geographic expansion once core functionality is validated[1][2].

### Conclusion

The construction of a useful MBE prototype using public data is feasible and can provide genuine value for supplier discovery and opportunity matching while clearly documenting its limitations. The prototype successfully demonstrates core capabilities and enables market validation. Procurement professionals using the system must understand that it accelerates certain aspects of supplier sourcing but does not eliminate the detailed due diligence required for safe contracting decisions. With transparent communication about data limitations and clear upgrade pathways toward production-grade systems with enhanced data access, a public-data-driven prototype can serve as a compelling proof of concept while establishing requirements for more comprehensive future systems.
