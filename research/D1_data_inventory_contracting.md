# Comprehensive Source Inventory for Richmond Minority-Business Contracting

This report provides a detailed inventory of sources and resources relevant to minority-business contracting opportunities in Richmond, Virginia, specifically designed to support procurement research, data analysis, and hackathon initiatives. The inventory encompasses official procurement portals, open data resources, vendor registration systems, certification directories, and supporting materials from both municipal and state authorities. Each source has been catalogued with accessibility information, update patterns, and potential applications for data-driven projects focused on supplier diversity and contracting equity.

## The Richmond Procurement Ecosystem Overview

The City of Richmond maintains a multifaceted procurement infrastructure designed to provide transparency, accessibility, and opportunity for diverse suppliers. The ecosystem encompasses multiple interconnected platforms and resources that serve different functions within the broader contracting system. Understanding this ecosystem is essential for anyone seeking to analyze, understand, or participate in Richmond's procurement activities. The City of Richmond's commitment to transparency and minority-business participation is reflected through the integration of several digital systems, each serving specific purposes within the procurement workflow. The primary platforms include official procurement portals managed by the Department of Procurement Services, open data repositories that provide historical contract information, vendor registration systems that maintain supplier databases, and specialized certification directories for minority and small businesses. These systems are complemented by state-level resources available through Virginia's centralized procurement platforms and the Department of Small Business and Supplier Diversity.

The interconnectedness of these platforms creates a robust infrastructure where suppliers can discover opportunities, register their businesses, track procurement activities, and access support services. However, the multiplicity of systems also presents challenges for data consolidation and analysis. Researchers and analysts seeking comprehensive views of Richmond's contracting patterns must often combine data from multiple sources. This inventory provides clear guidance on how to navigate these systems and identify which sources are most useful for specific research objectives or hackathon applications. The update frequencies vary across platforms, with some providing real-time information while others are updated on monthly or quarterly schedules. Understanding these temporal patterns is crucial for time-sensitive analyses of procurement trends and opportunities.

## City Procurement Portals and Official Channels

The City of Richmond operates several official channels through which procurement activities are managed, communicated, and executed. The primary portal serving suppliers is the iSupplier system, accessed through the Supplier Portal interface, which functions as the central registration point for all businesses seeking to conduct transactions with the city. This portal is managed by the Department of Procurement Services and serves as the gateway through which suppliers provide required documentation and maintain their business profiles. The registration process through iSupplier requires completion of IRS W-9 forms, ACH/EDI Payment Agreement forms, and supporting banking documentation. Once registered, suppliers gain access to procurement opportunities, account information, and payment status tracking. The approval process for new registrations typically completes within three to five business days if all documentation is properly provided.

The City has also transitioned to OpenGov Procurement as its primary platform for managing formal solicitations, particularly Invitations for Bid and Requests for Proposals. This transition represents a modernization of Richmond's procurement processes, replacing legacy systems reliant on paper documents and spreadsheets. OpenGov provides enhanced transparency by making procurement documents, bidder information, and bid amounts publicly accessible through a unified platform. The platform enables open communication between city procurement analysts and potential bidders, with all questions and clarifications documented within the system. Electronic submission of bids and proposals through OpenGov has streamlined the submission process and reduced administrative burdens for both the city and suppliers.

## Open Data Resources and Transparency Dashboards

Richmond's commitment to procurement transparency is exemplified through its comprehensive open data portal, which provides unprecedented access to historical and current contract information. The City Contracts dataset, accessible through the Open Data Portal, contains information on both active and inactive contracts covering the past five years. This dataset is updated monthly and provides granular detail including agency names, contract numbers, dollar amounts, supplier names, procurement types or methods, contract descriptions, purchase categories, and effective and end dates. The Socrata-based platform upon which this data is hosted provides programmatic access through both API and OData endpoints, enabling researchers and developers to extract and analyze contract data systematically. The data can be accessed directly through the web interface or downloaded in multiple formats compatible with analytical tools such as Excel and Tableau.

The Open Data Portal serves a dual purpose in Richmond's procurement transparency initiatives. First, it provides the general public, including taxpayers and residents, with access to information about how city resources are allocated through contracting activities. Second, it creates opportunities for data-driven analysis and research that can identify trends, patterns, and potential improvements in procurement practices. The portal's data governance structure ensures that sensitive information such as proprietary manufacturing processes and trade secrets, when properly declared by submitting parties, receives appropriate protection under Virginia law. However, the vast majority of procurement data is made fully public, consistent with the city's transparency objectives.

The visualization capabilities embedded within the Open Data Portal allow users to create custom views, filters, and analyses without requiring advanced technical skills. Users can sort by agency, contract value ranges, supplier characteristics, or procurement methods to identify specific contracts of interest. The ability to access historical data spanning multiple fiscal years enables longitudinal analysis of contracting patterns, supplier relationships, and spending trends across city departments. For researchers interested in minority business utilization, the portal provides the means to analyze contract awards by supplier type, though additional data from the Office of Minority Business Development is typically required to correlate suppliers with their MBE/ESB certification status.

## Vendor Registration and Supplier Diversity Systems

The City of Richmond operates a specialized supplier diversity management system designed specifically to support minority business enterprises and emerging small businesses in accessing city contracting opportunities. This system, accessible at richmondombd.diversitycompliance.com, maintains searchable directories of certified MBEs and ESBs, provides registration and recertification portals, and delivers information about available support services. The system operates under the Office of Minority Business Development (OMBD), located at 1500 East Main Street, 5th Floor, Richmond, VA 23219, with contact information available at (804) 646-5947.

The supplier diversity management system functions as both a business registration platform and a vendor directory. Firms interested in participating in city contracts can register within the system to be included in the OMBD vendor directory, which is available for search by procurement professionals. Certification through the OMBD requires meeting specific criteria including ownership and control requirements, income thresholds, and location requirements within the City of Richmond Enterprise Zone. The registration process for new vendors typically requires 24 to 48 hours if all required information is provided, while formal certification can be completed within 30 days. The system maintains records for both MBE registration and ESB certification, with different eligibility requirements and benefits associated with each designation.

An Emerging Small Business (ESB) in Richmond's system is defined as a business certified by OMBD for a period of seven years or less, with annual gross receipts of $500,000 or less for construction businesses or $250,000 or less for non-construction businesses, fewer than ten employees, not a subsidiary of another business, principal place of business within the City of Richmond Enterprise Zone, possessing a City business license, and paying required personal property, real estate, and business taxes. A Minority Business Enterprise (MBE) is defined as a business at least 51 percent of which is owned and controlled by individuals who are U.S. citizens or legal resident aliens, with both management and daily business operations controlled by these individuals.

## Certification and Directory Resources

Beyond the local City of Richmond systems, several state and federal resources provide complementary certification and directory services relevant to minority and small business contracting. The Virginia Department of Small Business and Supplier Diversity operates two primary certification programs: the Small, Women-owned, and Minority-owned Business (SWaM) program and the Disadvantaged Business Enterprise (DBE) program. These state-level certifications carry recognition and eligibility implications for state procurement contracts and are often reciprocally recognized in municipal procurement processes. The SWaM program covers small businesses, women-owned businesses, minority-owned businesses, micro businesses, and service-disabled veteran-owned businesses. The initial review period for both applications and recertifications is approximately 60 business days from receipt of a completed application and all required supporting documentation.

The Virginia Unified Certification Program includes two certifying agencies: the Department of Small Business and Supplier Diversity (DSBSD) and the Metropolitan Washington Airports Authority (MWAA). Federal DBE certification by either agency is fully accepted throughout Virginia. Firms seeking state contracts that do not receive federal transportation funds typically require SWaM certification rather than DBE certification. The distinctions between these programs, while subtle, carry important implications for eligibility and contract access. Directory listings on the state systems provide searchable access to certified businesses, with company names, contact information, physical addresses, phone numbers, websites, business types, and commodity codes. This information is compiled into publicly accessible directories that procurement professionals use to identify potential contractors and suppliers.

The Virginia Business Opportunities (VBO) system, accessed through eVA.virginia.gov, serves as the central marketplace for Virginia's procurement activities. This system lists sealed and unsealed solicitations including Quick Quotes, Invitations for Bid, and Requests for Proposals from all participating state agencies and local governments. Historic award information and bid tabulations are maintained within the system, providing researchers with detailed records of past procurement outcomes. The eVA system is freely accessible for viewing opportunities, with free registration available for vendors seeking to receive notifications and submit bids electronically.

## Procurement Documents and Forms

The City of Richmond maintains a comprehensive collection of procurement documents, policies, and standardized forms that guide the procurement process and establish requirements for all parties. These documents are essential references for understanding procurement procedures, compliance requirements, and contract administration processes. The Purchase Order Terms and Conditions document establishes the general contractual framework for all purchase orders issued by the city and defines payment terms, delivery requirements, compliance obligations, and dispute resolution procedures. The document specifies that payment by the City is due no later than 45 days after receipt of goods or services or receipt of a proper invoice, whichever is later. Unless otherwise stated in specific orders, all prices are net F.O.B. Destination with transportation charges prepaid.

The MBE/ESB Participation Commitment Form (Form MBE-2) is used by contractors to document their commitments to utilize minority and small businesses in fulfilling city contracts. This form requires specification of the MBE/ESB firm names, their scope of work, dollar amounts, participation types (subcontracting, supply purchases, joint venture, or other credibly viable methods), and ownership classifications. The form serves as both a commitment mechanism and a documentation tool for tracking MBE/ESB utilization and ensuring accountability in meeting established participation goals. The form defines the specific types of MBE/ESB participation that are recognized, including first-tier, second-tier, and third-tier subcontracting, supply purchases at 100 percent credit value, joint ventures with specified minimum point allocations, mentor-protégé arrangements, and blanket bonding arrangements.

The Forecast Calendar for Anticipated Solicitations provides forward-looking information about upcoming procurement projects across city departments. The most recent forecast calendar, updated in February 2026, lists anticipated solicitations organized by anticipated timeframe, estimated project value, project title, and project summary. The calendar covers multiple fiscal quarters and extends visibility into upcoming procurement opportunities. Projects are categorized by estimated value from Small (under $1 million) through XXL (over $15 million). This forward-looking information is valuable for suppliers seeking to prepare bid responses, schedule resources, and identify strategic opportunities aligned with their business capabilities.

## Contracting Notices and Solicitation Sources

The City of Richmond publishes contracting notices and solicitations through multiple channels to ensure maximum visibility and access for potential bidders. Primary among these are the Invitation for Bid (IFB) notices published on the city's procurement services website, which provide formal notice of competitive bidding opportunities. All bids and proposals are submitted to the Department of Procurement Services office at 900 East Broad Street, Room 1104, Richmond, Virginia 23219. The website provides downloadable solicitation documents in PDF format, accessible through free Adobe Acrobat Reader, allowing potential bidders to review specifications and requirements before determining whether to submit responses.

In addition to the city's direct publication channels, solicitation information is distributed through third-party procurement notice services such as PlanetBids. The PlanetBids vendor portal maintains a searchable database of Richmond procurement opportunities, with automatic filtering and notification capabilities. Bid opportunities are listed with posting dates, and the system allows vendors to create customized searches to identify contracts matching their business capabilities. Recent bid opportunities listed on PlanetBids include items such as "Richmond Art and Lighting Safety and Accessibility Improvements," "Port of Richmond Economic Impact Study," and various consulting services. The PlanetBids platform complements the city's primary procurement systems by providing an aggregated view of opportunities from multiple jurisdictions.

Public notice columns in local publications such as the Richmond Times-Dispatch and Richmond.com provide additional channels through which formal procurement notices are published. These traditional publication channels ensure that vendors without internet access or digital monitoring capabilities remain informed of procurement opportunities. The notice columns publish invitation to bid announcements, public hearing notices, and contract award announcements. This multi-channel publication approach reflects the city's commitment to ensuring that procurement opportunities are accessible to the broadest possible supplier base.

## Minority Business Support Services and Resources

The City of Richmond's Office of Minority Business Development provides comprehensive support services designed to strengthen minority-owned and emerging small businesses. These services include business technical assistance, business start-up and growth development, one-on-one business counseling, business assessments, business development workshops and series, registration and certification assistance, and financial options counseling and referrals. All of these services are provided at no cost to registered minority businesses. The OMBD operates with a vision of fostering greater economic and social equity within the community by ensuring parity in how the City of Richmond allocates taxpayer dollars and user fees for procurement of goods and services.

The OMBD's mission explicitly encompasses advancing opportunities for Minority Business Enterprises, Emerging Small Businesses, and Disadvantaged Business Enterprises to fully participate in contracting opportunities available within the City of Richmond. The office's core values include integrity and transparency, dedication to community, quality service, innovation and creativity, and continuous improvement. The OMBD actively works toward embedding the utilization and recognition of minority, disadvantaged, and emerging business enterprises as standard practice in Richmond's procurement activities. The office stands as one of the city's strongest advocates for increasing contract opportunities for minority-owned businesses and emerging small businesses across all sectors.

The City of Richmond's minority business commitment is formalized in procurement policy, which explicitly states that the city is strongly committed to utilizing minority individuals and firms for all goods and services, including construction, while providing all parties regardless of race or nationality with equal access to the procurement process. This commitment is reflected in procurement documentation, including MBE/ESB participation goals established for specific contracts and inclusive evaluation criteria that account for minority business participation in scoring and award decisions. The OMBD provides registration assistance, helping prospective vendors navigate the application process and ensuring that businesses meet all eligibility requirements before formal certification.

## Procurement Policy and Governance Documents

The City of Richmond's procurement activities are governed by a comprehensive policy framework established through municipal code and departmental procedures. Chapter 21 of the Richmond City Code addresses public procurement requirements and procedures, providing the legal foundation for all contracting activities. The Virginia Public Procurement Act establishes state-level requirements that apply to municipal procurement, and the Code of Virginia § 2.2-4301 provides additional statutory authority and requirements. These legal foundations ensure that Richmond's procurement processes comply with both local and state legal requirements while serving the city's policy objectives.

City Auditor reports provide additional insights into procurement operations and effectiveness. The December 2, 2022 Procurement Services Audit (City Auditor's Report #2023-07) examined the Department of Procurement Services' procedural processes during the 12 months ended December 31, 2021, focusing on Invitations for Bid, Requests for Proposals, and contract renewals. The audit noted that average execution times for contracts were 223 calendar days for Invitation to Bid processes and 308 calendar days for Request for Proposals processes. The audit identified process improvements that have been implemented, including the adoption of the OpenGov Procurement software, which represents a modernization from legacy paper-based and spreadsheet-dependent systems. The audit report provides detailed observations about compliance with DPS Policy Numbers 46 and 60, concerning competitive negotiated procurements and competitive sealed bidding.

The OMBD Policy and Procedures Section 6 provides detailed guidance on MBE/ESB participation mechanisms, including provisions for first-tier, second-tier, and third-tier subcontracting, supply purchases, joint ventures, mentor-protégé arrangements, and other credibly viable methods. These policies establish how MBE/ESB credits are calculated and tracked, ensuring accountability and consistency in meeting participation goals. The policies also establish that registered MBE firms not yet certified have 180 days to obtain certification or face loss of credit toward participation goals.

## Data Accessibility and Integration Considerations

Researchers and hackathon participants seeking to work with Richmond procurement data should be aware of several factors affecting data accessibility and integration. While the City Contracts Open Data Portal provides monthly updated contract information in machine-readable formats, the data requires correlation with additional sources to fully identify MBE/ESB status, procurement methods, and departmental context. The OMBD supplier directory provides certified MBE/ESB information, but this system may not be fully machine-readable or provide comprehensive historical data. Integration of data from multiple systems may require significant data cleaning and reconciliation efforts to ensure accurate analysis.

The transition to OpenGov Procurement represents a significant modernization of Richmond's data infrastructure, enabling real-time tracking of solicitation activities and bid submissions. However, this system's historical data may not extend back as far as the Open Data Portal's contract database. Researchers conducting longitudinal analysis must account for these system transitions and any changes in data capture or categorization that may have accompanied them. The monthly update pattern for the City Contracts dataset ensures that recent procurement activities are captured, though there may be modest lag between contract execution and data publication. The real-time or near-real-time nature of the OpenGov platform provides opportunities for monitoring current procurement activities but may not be suitable for comprehensive historical analysis without additional archiving.

Several of the key resources identified in this inventory require some form of registration or direct engagement to access specific information. The iSupplier system requires vendor registration to maintain business profiles and access account information. The OMBD supplier diversity management system requires separate registration for formal certification applications, though the public directories can be searched without registration. The eVA system offers free registration for vendors seeking automated notifications. These registration requirements, while generally low-barrier and free, create access considerations for fully automated data collection or analysis.

## Historical Context and Evolution of Richmond's Procurement Systems

Understanding the historical development of Richmond's procurement systems and policies provides important context for interpreting current data and systems. The significant historical incident referenced in procurement literature is the Richmond v. J.A. Croson Co. Supreme Court case from the 1980s, which examined the legality of Richmond's race-based set-aside program for construction contracts. In the 1980s, Richmond had implemented a 30 percent set-aside requirement for minority-owned subcontractors on construction contracts, a response to findings that while the city was 50 percent Black, only 0.67 percent of prime construction contracts had been awarded to minority businesses. When J.A. Croson Co. applied for a waiver from this requirement, asserting inability to find qualified minority subcontractors, the city rejected the application, resulting in Croson's loss of the contract. The Supreme Court's decision in this case influenced the evolution of procurement set-asides nationwide, leading to a shift from explicit racial classifications toward race-neutral "goals" rather than "set-asides." This legal evolution explains Richmond's current use of MBE/ESB participation goals in its procurement framework.

Contemporary procurement policy evolution has seen increasing emphasis on transparency, data accessibility, and systematic goal-setting. The Biden administration's 2021 reforms to federal contracting allocations committed to increasing the socially and economically disadvantaged business goal to 15 percent by 2025, representing an additional $100 billion allocation to SDBs over five years. These reforms emphasize greater transparency regarding race and ethnicity of business owners and adoption of management practices fostering accountability in meeting goals. States including Maryland, which has established a 29 percent goal for minority and women-owned businesses (though actual awards have consistently underperformed this goal), provide examples of ambitious goal-setting within the constraints of post-Croson legal frameworks.

## Recommendations for Hackathon Participants and Data Users

For hackathon participants seeking to work with Richmond's procurement and minority-business contracting data, several recommendations emerge from this inventory. First, begin with the City Contracts Open Data Portal for comprehensive historical contract data, supplemented by the OMBD supplier directories for identification of MBE/ESB certified businesses. Second, utilize the OpenGov platform for tracking current solicitations and real-time procurement activity. Third, integrate data from multiple sources to create comprehensive datasets suitable for analysis, recognizing that no single system provides all necessary information for complete procurement analysis. Fourth, consult the forecast calendar to understand upcoming procurement trends and identify potential future opportunities. Fifth, review relevant policy documents and audit reports to understand the governance framework and historical context affecting current practices.

Data quality considerations should include attention to missing or incomplete information in historical datasets, the potential for business name variations across different registration systems, and the lag between contract execution and data publication. Researchers should establish clear definitions for key terms such as MBE status, ESB certification status, and participation types. Consideration should be given to how businesses are categorized by industry, since this categorization affects both procurement analysis and policy implications. The geographic components of eligibility requirements, particularly the Enterprise Zone requirements for ESB status, warrant careful attention when analyzing supplier participation patterns.

Potential hackathon applications emerging from this inventory include developing improved data visualization tools for procurement transparency, creating automated notifications for procurement opportunities matching specific supplier capabilities, building predictive models for contract awards based on historical bidding patterns, analyzing spending patterns to identify potential procurement inefficiencies, tracking minority-business participation trends over time, creating dashboards comparing Richmond's procurement metrics against peer cities, developing tools to help small businesses identify relevant procurement opportunities, and building systems to monitor compliance with MBE/ESB participation goals.

The City of Richmond maintains a sophisticated, multi-platform procurement infrastructure providing unprecedented transparency and access to contracting information. The inventory of sources presented above encompasses official city procurement systems, open data resources, vendor registration platforms, certification directories, and supporting services spanning local and state jurisdictions. Each source contributes unique value to the procurement ecosystem while creating opportunities for data-driven analysis, research, and innovation.

The integration of historical contract databases, real-time procurement platforms, and comprehensive supplier directories creates a rich environment for understanding procurement patterns, supplier diversity progress, and opportunities for improvement. The city's commitment to transparency and minority-business participation is evidenced through both formal policy commitments and the investment in digital infrastructure enabling public access to contracting information. The transition to modern procurement software platforms like OpenGov represents ongoing evolution toward more efficient, transparent, and equitable procurement practices.

For researchers, analysts, and hackathon participants seeking to engage with Richmond's procurement data, this inventory provides a roadmap of available resources, their characteristics, access patterns, and potential applications. The complementary nature of different sources—historical data from open repositories, current opportunities from active procurement platforms, supplier information from registration systems, and forward-looking information from planning calendars—enables comprehensive analysis of Richmond's procurement ecosystem. By thoughtfully leveraging these resources, stakeholders can contribute to greater transparency, efficiency, and equity in how the City of Richmond allocates resources through contracting with the private and nonprofit sectors.
