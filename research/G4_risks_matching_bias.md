# Bias Risks and Ethics in Matching and Recommendation Systems: A Comprehensive Evaluation of Algorithmic Fairness Challenges

Matching and recommendation systems have become fundamental infrastructure in digital economies, yet they present profound risks for introducing and amplifying bias at scale. This report evaluates the theoretical and empirical risks that emerge when these systems infer "fit" between users and items, jobs and candidates, or other entities based on incomplete data and historical patterns that themselves may encode systemic inequalities. The central challenge lies in the fact that algorithmic matching systems are trained on historical data that reflects past discrimination, underrepresentation of minority groups, and incomplete information about actual qualifications and preferences. When these systems then use sophisticated machine learning techniques to identify similarities and make recommendations, they inadvertently perpetuate and often amplify the biases present in their training data. This report examines the multifaceted pathways through which bias enters matching systems, evaluates existing mechanisms for detecting these biases, and provides evidence-based recommendations for implementing fairness checks and transparency measures that can help mitigate these harms.

## Historical and Structural Biases in Training Data

The foundation of bias in matching and recommendation systems begins with the data used to train them. Historical data bias occurs when datasets reflect past norms and practices rather than current reality or what we might consider fair outcomes[7]. This distinction is particularly important because organizations often justify the use of historical data by claiming it represents "what worked" in the past, without acknowledging that past hiring, lending, or matching decisions may have been systematically unfair. In recruitment contexts, for example, historical hiring data may underrepresent women in leadership roles, technology positions, and senior pay bands not because women are less qualified, but because past hiring decisions were influenced by conscious or unconscious discrimination[7]. When a machine learning model trained on this historical data learns patterns from successful hires, it essentially learns to perpetuate the same discrimination that produced those historical outcomes.

The implications of historical data bias extend across multiple dimensions of the matching problem. Career breaks and flexible working patterns are often misinterpreted as indicators of risk or lower potential, yet these patterns disproportionately affect women who have taken maternity leave or adjusted their work schedules[7]. Promotion and performance data can reflect access to opportunity rather than actual capability, meaning that groups who were historically denied mentorship, challenging assignments, or sponsorship will appear less accomplished in the historical record[7]. A crucial insight from the research is that these biases are not merely descriptive of the past but become prescriptive for the future when encoded into algorithmic systems. The systems then recommend or match based on these flawed historical patterns, ensuring that individuals from groups that faced historical discrimination continue to face algorithmic discrimination even when explicit discrimination policies have been eliminated.

This problem becomes especially acute in job recommendation systems. Research examining China's four largest job boards found that when identical male and female worker profiles sought jobs in the same industry-occupation cell, jobs recommended to women paid 0.2 percent less, requested 0.9 percent less experience, came from smaller firms, and contained 0.07 standard deviations more stereotypically female content such as requests for patience, carefulness, and beauty[19]. The dominant driver of these gender gaps was content-based matching between posted job ads and the declared gender in workers' resumes, demonstrating that the problem lies not with the workers' qualifications but with how recommendation algorithms process information about gender in combination with job descriptions[19]. This research reveals that even when we might think an algorithm is making purely content-based decisions, the presence of protected characteristics in the data (in this case, gender) fundamentally shapes matching outcomes.

## Proxy Variables and Structural Discrimination

One of the most insidious forms of bias in matching systems occurs through proxy variables, which are ostensibly neutral features that are correlated with protected characteristics such as race, gender, or national origin[8]. The challenge with proxy variables is that they can introduce discrimination while maintaining what appears to be algorithmic neutrality. An algorithm that never explicitly considers race might still discriminate on the basis of race if it uses zip codes as a feature, because zip codes are often correlated with racial demographics due to residential segregation[20]. Similarly, a system might use educational credentials as a feature, but if historical barriers prevented certain groups from accessing education, those credentials become a proxy for the discrimination that occurred in the educational system.

The formal analysis of proxy discrimination has become increasingly sophisticated. Researchers have developed frameworks using abductive explanations to identify which features act as unjustified proxies for protected attributes by revealing hidden structural biases[8]. In this framework, a proxy variable is defined as a non-admissible attribute that is functionally dependent on an unobserved protected attribute[8]. The critical distinction is between proxies that are causally connected to protected attributes versus those that merely correlate with them. A feature that is causally influenced by a protected attribute represents a deeper form of discrimination because it channels the effect of the protected attribute through what appears to be an objective measure.

Real-world examples illustrate how proxy discrimination operates in practice. In healthcare, underrepresentation of minority groups in training data can skew predictive AI algorithms, with computer-aided diagnosis systems returning lower accuracy results for Black patients than white patients[3]. In a famous case, an algorithm used for predicting healthcare resource needs systematically predicted lower resource needs for Black patients compared to white patients despite equivalent risk levels, because the algorithm used healthcare cost consumption as a proxy variable, and Black patients historically received less spending due to discrimination in healthcare systems[34]. The algorithm had learned not to predict higher resource needs for Black patients because the proxy variable (cost) was lower, thus perpetuating and institutionalizing historical inequities. This example demonstrates that even when an algorithm performs well on accuracy metrics, it can simultaneously encode and amplify historical discrimination.

In facial recognition systems, proxy discrimination takes a different form. Research from MIT found that some general-purpose commercial facial recognition systems were unable to recognize darker-skinned individuals, with even worse performance for darker-skinned women[3]. The underlying cause was training data that misrepresented real demographics, skewing the results. In this case, the demographic composition of the training data itself acts as a proxy for the protected characteristic of race; by training on predominantly light-skinned individuals, the system implicitly learned to be worse at recognizing dark-skinned individuals. The "neutral" feature of image data becomes a proxy for race through the biased composition of the training dataset.

## Feedback Loops and Bias Amplification

Matching and recommendation systems are not static systems that make predictions based on historical data and then stop. Rather, they operate within feedback loops where algorithmic recommendations influence user behavior, which generates new data that retrains the system, creating a cycle that can amplify biases over time[11]. Understanding these feedback loops is essential because they explain how modest biases in an initial algorithm can compound into severe disparities. The feedback loop operates in three stages: first, users selectively engage with recommended items based on various factors including confirmation bias and homophily; second, the recommender observes this engagement and adjusts what it shows in future iterations; third, the changed recommendations further shape user behavior and preferences[6].

A critical insight from recent research is that this feedback loop is generally stronger for users belonging to minority groups[11]. When an algorithm recommends fewer items relevant to minority users' preferences because the historical data underrepresented those preferences, minority users engage less with those recommendations. The algorithm then interprets this lower engagement as a signal that minority users do not prefer those items, causing it to recommend them even less frequently in future iterations. Meanwhile, the same items continue to be recommended to majority users at high frequency because the historical training data contained abundant examples of majority users engaging with those items. This creates a self-reinforcing cycle where minority groups experience increasingly narrow recommendation sets over time.

Research on popularity bias demonstrates this mechanism empirically. Popularity bias occurs when a few highly popular items are recommended frequently while the majority of other items are ignored[11]. However, the impact of this popularity bias is not uniform across users. Users who prefer "long-tail" items—items that are not particularly popular overall but that align with niche interests—are disproportionately harmed by popularity bias because the feedback loop reinforces mainstream items at the expense of niche items[46]. As the recommender system observes that popular items receive more engagement (partly because they are recommended more often, creating a feedback loop), it increasingly emphasizes those popular items. For users with mainstream preferences, this creates a positive feedback loop where their preferred items are recommended more, which increases engagement, which further increases recommendations. For users with niche preferences, a negative feedback loop takes hold where their preferred items are recommended less, reducing engagement, which further decreases future recommendations.

The amplification of bias through feedback loops has measurable consequences. Research simulating user interactions with recommenders in an offline setting demonstrated that feedback loops lead to declining aggregate diversity in recommendations, shifting the representation of users' tastes over time, and homogenization of user experiences[11]. The term "homogenization" refers to a decrease in the variance of items recommended across the entire system, meaning that all users are gradually pushed toward similar recommendation sets even if they had diverse preferences initially. This effect is particularly problematic because it reduces the diversity of content that users are exposed to, potentially narrowing their perspectives and limiting their discovery of items that might genuinely interest them.

## Incomplete Data and the Cold-Start Problem

Matching systems face a particular challenge when they must make recommendations for new users or items with little historical data—the "cold-start problem." When a new user joins a platform, the system has no history of their interactions to use for personalization. Similarly, when a new item is added to a platform's catalog, there are no user interactions with that item to use for predicting whether it might match users' preferences. Systems designed to address this challenge often attempt to leverage side information or content features to make predictions about new items or users. However, this approach creates a novel form of bias: because the system cannot rely on collaborative filtering (which requires user-item interactions), it must make predictions based on content features and metadata.

Research on cold-start recommendation has revealed that systems designed to address the cold-start problem can inadvertently inherit biases from the warm models they are trained to imitate[23]. Cold-start recommenders are often trained with supervision from warm collaborative filtering models in order to leverage collaborative and content information from available interaction data. However, because they learn to replicate the behavior of collaborative filtering methods, cold-start models may inherit their predictive biases. Specifically, because cold-start systems cannot infer popularity from interaction data, they instead attempt to estimate it based solely on content features[23]. This can lead to significant over-prediction of certain cold items with similar content to popular warm items, even if their ground truth popularity is very low[23]. The system essentially uses the content of items to predict their popularity, learning that if an item has similar keywords to a popular item, it should also be predicted as popular. This creates a scenario where new items that are superficially similar to popular items get recommended frequently, while new items that are different from existing popular items—regardless of their actual quality or relevance—are recommended rarely.

The problem is compounded by the fact that incomplete data means the system must make assumptions about what features matter. Content-based recommendation algorithms, which are widely used as a supplement to collaborative filtering, are based on natural language processing, and natural language processing algorithms are well known to encode gender stereotypes[1]. For instance, NLP algorithms are more likely to associate female names with family-related words than career words compared with male names[1]. When a job recommendation system uses content-based matching, it may learn associations between gendered language in job descriptions (such as keywords like "nurturing," "collaborative," or "supportive") and particular genders. If women's names appear more frequently in the training data alongside jobs containing such gendered language, the system learns to recommend those jobs more frequently to women, even though such language may reflect stereotyping rather than actual job requirements.

## Algorithmic Design Choices and Feature Selection Bias

Beyond the properties of the training data itself, bias can arise from how system designers make choices about algorithm design and feature selection. Algorithms are not neutral mathematical objects but rather reflect the values and assumptions of the people who design them[22]. Programming errors, such as an AI designer unfairly weighting factors in the decision-making process, can unknowingly transfer into the system[3]. Weighting is often presented as a technique to avoid bias, as it involves adjustments to data so that it better reflects the actual population. However, weighting itself requires assumptions from designers, which can lead to inaccuracies and introduce bias[3]. When developers embed subjective rules based on their own conscious or unconscious biases into the algorithm, those biases become embedded in the system's decision-making.

Feature selection bias represents a particularly insidious form of design bias. Feature selection bias occurs when the set of features chosen to train a model does not adequately represent the underlying problem or is not equally relevant across all subpopulations within the dataset[34]. This can happen through omission, where important features that would predict fair outcomes are excluded from the model, or through commission, where irrelevant features are included based on designers' assumptions. For example, AI models developed for COVID-19 patient risk prediction, triage, and contact tracing showed feature selection bias through inadequate representation of social determinants of health such as race, socioeconomic status, and access to technology[34]. By not including these features, the models failed to account for how structural factors shape health outcomes and disease risk, leading to biased predictions that underestimated risk for vulnerable populations.

The selection of features to use in a matching system fundamentally shapes its outcomes. In hiring contexts, if a matching system is trained to predict "job success" but the historical data on which success was measured is incomplete or biased, then the system will learn to match candidates to jobs in ways that perpetuate whatever biases shaped the initial success metrics. If women were historically promoted less frequently, then historical data on promotions will show women as less successful, and a matching system trained on that data will learn to match women to positions with lower advancement potential. The system is not necessarily "wrong" in a statistical sense—it may accurately predict historical patterns—but those historical patterns reflect discrimination.

## Demographic Parity, Equalized Odds, and Other Fairness Metrics

Evaluating whether a matching or recommendation system is fair requires choosing fairness metrics, and different metrics capture different notions of fairness. Demographic parity is one approach to fairness that requires equal acceptance rates for both majority and minority groups, regardless of individual qualifications[12]. If a matching system achieves demographic parity, a user's chance of being matched or recommended an item does not vary by demographic group. For example, if 20 percent of majority-group candidates are matched to opportunities and 20 percent of minority-group candidates are matched, the system satisfies demographic parity[12]. The key benefit of demographic parity is that it ensures both majority and minority groups are represented in matched outcomes in the same proportions as they are in the candidate pool[12].

However, demographic parity has a significant limitation: it does not take into account the distribution of actual qualifications across demographic groups[12]. In a scenario where the majority group has more qualified candidates than the minority group, achieving demographic parity may require matching some unqualified majority-group candidates while rejecting some qualified minority-group candidates, simply to achieve equal numbers in the final outcome. When examining acceptance rates separately for qualified candidates, demographic parity can mask significant disparities[12]. If the acceptance rate for qualified majority-group candidates is 46 percent while the acceptance rate for qualified minority-group candidates is only 27 percent, the system is failing to provide equal opportunity even though it achieves demographic parity in overall acceptance rates[12].

Equalized odds represents an alternative fairness metric that focuses on ensuring equal treatment of people with equal qualifications. Equalized odds requires that the true positive rate and false positive rate are equal across all groups[21]. In other words, the system should be equally accurate (or equally inaccurate) for different demographic groups. This metric ensures that predictions are equally accurate (or equally erroneous) for different sensitive groups[21], meaning that groups should not suffer disproportionately from either false positives or false negatives. However, equalized odds also has limitations and can be more complex to implement than demographic parity, potentially requiring more advanced techniques like reweighting or adversarial learning[21].

The choice of fairness metric matters enormously because different metrics prioritize different values. Demographic parity prioritizes representation, while equalized odds prioritizes accuracy. In contexts where the historical representation of groups is already unequal (because of past discrimination), demographic parity might actually require the matching system to be "unfair" to the overrepresented group to achieve equal representation. Conversely, equalized odds might permit continued disparities in acceptance rates if the underlying population has different qualification rates. There is no single "correct" fairness metric, and the appropriate choice depends on the specific context and values of the organization deploying the system[21].

## Transparency, Explainability, and Feature Disclosure

Addressing bias in matching systems requires not only technical interventions but also transparency and explainability about how the systems operate. Users and affected parties have a legitimate interest in understanding why they were matched or recommended something, what factors influenced that decision, and whether those factors are fair. Transparency measures "should give an honest account of how the recommendations are selected and how the system works"[10]. This includes disclosing the interests and attributes that the platform may have inferred about users, including how the system made these inferences[10]. When a user is recommended something, they should be able to understand what specific actions they took that led to that recommendation.

Real-world examples illustrate why transparency matters. A user reported seeing an advertisement on his Facebook feed asking "COMING OUT? NEED HELP?" without understanding how or why he received that ad[10]. The advertisement was targeted based on inferences the platform had made about his sexual orientation, inferences that the user had not explicitly confirmed and that turned out to be inaccurate. From his perspective, the recommendation was unexplained and potentially revealing of information he had not disclosed. More fundamentally, the transparency deficit meant he could not understand or challenge the inference about his identity, could not correct the inaccuracy, and had limited ability to control what information about himself the platform could use for targeting.

Transparency at the user level requires several components. Users should have access to explanations of why specific recommendations were made, including information on the different signals and user characteristics the recommendation system considered[10]. This explanation should include not just the final algorithmic decision but the factors that influenced it. If the system inferred a user's interests based on their browsing history, that inference should be disclosed. If the system used demographic information in making recommendations, that fact should be disclosed. Users should also have access to information about what interests and attributes the platform believes it has inferred about them, including sensitive attributes such as race, age, or sexual orientation[10]. Additionally, systems should provide explanations for "Why Not" cases—when a user expects certain results that the recommender system does not produce, they should be able to understand why.

Beyond user-level transparency, system-level documentation should disclose how recommendation systems work at an aggregate level[10]. This includes information about what signals and factors the system considers, how it weighs them, and what changes have been made to the system over time[10]. If the platform changes how its recommendation algorithm works, these changes should be publicly disclosed with explanations for why they were made[10]. This documentation should extend to information about how the system tests its recommendation systems and determines their effectiveness[10]. At minimum, platforms should disclose information on how well their systems predict the preferences of different demographic groups[10], allowing external reviewers to assess whether the system has demographic disparities in performance.

The research on model explainability tools like SHAP and LIME reveals important limitations in how we can achieve transparency about complex algorithms. SHAP provides both global and local explanations and considers different combinations of features when calculating attribution, while LIME is limited to local explanations and fits a local linear model[50]. However, both methods have significant limitations when features are correlated (collinear), as is common in real datasets[50]. When features are correlated, both SHAP and LIME may struggle to accurately identify which features are actually responsible for a prediction, because the importance of a feature is entangled with the importance of other correlated features[50]. This creates a situation where explainability tools might provide confident-sounding explanations that are actually misleading, giving false confidence that we understand why the system made a particular decision.

Moreover, research has shown that adversaries can successfully infer sensitive attributes from model explanations in both cases where the sensitive attributes were included in training data and where they were censored. This reveals a paradoxical situation: the very mechanisms designed to make systems more transparent and fair (explainability) can become attack surfaces that compromise privacy and reveal sensitive information. An explanation that shows which features most influenced a decision can leak information about the actual values of those features, and through careful analysis of multiple explanations, an adversary can infer information about protected attributes.

## Regulatory Frameworks and Audit Approaches

Responding to the evident risks of bias in algorithmic matching systems, regulatory bodies globally are establishing frameworks that require greater fairness, accountability, and transparency. The European Union's Digital Services Act and AI Act represent among the most comprehensive regulatory approaches, aiming to ensure transparency, risk assessment, and user control in recommendation systems through audits, data access provisions, and opt-out options[2]. The EU AI Act imposes specific requirements on high-risk AI systems, including those used for employment decisions, education assessment, and creditworthiness evaluation—contexts where recommendation and matching systems are commonly deployed.

Audit frameworks have emerged as a key regulatory instrument for identifying and addressing algorithmic bias. The E2EST/AA (Elements of Ethically Sustainable Trustworthy AI Assessment and Auditing) framework distinguishes between moments and sources of bias, providing auditors with an overview of the possible causes of disparate impact[43]. This framework goes beyond simply measuring statistical disparities and attempts to identify the underlying mechanisms through which bias arises. Moment-based sources of bias include data management bias, bias in algorithm design, bias in training, bias in deployment, and feedback loop bias[43]. Source-based categories include data quality assurance, bias control procedures, human supervision mechanisms, and mechanisms enabling data subjects to request intervention or refute results[43].

The regulatory emphasis on regular periodic audits reflects recognition that algorithms cannot be evaluated once and declared fair. Platforms should hire independent auditors to conduct regular periodic audits of recommendation algorithms in order to identify potentially harmful outcomes and take steps to address findings[26]. These audits should specifically evaluate how algorithmic recommendation systems can inappropriately influence or manipulate user perspectives and behaviors, promote concerning topics of information, and cause discrimination[26]. Companies should conduct these audits proactively, as well as in response to concerns surfaced by community partners, civil society organizations, researchers, and activists[26]. Critically, independent auditors should be permitted to review and verify relevant internal models and data, including the results of tests the company runs to evaluate how effective their recommendation system is[26].

Regulatory approaches in the United States have taken a different form, emphasizing transparency and accountability through proposed legislation[2]. The Algorithmic Accountability Act concept involves notice to impacted populations when algorithmic bias appears to have occurred, a comment period for affected parties, and federal and other entities supporting users' right to challenge algorithmic decisions that feel unfair[35]. A critical element of any regulatory approach is that operators of algorithms must be more transparent in their handling of sensitive information, especially if the potential proxy could itself be a legal classificatory harm[35].

## Bias Mitigation Strategies and Technical Interventions

Addressing bias in matching and recommendation systems requires both technical interventions and organizational approaches. At the data level, bias can be mitigated through careful data collection and preprocessing. Diverse and representative data is essential; machine learning is only as good as the data that trains it, and for AI to better reflect the diverse communities it serves, a far wider variety of humans' data must be represented in models[3]. Data fed into machine learning models and deep learning systems must be comprehensive and balanced, representative of all groups of people and reflective of the actual demographics of society[3]. In practical terms, this means not just collecting data from diverse populations but ensuring that the data is representative of how those populations actually behave, without oversampling or undersampling any particular group.

Data preprocessing techniques can be used to remove or mitigate the effects of biases. Oversampling underrepresented groups, reweighting the data, or using techniques such as adversarial debiasing can help balance the data and reduce the impact of biases[4]. Oversampling means creating more training examples for underrepresented groups, either by duplicating existing examples or generating synthetic examples. Reweighting means adjusting the importance placed on different examples during training, so that examples from underrepresented groups contribute more strongly to the learned patterns. Adversarial debiasing involves training a model to be less influenced by sensitive attributes by introducing adversarial examples that penalize the model when it makes decisions that correlate with those attributes[34].

At the algorithmic level, fairness constraints can be incorporated directly into the recommendation algorithm. Calibrated recommendations ensure that the distribution of items recommended to a user matches the distribution of the user's individual past preferences[16]. If a user had diverse preferences in the past, this diversity should be reflected in the recommendations; conversely, if a user had monothematic interests, the recommendations should reflect that focused interest. Calibration is personalized, meaning that different users receive different levels of diversity based on their own preference distributions rather than applying a uniform diversity target to all users[16]. This approach helps prevent both over-specialization (where a user gets stuck in a narrow genre) and inappropriate diversification (where a user is pushed toward items they do not actually prefer).

Fair collaborative filtering approaches can explicitly incorporate fairness constraints into the similarity computations that drive recommendations. One method first calculates user and item similarity matrices using cosine similarity, then computes a fairness-corrected item similarity matrix by blending the original item similarity matrix with an identity matrix, guided by a fairness constraint parameter[4]. This approach maintains the collaborative filtering logic while reducing the extent to which the system amplifies historical biases in item similarities.

Hybrid approaches that combine content-based, collaborative filtering, and knowledge-based recommendations can also improve fairness by leveraging multiple information sources. A system that combines different recommendation techniques and adapts the weightings of each technique based on user feedback can be more flexible in balancing accuracy with fairness concerns[4]. When the system receives feedback indicating that a user disliked a recommendation, it can adjust its weights to reduce reliance on whatever technique generated that poor recommendation, potentially moving toward techniques that are more diverse or more fair.

Position bias—the tendency for users to engage with items that appear in higher positions regardless of their actual relevance—represents another dimension requiring active mitigation[36]. One approach is to add randomness to item positions, which can prevent the reinforcement of position bias while still maintaining generally relevant recommendations. Methods such as FairPairs (which swaps items at adjacent positions) or RandPair (which swaps items at positions 1 and k) introduce controlled randomness[36]. Alternatively, Boltzmann exploration involves normalizing recommendation scores into probabilities and sampling recommendations based on these probabilities, giving each recommended item a chance to appear in higher positions weighted by its recommendation probability[36].

## Addressing Disparate Impact and Disparate Treatment

Legal frameworks for understanding algorithmic fairness distinguish between disparate treatment and disparate impact, concepts originally developed in employment discrimination law but increasingly applied to algorithmic systems[20]. Disparate treatment occurs when an employer or AI system treats an employee or job applicant differently because of their membership in a protected class[20]. This is intentional discrimination, where the algorithm explicitly uses a protected characteristic or a proxy for one to make different decisions for different people. Disparate impact, by contrast, refers to policies or practices that appear neutral but result in a disproportionate negative effect on members of a protected class[20]. The focus is on the impact of the decision, not the intent behind it.

An algorithm might avoid disparate treatment by never explicitly considering a protected characteristic like race or gender, but still cause disparate impact if it uses proxy variables or makes design choices that have disparate effects across groups. A matching system that uses zip codes as features could have disparate impact on racial minorities even though race is never explicitly considered, because zip codes correlate with race due to residential segregation. Similarly, a recommendation system that uses education or work experience as features might have disparate impact on groups that faced historical barriers to education and employment, even though these features are ostensibly objective and neutral.

Both disparate treatment and disparate impact are prohibited under employment discrimination laws, including Title VII of the Civil Rights Act[20]. However, they require different approaches to mitigation. Disparate treatment requires identifying and removing explicit discrimination from the algorithm. Disparate impact requires analyzing the effects of ostensibly neutral features and making design choices that reduce their harmful effects, which may require using protected characteristics explicitly to detect and correct for bias[35].

## Multi-Stakeholder Fairness in Two-Sided Platforms

Many modern matching platforms are two-sided markets where fairness concerns apply to both sides—both the users consuming recommendations and the providers whose items are being recommended or matched[32]. A job board, for example, must consider fairness to both job seekers (users) who want to find relevant opportunities and employers (providers) who want to find qualified candidates. Similarly, an e-commerce marketplace must consider fairness to both consumers who want personalized recommendations and sellers who want their products to be discovered.

Fairness for providers raises distinct concerns from fairness for consumers. Provider fairness focuses on ensuring that items from a wide range of providers are recommended, not just items from popular or well-established sellers[17]. Without explicit attention to provider fairness, recommendation systems can create "the winner takes all" effects where a small number of popular items and successful sellers receive disproportionate visibility, while most items and sellers receive little or no exposure. This can harm both consumers (who lose access to diverse options) and smaller sellers (who cannot gain visibility through the recommendation system regardless of their quality)[17]. Two-sided fairness approaches attempt to guarantee fairness simultaneously for both consumers and providers, such as the FairRec algorithm which guarantees Maxi-Min Share of exposure for producers and Envy-Free up to One Item fairness for customers[17].

## Temporal Dynamics and Concept Drift

Matching and recommendation systems operate in dynamic environments where user preferences evolve, item popularity changes, and seasonal effects emerge. Temporal dynamics in recommendation systems can be modeled by explicitly accounting for how user preferences and item relevance change over time[31]. This involves capturing patterns in user behavior, item popularity, and contextual shifts that occur as time progresses. By incorporating time-aware components, models can adapt to evolving trends, seasonal effects, or shifts in individual user interests, leading to more accurate and relevant recommendations[31].

Session-based recommenders, often used in e-commerce, treat user sessions as sequences of actions within a short time window and use techniques like Gated Recurrent Units or attention mechanisms to model short-term preferences within sessions[31]. For longer-term patterns, temporal embedding techniques allow user and item representations to evolve over time. Matrix factorization methods like TimeSVD++ extend traditional collaborative filtering by introducing time-dependent latent factors, allowing the system to capture trends like a user's shifting interest in genres or seasonal item popularity[31].

However, temporal dynamics also introduce a source of bias: concept drift, which is the gradual change in data patterns over time. An algorithm trained on historical data may not perform well when user preferences or item characteristics shift. Techniques like online learning update models incrementally as new data arrives, avoiding the need for full retraining, and periodicity-aware models can incorporate recurring patterns such as daily or weekly trends[31]. The challenge is that bias detection and mitigation efforts must themselves be adaptive, continuously monitoring the system as it evolves and detecting emerging biases that may arise from changing conditions.

## Privacy Risks Associated with Transparent Recommendation Systems

A paradoxical challenge emerges when attempting to increase transparency in recommendation systems to address fairness concerns: transparency can introduce privacy risks. Recommender systems that rate items across disjoint domains face a privacy risk analogous to the one that occurs with statistical database queries[27]. Users with eclectic tastes who rate products across different types or domains—what researchers call "straddlers"—highlight the conflict between personalization and privacy[27]. While straddlers enable serendipitous recommendations because the system can learn they have diverse interests, information about their existence could be used in conjunction with other data sources to uncover their identities and reveal personal details[27].

Explanations in recommender systems provide information that indicates the recommendation's strength based on the number of people involved[27]. These explanations can reveal information analogous to that provided by statistical database queries, and combined with other knowledge, this information could be used to identify a specific person[27]. The risk is particularly acute for straddlers whose novel rating patterns make them easily characterized and susceptible to tracking procedures[27]. By publishing aggregate statistics about who rates items together, or by providing explanations that reveal the number of users who have similar preference patterns, a transparent recommender system could inadvertently enable privacy attacks.

This creates a tension between transparency for fairness and privacy protection. Measures designed to make systems more transparent (by explaining recommendations, publishing statistics about system behavior, or enabling external audits) can potentially be exploited to infer sensitive information about individuals. Balancing these competing interests requires careful design of what information is disclosed, at what level of aggregation, and with what protections against re-identification attacks.

## Ethical Data Minimization and the Limits of Privacy

While data minimization—collecting only the minimum data necessary to provide a service—is generally viewed as an ethical principle, research suggests there are ethical limits beyond which further minimization can itself become unethical[48]. The ethical limit is reached when minimizing data collection prevents the platform from providing the promised service or when it hinders the ability to identify and correct biases or errors in the system[48]. Over-minimization can also limit the potential for beneficial secondary uses, such as public health research, requiring a careful balance[48].

This creates a genuine dilemma for organizations attempting to be both privacy-preserving and fairness-promoting. To detect bias in a recommendation system, auditors typically need access to granular data about how the system behaves for different demographic groups. But collecting and maintaining such data creates privacy risks. The ethical path forward requires transparency about this tradeoff and clear governance about how sensitive data is accessed, by whom, and under what constraints.

## Organizational Approaches and Red Team Methodology

Addressing algorithmic bias effectively requires not just technical solutions but organizational structures and practices that prioritize fairness. Red team methodology, borrowed from cybersecurity and adapted for algorithmic ethics, provides a framework for identifying potential biases before systems are deployed[45]. Red teams are composed of people with a variety of social and professional backgrounds, demographic groups, and interdisciplinary expertise appropriate for the deployment context of their AI system[45]. By bringing diverse perspectives to bear on evaluating an algorithm, red teams can identify risks and vulnerabilities that might be missed by the algorithm's developers.

The FAIR methodology describes six stages for implementing red teaming for AI ethics: reconnaissance (gathering intelligence from all stakeholders), assessment (evaluating potential dangers), response (implementing risk mitigation plans), monitoring (ensuring mitigation strategies are effective), and feedback (using findings to improve the system)[45]. This approach ensures that bias detection is not a one-time event but an ongoing process with multiple stakeholders involved.

A bias impact statement represents another organizational best practice, helping organizations probe and avert potential biases baked into or resulting from algorithmic decisions[35]. Operators of algorithms should brainstorm a core set of initial assumptions about the algorithm's purpose prior to its development and execution, considering who will be affected by the algorithm, whether they have appropriate training data, whether that data is sufficiently diverse, what groups they are worried about, how potential bias will be detected and tested, and what threshold will be used for measuring and correcting bias[35]. Developing this statement requires cross-functional and interdisciplinary teams that include not just technical staff but also domain experts, ethicists, affected community representatives, and others who can identify potential harms that technologists might overlook.

## Building Trust Through Fairness and Accountability

Ultimately, building trustworthy matching and recommendation systems requires commitment to fairness and accountability at every level—from data collection through deployment and beyond. Organizations that exercise effective algorithmic hygiene before, during, and after introducing algorithmic decision-making should be rewarded and potentially given public-facing acknowledgement for best practices[35]. Consumer trust in these systems depends on evidence that organizations are actively addressing bias and making their decision-making processes transparent and auditable.

The regulatory landscape continues to evolve, with different jurisdictions taking different approaches. The European Union has taken a more prescriptive regulatory approach, while the United States has emphasized market mechanisms and industry self-regulation. Yet globally, there is increasing recognition that algorithmic systems in matching and recommendation contexts carry sufficient risk to require oversight. This convergence reflects a growing understanding that despite the apparent objectivity of mathematics and code, algorithmic systems encode and can amplify human biases at scale, requiring conscious efforts to identify and mitigate those biases.

## Conclusion: Toward Fairer Matching Systems

Matching and recommendation systems present a compelling case study in how bias operates in artificial intelligence systems. These systems take incomplete data reflecting historical inequalities and use sophisticated algorithms to identify patterns, often learning to perpetuate and amplify the very inequalities present in the training data. The pathways through which bias enters such systems are multiple and often subtle: through historical data patterns, proxy variables, algorithmic design choices, feedback loops that amplify initial biases, and organizational pressures to optimize for engagement or profit without adequate attention to fairness.

Yet research also demonstrates that bias is neither inevitable nor immutable. Technological approaches like calibrated recommendations, fairness-aware algorithms, and adversarial debiasing can reduce the extent to which systems amplify historical inequalities. Organizational practices like red teaming, bias impact statements, and regular audits can identify problems before they harm users and providers at scale. Regulatory frameworks establishing transparency requirements and audit rights can create accountability structures that incentivize organizations to address bias proactively.

The most important insight from current research is that fairness in matching systems cannot be achieved through algorithm design alone. It requires attention to data quality and representativeness, careful thought about what features are used and why, transparent disclosure of system behavior and limitations, ongoing monitoring and auditing, and organizational commitment to fairness even when it conflicts with other objectives like engagement maximization. It requires acknowledging that trade-offs exist—between transparency and privacy, between accuracy and fairness, between personalization and diversity—and making explicit choices about how to navigate these trade-offs rather than allowing default technical choices to make those determinations.

For organizations deploying matching and recommendation systems, the path forward requires first understanding the specific sources of bias risk in their particular system and context[1][3][8][19]. Second, it requires choosing fairness metrics appropriate to that context and committing to meeting those metrics[12][21][32]. Third, it requires implementing technical controls like fairness constraints and demographic monitoring[16][26]. Fourth, it requires building organizational structures that prioritize fairness through diverse teams, bias impact statements, and regular audits[35][45]. Finally, it requires transparency with affected stakeholders about how the system works, what data it uses, and what biases or limitations it may have[10][26].

The stakes of getting this wrong are high. Biased matching systems in hiring contexts perpetuate employment discrimination and limit economic opportunity[19]. Biased recommendation systems in content consumption narrow the information and perspectives that users are exposed to[6][46]. Biased matching systems in lending and credit shape access to capital and economic resources. As these systems become increasingly consequential in mediating access to opportunities, information, and resources, the imperative to address their bias becomes more pressing. The evidence presented in this report demonstrates both the seriousness of bias risks and the feasibility of meaningful mitigation approaches, providing both warning and hope that more equitable algorithmic futures are possible with sustained effort and commitment.
