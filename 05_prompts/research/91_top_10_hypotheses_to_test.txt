Top 10 Hypotheses to Test

1) A major barrier for minority-owned businesses is not the lack of posted opportunities, but the difficulty of understanding relevance and first steps from the current procurement presentation.
2) Plain-language summaries and service-category matching would be more useful to first-time vendors than raw portal search alone.
3) Public data from Richmond’s procurement-related sources is sufficient to support a meaningful discovery or explainer prototype, even if not sufficient for a full production-grade tool.
4) The biggest friction in starting a small business in Richmond is sequencing confusion across multiple departments rather than absence of any single program.
5) A rule-based navigation tool could reduce entrepreneur confusion even without deep system integration, if the public process logic can be reconstructed well enough.
6) The most important information for the small-business navigation problem is currently fragmented across webpages and staff know-how rather than centralized in one usable public flow.
7) Teams that focus on narrowing scope to one user persona and one journey branch will produce stronger demos than teams trying to solve “starting a business” broadly.
8) Judges and stakeholders will respond more positively to prototypes that improve clarity and navigation than to ambitious systems that claim to automate government decisions.
9) The strongest continuation pathway will come from prototypes that produce useful artifacts for staff or partners, not just polished user interfaces.
10) A high-trust Pillar 4 prototype will need to foreground official-source links and explicit limits, because users are likely to over-trust confident-sounding civic software.
