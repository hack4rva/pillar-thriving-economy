Use the pillar problem statement outline and all attached/source materials to synthesize a high-quality research prompt package for the Richmond Civic Hackathon.

Your task is not to answer the pillar problem itself. Your task is to produce the best possible **research prompts** that a researcher, analyst, or AI system could use to deeply investigate the pillar and generate actionable inputs for hackathon teams.

Context:

* Event: Richmond Civic Hackathon
* Date: March 27–29, 2026
* Purpose: help teams build credible, community-centered, weekend-buildable civic solutions aligned to Mayor Danny Avula’s Mayoral Action Plan
* Output should support one pillar at a time
* The pillar problem statement outline may include BlueSky framing, targeted problem statements, stakeholders, constraints, known data sources, desired outcomes, and evaluation criteria

Instructions:

1. Read and absorb the full pillar problem statement outline and any supporting material.
2. Extract the core research dimensions embedded in the outline, including:

   * user groups affected
   * pain points and unmet needs
   * existing programs, services, and interventions
   * policy, process, and operational constraints
   * available public datasets and source systems
   * local Richmond context
   * institutional actors and stakeholders
   * risks, tradeoffs, and implementation barriers
   * signals of what could plausibly be prototyped in a weekend
3. Based on that material, generate a **research prompt set** that is organized into clear sections.
4. The prompts should be designed to produce research that is:

   * Richmond-specific
   * grounded in evidence
   * useful for teams building software prototypes
   * realistic within hackathon constraints
   * explicit about uncertainty where facts are missing
5. Do not produce vague brainstorm prompts. Produce prompts that force useful, concrete research.
6. Favor questions that help teams decide:

   * what problem is real and urgent
   * who the user is
   * what data exists
   * what has already been tried
   * where software can genuinely help
   * what should not be built
7. Include prompts that distinguish between:

   * problems suitable for software
   * problems that require policy change, budget, staffing, or institutional authority
8. Include prompts that surface prior art from other cities, nonprofits, civic tech groups, universities, and hackathons.
9. Include prompts that help identify:

   * high-value service navigation opportunities
   * transparency and public information gaps
   * workflow inefficiencies inside nonprofits or government
   * opportunities for document translation, summarization, matching, dashboards, maps, and public-facing search tools
10. Include prompts that test feasibility, ethics, and data quality.
11. Assume the final audience is a mix of technologists, nonprofit leaders, city stakeholders, mentors, and judges.

Output format:
Produce the result in the following exact structure.

# Pillar Research Prompt Package

## 1. Pillar Summary

Provide a concise synthesis of the pillar based only on the provided materials:

* core challenge
* likely users
* likely civic opportunity areas
* major constraints
* likely categories of viable hackathon solutions

## 2. Master Research Prompt

Write one excellent master prompt that could be given to a senior researcher or advanced AI system to produce a full research brief for this pillar.

## 3. Sub-Prompts by Category

Create strong prompts under each of these headings:

### A. Problem Landscape

Prompts to investigate the true shape of the problem in Richmond.

### B. User and Stakeholder Research

Prompts to identify who is affected, who serves them, and who holds relevant operational knowledge.

### C. Existing Services, Programs, and Interventions

Prompts to map what already exists and where the gaps are.

### D. Data and Source Inventory

Prompts to find public datasets, source systems, directories, documents, and structured information that teams could use.

### E. Prior Art and Comparable Solutions

Prompts to find similar tools, pilots, civic products, or hackathon projects from other places.

### F. Opportunity Framing for Hackathon Teams

Prompts to identify the most buildable, high-value software opportunities.

### G. Risks, Ethics, and Guardrails

Prompts to identify privacy issues, harmful automation risks, legal concerns, and misleading product patterns.

### H. MVP Feasibility

Prompts to determine what can realistically be built and demoed in 48 hours.

### I. Demo and Judging Alignment

Prompts to help teams shape ideas that will be understandable, credible, and impressive on demo day.

## 4. Top 10 Research Questions

Distill the entire pillar into the 10 most important research questions.

## 5. Top 10 Hypotheses to Test

Write 10 concrete hypotheses that good research could validate or disprove.

## 6. Red Flags

List the most likely ways teams could misunderstand the problem, misuse data, overclaim impact, or build something unrealistic.

## 7. High-Probability Project Archetypes

Based on the materials, propose 5 to 10 likely project archetypes that could emerge from the research, such as:

* service navigator
* public information explainer
* eligibility guide
* dashboard
* map
* workflow assistant
* document translator
* search or matching tool

For each archetype, include:

* why it fits the pillar
* likely user
* likely required data
* likely risks
* why it is or is not feasible in a weekend

## 8. Missing Information

Explicitly identify what the provided pillar materials do not contain that would be important to gather before the hackathon.

Quality bar:

* Be Richmond-aware
* Be skeptical of generic AI hype
